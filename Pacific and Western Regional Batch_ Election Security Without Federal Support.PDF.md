\## CALIFORNIA

\### State election authority and legal framework

\*\*Secretary of State Shirley N. Weber, Ph.D.\*\* (D) administers
California's elections, overseeing 58 county registrars of voters.
California's Elections Code is among the most comprehensive in the
nation, anchored by the \*\*California Voting Rights Act\*\* (Elec. Code
§§ 14025– 14032), enacted in 2002,
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/code-elec/division-14/chapter-1-5/section-14025/)
which is stronger than the federal VRA — requiring no showing of
geographically concentrated minority districts \[Wikipedia\]
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_Voting_Rights_Act) and
imposing no intent requirement. \[Victor Valley College\]
(https://www.vvc.edu/sites/default/ﬁles/ﬁles/FFF_Nav_CALRA_V1_111714.pdf)

California is a universal vote-by-mail state with all active registered
voters receiving mail ballots. \[CA\]
(https://www.sos.ca.gov/elections/upcoming-elections/primary-election-june-2-2026)
In-person voting remains available at vote centers. All systems require
\*\*paper ballot audit trails\*\* (Elec. Code § 19250),
\[CA\](https://www.sos.ca.gov/elections/ovsta/frequently-requested-information/voting-systems-used-counties/how-use-your-countys-voting-system/premier-optical-scan-automark-voting-system)
with optical scan tabulation (Dominion ImageCast, ES&S, Hart InterCivic
Verity) across counties. Los Angeles County uses its custom VSAP system.
Risk-limiting audits are supported statewide.

Home rule authority under Article XI of the California Constitution
grants charter cities signiﬁcant autonomy, including over municipal
election procedures, though state election laws apply to state and
federal elections.

\*\*Key 2024–2025 legislation:\*\* The \*\*PEACE Act\*\* (AB 2642,
signed September 24, 2024) is the ﬁrst-in-nation law \[Brennan Center
for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/analysis-opinion/california-assembly-passes-bill-equip-voters-and-election-administrators)
creating a \*\*private right of action for voter intimidation\*\* (Elec.
Code §§ 18580–18582) and establishing a legal presumption that open
carry of ﬁrearms while observing voting constitutes intimidation. The
Defending Democracy from Deepfake Deception Act (AB 2655) requires
platforms to restrict AI-generated deceptive election content near
elections. SB 280 (2025) added Section 8162 to the Elections Code.
\[CA\](https://www.sos.ca.gov/elections/upcoming-elections/primary-election-june-2-2026/key-dates-and-deadlines)

\### Cybersecurity infrastructure and capabilities

California operates one of the most layered cybersecurity ecosystems of
any state. The \*\*California Cybersecurity Integration Center
(Cal-CSIC)\*\*,
\[CA\](https://www.caloes.ca.gov/oﬃce-of-the-director/operations/homeland-security/california-cybersecurity-integration-center/)
codiﬁed in Government Code § 8586.5
\[CA\](https://pfwt.caloes.ca.gov/oﬃce-of-the-director/operations/homeland-security/california-cybersecurity-integration-center/)
and housed within Cal OES, serves as the central coordination hub. The
\*\*Oﬃce of Information Security\*\* within the Department of Technology
operates SOCaaS — a cloud-native, 24/7 Security Operations Center
serving 112+ public-sector organizations with estimated savings
exceeding \$54 million.
\[CGI\](https://insider.govtech.com/california/news/contributed-how-california-is-centralizing-public-sector-cybersecurity)
The \*\*Election Security Task Force\*\* (joint SOS/Cal OES initiative)
coordinates county-level cybersecurity
\[CA\](https://www.sos.ca.gov/elections/election-cybersecurity) with the
Election Security Subcommittee of the California Cybersecurity Task
Force.

California's National Guard maintains robust cyber capabilities:
\*\*Cyber Protection Team 171\*\* (Army), \[CA\]
(https://calguard.ca.gov/cybernetworkdefense/) the \*\*195th Wing\*\*
(Air National Guard, dedicated cyber), the \*\*261st Cyberspace
Operations Squadron\*\*,
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/California_State_Guard)
\[195th Wing\]
(https://www.195wg.ang.af.mil/Units/261st-Cyber-Operations-Squadron/)
and Joint Task Force Cyber, which hosts the multi-state \*\*Cyber
Dawn\*\* exercise. The Guard's Cyber Network Defense Team conducts
penetration tests of state agencies on a rotational basis.
\[CA\](https://calguard.ca.gov/cybernetworkdefense/)

\*\*HAVA funding totals approximately \$491 million\*\*
\[eac\](https://www.eac.gov/funding-levels-by-state) — by far the
largest allocation among the six states, reﬂecting California's 12%
share of national voting-age population. This includes \$73.5 million in
Election Security Grants (2018–2020) and \$36.5 million in CARES Act
funds.

The loss of CISA/EI-ISAC services is signiﬁcant but manageable for
California given its self-funded SOCaaS, Cal-CSIC, and large IT
workforce. However, the state's 58-county system means that smaller,
rural counties that relied heavily on federal scanning and intelligence
services face outsized impacts.

\### Physical security and polling place protections

California maintains a \*\*100-foot electioneering-free buffer zone\*\*
(Elec. Code §§ 18370, 18541, 18546) measured from the entrance to
polling places and from the room where voters sign the roster.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/california/2007/elec/18540-18548.html)

\*\*Firearms protections are among the strongest in the nation.\*\*
Elec. Code § 18544(a) prohibits any person possessing a ﬁrearm from
being stationed at or near a polling place.
\[CA\](https://oag.ca.gov/system/ﬁles/2025-dle-14.pdf) Penal Code
§ 26230(a)(25) prohibits concealed carry licensees from carrying at
voting locations. \[Brennan Center for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/california-election-interference-law-handbook)
The PEACE Act (Elec. Code § 18581) creates a \*\*presumption of
intimidation\*\* for open carry while observing voting. \[Brennan Center
for Justice
+2\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/california-election-interference-law-handbook)
Critically, Elec. Code § 18544 also prohibits uniformed peace oﬃcers,
private guards, or security personnel from being stationed at polls
\*\*without written authorization\*\* from the elections oﬃcial
\[Brennan Center

for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/california-election-interference-law-handbook)
— making unauthorized law enforcement presence a felony.
\[CA\](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/ahead-general-election-attorney-general-bonta-and-secretary-state-weber-remind)
\[Brennan Center for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/california-protections-against-intimidation-voters-and-election-workers)

Poll watchers need no credentials or party aﬃliation — anyone may
observe \[CapRadio\]
(https://www.capradio.org/articles/2020/10/02/politifact-california-whats-allowed-and-whats-not-at-voting-sites-in-california/)
(Elec. Code § 2300(a)(9)(A)). However, watchers cannot communicate with
voters within 100 feet, question voter qualiﬁcations, or photograph
voters (Elec. Code § 18541).
\[CA\](https://oag.ca.gov/system/ﬁles/2025-dle-14.pdf)

\*\*Voter intimidation\*\* is a felony under Elec. Code § 18540 (16
months to 3 years imprisonment). \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/california/2007/elec/18540-18548.html) The
PEACE Act provides prevailing plaintiffs with attorney's fees, expert
fees, and litigation expenses, \[Perkins
Coie\](https://perkinscoie.com/insights/article/californias-peace-act-provides-private-right-action-election-intimidation)
and grants the AG enforcement authority.
\[CA\](https://leginfo.legislature.ca.gov/faces/billNavClient.xhtml?
bill_id=202320240AB2642) The Ralph Civil Rights Act (Civil Code § 51.7)
provides an additional civil cause of action for political
activity-based threats.
\[LegalClarity\](https://legalclarity.org/voting-intimidation-laws-in-california-what-you-need-to-know/)

Anti-paramilitary protections include Penal Code § 11460 (assembling as
a paramilitary organization) \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/california/2007/pen/11460.html) and
Constitutional subordination of militia to civil authority.

\## OREGON

\### State election authority and legal framework

\*\*Secretary of State Tobias Read\*\* (D) took oﬃce in January 2025 and
oversees Oregon's 36 county clerks through the Elections Division.
Oregon has been a \*\*100% vote-by-mail state since 2000\*\* — the
nation's ﬁrst. \[Oregon Capital Chronicle\]
(https://oregoncapitalchronicle.com/2025/04/04/oregon-washington-sue-trump-over-sweeping-elections-order/)
There are no traditional polling places. County clerks mail ballots
14–20 days before each election, and voters return them by mail or at
oﬃcial ballot drop sites that must remain open on Election Day until 8
PM (ORS 254.470).
\[OPB\](https://www.opb.org/article/2024/11/04/how-voter-ballots-counted-oregon-election-county/)
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/oregon/2019/volume-06/chapter-254/section-254-470/)

Vote-counting systems include \*\*ES&S, Clear Ballot\*\* (ClearVote,
processing ~57% of state votes), and \*\*Hart InterCivic Verity\*\* —
all optical scan, none internet-connected. Oregon's 36 counties each
perform post-election audits. \[Oregoncapitalinsider\]
(https://oregoncapitalinsider.com/2016/11/03/vote-tabulator-machines-checked-against-hand-count/)

Home rule authority exists under Article VI, § 10 and Article XI, § 2 of
the Oregon Constitution, but \*\*ﬁrearms regulation is preempted at the
state level\*\* under ORS 166.170, with limited exceptions for discharge
(ORS 166.171–172) and possession of loaded ﬁrearms in public places (ORS
166.173).

\*\*Key 2024–2025 legislation:\*\* Oregon enacted two laws expanding
language translation requirements for voting materials (2024). \[Brennan
Center for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/voting-laws-roundup-may-2024)
SB 243 (2025) proposed giving local governments authority over ﬁrearm
rules in public buildings \[OPB\]
(https://www.opb.org/article/2025/03/27/oregon-gun-law-ﬁrearms-weapons-guns-measure-114-senate-bill-243-waiting-period-bump-stock/)
— a potentially signiﬁcant development for municipal ordinance strategy.
\*\*Measure 114\*\* (2022), requiring permits-to-purchase ﬁrearms and
banning large-capacity magazines, \[Ballotpedia\]
(https://ballotpedia.org/Oregon_Measure_114,\_Changes_to_Firearm_Ownership_and_Purchase_Requirements_Initiative\_(2022))
has \*\*never gone into effect\*\* \[Oregon Department of
Justice\](https://www.doj.state.or.us/wp-content/uploads/2025/03/COA-opinion.pdf)
due to legal challenges; the Oregon Supreme Court heard oral arguments
November 6, 2025, \[Ballotpedia\]
(https://ballotpedia.org/Oregon_Measure_114,\_Changes_to_Firearm_Ownership_and_Purchase_Requirements_Initiative\_(2022))
with a decision pending.

\### Physical security and polling place protections

Because Oregon has no traditional polling places, protections center on
\*\*ballot drop-off sites and county election oﬃces\*\*. ORS 260.695(3)
establishes a \*\*100-foot electioneering-free zone\*\* measured
radially from any entrance to a building designated for ballot deposit.
\[Mindy-ﬁscher-writer
+2\](http://mindy-ﬁscher-writer.com/2016/10/know-rights-voter-intimidation-laws-state/)
However, the Oregon AG has concluded this ban may violate the Oregon
Constitution's robust free speech provisions (Article I, § 8), meaning
campaigning near drop sites may be \*\*protected speech\*\*.
\[state\](https://www.doj.state.or.us/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/CJ-Election-Guide-CJ-BW.pdf)

\*\*Firearms protections are limited.\*\* There is \*\*no speciﬁc
statute\*\* prohibiting ﬁrearms at ballot drop sites. However, ORS
166.370 prohibits ﬁrearm possession in \*\*public buildings\*\* (a Class
C felony), \[Oregon Department of
Justice\](https://www.doj.state.or.us/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/CJ-Election-Guide-CJ-BW.pdf)
which covers county election oﬃces in government buildings. ORS 166.173
allows cities and counties to regulate loaded ﬁrearms in public places.
\[state\](https://www.doj.state.or.us/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/CJ-Election-Guide-CJ-BW.pdf)
Oregon is generally an \*\*open carry state\*\* with no permit required;
concealed carry requires a license (ORS 166.291). The Measure 114
permit-to-purchase requirement, if upheld, would add a layer of
regulation but would not speciﬁcally address ballot drop-site carry.

\*\*Poll watchers\*\* must present written authorization signed by a
political party oﬃcer, candidate, or county clerk (ORS 254.482). Numbers
are limited to prevent interference with orderly procedures.
\[state\](https://www.doj.state.or.us/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/CJ-Election-Guide-CJ-BW.pdf)
\[Oregon Public Law\](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_254.482)

\*\*Voter intimidation\*\* is a \*\*Class C felony\*\* under ORS 260.665
\[Oregon Public Law\](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_260.993)
(undue inﬂuence: force, violence, restraint, or threat thereof to induce
voting behavior). Harassment of an election worker performing oﬃcial
duties is a \*\*Class A misdemeanor\*\* (ORS 166.065(4)(c)).
\*\*Unlawful paramilitary activity\*\* is a Class C felony under ORS
166.660. \[state
+2\](https://www.doj.state.or.us/wp-content/uploads/2024/11/CJ-Election-Guide-CJ-BW.pdf)

Oregon's voter intimidation statute AG enforcement authority includes
civil penalties up to \$10,000 under ORS 260.995. \[Oregon Public
Law\](https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_260.995) However, \*\*no
stand-alone Oregon Voting Rights Act\*\* exists.

(https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=9.41.284) However, there

\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/location-restrictions-in-washington/)

misdemeanors.

\*\*Poll watchers/observers\*\* authorized by political parties may
access voting facilities (RCW 29A.84.510(5)). \[Washington State
Legislature\](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=29A.84.510)
Observers may not touch ballots or election systems (RCW 29A.40.100).

felony\*\* (RCW 29A.84.555). \[MRSC\]
(https://mrsc.org/explore-topics/elections/basics/elections-administration)

\*\*Voter intimidation\*\* is a \*\*Class C felony\*\* under RCW
29A.84.620 (using menace, force, threat, or unlawful means to
hinder/deter voting).
\[Wa-law\](https://wa-law.org/bill/2021-22/hb/1716/S/rcw/29A_elections/29A.84_crimes_and_penalties.html)
Inﬂuencing a voter to withhold their vote is a gross misdemeanor (RCW
29A.84.630). \[Washington State Legislature\]
(https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=29a.84&full=true&pdf=true)
Paramilitary organizations are prohibited under RCW 38.40.120 (unlawful

\## ALASKA

\### State election authority and legal framework

Elections are administered by the \*\*Alaska Division of Elections\*\*
under \*\*Lt. Governor Nancy Dahlstrom\*\* (R). \*\*Director Carol
Beecher\*\* runs day-to-day operations from four regional oﬃces (Juneau,
Anchorage, Fairbanks, Nome). Alaska uses a borough/city municipal system
rather than counties.

Alaska is a \*\*paper ballot state\*\* with \*\*270 optical scan
precincts\*\* using Dominion ImageCast tabulators (not
internet-connected) and \*\*131 hand-count precincts\*\* — typically in
remote rural communities where election boards hand-count ballots and
call results to regional oﬃces. Each precinct has at least one
accessible voting tablet (BMD) for disability and language assistance.
The State Ballot Review Board audits at least 5% of votes from each
House district.

\*\*Ranked-choice voting remains in effect\*\* after a 2024 repeal
measure (Ballot Measure 2) failed by just \*\*743 votes\*\* (0.23%)
following a recount completed December 10, 2024. A new repeal petition
(25USCV) is under review as of January 2026.

\*\*Key 2024–2025 legislation:\*\* SB 64, an omnibus election bill,
addresses expanded early voting in rural communities, synthetic media in
electioneering, voter registration procedures, and strengthened unlawful
interference provisions.

Alaska's home rule municipalities (e.g., Municipality of Anchorage,
Matanuska-Susitna Borough) can run their own municipal elections but
must follow state law for state and federal contests.

\### Cybersecurity infrastructure and capabilities

Alaska's cybersecurity is led by the \*\*Oﬃce of Information
Technology\*\* under \*\*CIO Bill Smith\*\*. The state's cybersecurity
posture has been described as \*\*"challenged" and "very, very
vulnerable"\*\* by former oﬃcials. Alaska has experienced \*\*signiﬁcant
CISO turnover\*\* — at least three CISOs since 2017. Former State CISO
Mark Breunig later became CISA's Cybersecurity State Coordinator for
Alaska. An \$11.6 million cybersecurity contract with Evotek was
terminated under disputed circumstances.

Election cybersecurity measures include CISA Cyber Hygiene Scanning
registration, EI-ISAC participation (now defunded), logic and accuracy
testing, and bipartisan absentee ballot review. The Division of
Elections participates in national tabletop exercises.

Alaska's National Guard cyber capability is the \*\*smallest in this
batch\*\*: approximately \*\*15 cyber/computer security specialists\*\*
who participate in Cyber Shield exercises. No dedicated cyberspace
operations squadron exists. Alaska was \*\*not listed\*\* among states
that activated Guard cyber units for election support in recent cycles.

\*\*HAVA funding totals approximately \$27 million\*\* (\$6 million in
Election Security Grants at the minimum ﬂoor allocation). Alaska's
centralized election administration means the state bears nearly all
election costs — creating eﬃciency but also a single point of failure.

\*\*The CISA withdrawal impact is most severe for Alaska.\*\* The
state's resource-constrained cybersecurity, minimal Guard cyber
capability, small IT workforce, and dependence on federal
information-sharing networks make it the most vulnerable of the six
states.

\### Physical security and polling place protections

Alaska maintains a \*\*200-foot electioneering-free buffer zone\*\* (AS
15.15.170, AS 15.56.016) — tied with Hawaii for the largest in this
batch.

\*\*Alaska has NO statute prohibiting ﬁrearms at polling places. This is
the most critical ﬁnding in the entire batch.\*\* Alaska is a
\*\*constitutional carry state\*\* — no permit is needed for either open
or concealed carry (open carry legal at 16+, concealed at 21+). Article
1, § 19 of the Alaska Constitution provides that the right to keep and
bear arms "shall not be denied or infringed by the State or a political
subdivision of the State." Alaska's statutory ﬁrearms prohibitions are
limited to K–12 school grounds (AS 11.61.195), courthouses (AS
11.61.220(a)(2)), child care centers, domestic violence shelters, and
bars. \*\*Polling places in community centers, churches, ﬁre stations,
or other non-school venues have no ﬁrearms restriction whatsoever.\*\*
Per Giffords Law Center: "Alaska has no statute prohibiting ﬁrearms in
the following places... Polling places."

This gap is compounded by three factors. First, approximately \*\*131
hand-count precincts\*\* operate in remote villages accessible only by
aircraft, boat, or snowmachine — most with \*\*no resident law
enforcement\*\*. Alaska State Troopers have extremely limited rural
coverage, and Village Public Safety Oﬃcer positions face chronic
vacancies. Second, Alaska's signiﬁcant \*\*Alaska Native populations\*\*
have historically faced voting access challenges, with the \*Toyukak v.
Dahlstrom\* consent decree requiring comprehensive language assistance
through December 2026. Third, the absence of any ﬁrearms restriction at
polls means the only recourse is \*\*after-the-fact prosecution\*\*
under general voter intimidation statutes (AS 15.56.030, a Class C
felony), which provides no preventive deterrent.

\*\*Poll watchers\*\* may observe all election procedures, closely
observe ballot preparation, and challenge voters they suspect are
unqualiﬁed (must be in writing with stated reason). Watchers who create
a public disturbance may be removed.

\### Legal strategies and constitutional protections

\*\*Alaska did NOT join the EO 14248 lawsuit\*\* and has \*\*cooperated
with EO 14248\*\* — the Lt. Governor's oﬃce conﬁrmed that Alaska
provided the DOJ with its \*\*complete voter registration list\*\* in
December 2025, citing AS 15.07.195(c)(1). Alaska's AG is Republican, and
the state has not opposed the executive order.

\*\*AG Stephen J. Cox\*\* was appointed August 29, 2025, replacing Treg
Taylor who resigned to run for governor. Cox is a former DOJ oﬃcial and
is subject to legislative conﬁrmation in 2026. There is no AG election
protection program or hotline.

Federal voter intimidation statutes (18 U.S.C. §§ 241, 245, 594; 52
U.S.C. § 20511) and private rights of action under 42 U.S.C. § 1983
remain available. Alaska Native voting protections operate primarily
through the Voting Rights Act § 203 consent decree (\*Toyukak v.
Dahlstrom\*), enforceable through federal court.

\## Implications for municipal ordinance strategy under 18 U.S.C. § 592

\### Alaska is the clear highest-priority target

Alaska presents the most urgent case for municipal ordinance work in
this entire batch — and potentially nationally. The state's
\*\*constitutional carry regime combined with zero ﬁrearms prohibition
at polling places\*\* creates a gap that is both legally signiﬁcant and

practically dangerous. Alaska's remote polling places, minimal law
enforcement presence, and vulnerable Alaska Native communities compound
the risk. The state's cooperation with EO 14248 and Republican
governance mean no state-level remedy is forthcoming.

However, the municipal ordinance path faces a structural obstacle:
Alaska's constitutional provision that the right to bear arms "shall not
be denied or infringed by the State or a political subdivision of the
State" (Art. 1, § 19) could be read to prohibit municipal ﬁrearms
restrictions at polls. The legal strategy would need to frame ordinances
under 18 U.S.C. § 592 as enforcement of \*\*federal\*\* law — not state
ﬁrearms regulation — which could survive constitutional scrutiny because
the municipality would be acting as an agent of federal enforcement
rather than exercising independent ﬁrearms authority. The
\*\*Municipality of Anchorage\*\* and \*\*Fairbanks North Star
Borough\*\* (both home-rule municipalities) are the highest-priority
targets, as they have the largest voting populations and institutional
capacity for ordinance adoption.

\### Oregon's outdoor drop-site gap deserves attention

Oregon's lack of any ﬁrearms prohibition at outdoor ballot drop sites —
combined with state preemption of local ﬁrearms regulation (ORS 166.170)
— creates a secondary gap. However, SB 243 (2025), which would give
local governments authority over ﬁrearm rules in public buildings, could
open a pathway. If enacted, municipal ordinances extending protections
to ballot drop-site perimeters would become viable. Portland, Eugene,
and Salem are natural targets given progressive city councils and
existing engagement on gun safety issues.

\### Washington's CPL exception is a narrower but real gap

Washington's exception allowing concealed pistol license holders to
carry at voting centers (though not counting centers) creates a
vulnerability. However, Washington's statewide ﬁrearms preemption (RCW
9.41.290) limits municipal action. The ordinance strategy would need to
focus on the 18 U.S.C. § 592 federal enforcement mechanism rather than
direct ﬁrearms regulation.

\### Supportive environments where state frameworks are already strong

\*\*California\*\* and \*\*Colorado\*\* have the most comprehensive
existing protections and represent environments where municipal
ordinances would reinforce rather than create new protections.
California's PEACE Act (with its private right of action and ﬁrearms
presumption of intimidation) and Colorado's combined Vote Without Fear
Act and Sensitive Spaces Act leave minimal gaps. Municipal ordinances in
these states would serve primarily as \*\*belt-and-suspenders\*\*
reinforcements and as model legislation for export to other
jurisdictions. Los Angeles, San Francisco, Denver, and Boulder are
natural partners for model ordinance development.

\*\*Hawaii's\*\* 200-foot sensitive zone designation and strict ﬁrearms
regime leave effectively no gap for municipal action. Hawaii's
four-county structure and all-mail system make it the least relevant
target for municipal ordinance work.

\### Coalition opportunities based on EO 14248 participation

Five of six states are actively litigating against EO 14248, creating a
natural coalition. The three states in the 19-state lawsuit (CA, CO, HI)
share legal counsel and coordination infrastructure. Oregon and
Washington's separate but parallel litigation provides complementary
resources. Key coalition opportunities include joint amicus strategies
on § 592 enforcement, coordinated model ordinance drafting through the
AG oﬃces already in litigation partnership, and leveraging the
preliminary injunction framework from Judge Casper's June 13 ruling and
Judge Chun's January 9 ruling as legal precedent for municipal
authority.

Alaska's non-participation isolates it from this coalition
infrastructure — which paradoxically makes it both the state most in
need of municipal intervention and the state where that intervention
faces the most resistance.

\### Speciﬁc gaps that municipal ordinances could ﬁll

The research identiﬁes four concrete gaps where municipal ordinances
enforcing 18 U.S.C. § 592 could provide meaningful protection:

\- \*\*Alaska polling places (all non-school venues):\*\* No ﬁrearms
prohibition exists. Municipal ordinances could establish
armed-presence-free zones around polling places by invoking federal
authority under § 592 and framing the prohibition as enforcement of
federal anti-intimidation law rather than state ﬁrearms regulation.

\- \*\*Oregon outdoor ballot drop sites:\*\* Current protections cover
only public buildings. Municipal ordinances could extend prohibitions to
a deﬁned perimeter around outdoor drop-site locations, particularly if
SB 243 passes.

\- \*\*Washington voting centers (CPL exception):\*\* Municipal
ordinances could close the gap allowing concealed carry by CPL holders
at voting centers, though state preemption makes this legally
challenging without the § 592 federal hook.

\- \*\*Remote/rural polling places across all six states:\*\* Where law
enforcement response times are measured in hours rather than minutes,
preventive ordinances with clear posted prohibitions serve a deterrence
function that after-the-fact prosecution cannot achieve.

\### Strategic sequencing recommendation

The recommended sequencing for municipal ordinance work is: \*\*(1)
Alaska\*\* — highest urgency due to complete absence of ﬁrearms
protections and constitutional carry; \*\*(2) Oregon\*\* — secondary gap
at outdoor drop sites with potential legislative opening via SB 243;
\*\*(3) Washington\*\* — narrower CPL exception gap; \*\*(4) California
and Colorado\*\* — model ordinance development for export; \*\*(5)
Hawaii\*\* — monitoring only, no signiﬁcant gaps identiﬁed.
