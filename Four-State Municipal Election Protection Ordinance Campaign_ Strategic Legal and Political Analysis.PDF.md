\# Four-state municipal election protection ordinance campaign:
strategic legal and political analysis

\*\*The campaign to pass municipal ordinances enforcing 18 U.S.C. §
592—the federal ban on armed troops at polling places—faces a hostile
but navigable legal landscape across Idaho, Indiana, North Dakota, and
South Dakota.\*\* Indiana offers the strongest immediate passage
opportunity through Indianapolis and Bloomington, where Democratic
supermajorities have demonstrated willingness to adopt progressive
ordinances even under preemption threat. North Dakota's constitutionally
mandated liberal construction of home rule charters
\[Reformfargo\](https://reformfargo.org/ballot-initiative) and its deep
tribal voting rights coalition create a uniquely defensible legal
position. Idaho and South Dakota present the steepest challenges—both
are Dillon's Rule states with aggressive attorneys general and
Republican supermajorities—but each contains at least one viable
municipality where passage could generate signiﬁcant strategic and
narrative value.

The federal constitutional foundation is robust. The anti-commandeering
doctrine (\*Printz v. United States\*, \*Murphy v. NCAA\*) and validated
sanctuary-city precedent (\*United States v. California\*) establish
that municipalities cannot be compelled to assist federal operations.
The ordinance's strongest framing is as enforcement of existing federal
criminal law rather than obstruction—18 U.S.C. § 592 makes it a
\*\*ﬁve-year felony\*\* for any person in federal service to station
armed personnel at polling places. \[Legal Information Institute\]
(https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/592) \[U.S. House of
Representatives\](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?
req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section592&amp=&num=0&amp=&edition=prelim)
Every target state shares three common threats: ﬁrearms preemption
statutes that opponents will attempt to weaponize, Republican
legislative supermajorities capable of reactive preemption, and
conservative attorneys general with enforcement mandates. The campaign's
durability depends on drafting discipline, coalition depth, and a
sequencing strategy that builds momentum before opposition can
coordinate across state lines.

\## I. IDAHO

\### 1. Home rule authority constrained by Dillon's Rule

Idaho is a \*\*Dillon's Rule state\*\* \[Water Systems Council\]
(https://www.watersystemscouncil.org/download/wellcare_information_sheets/other_information_sheets/Dillons-Rule.pdf)
where municipalities may exercise only powers expressly granted,
necessarily implied, or absolutely essential.
\[Uslegalforms\](https://legal-resources.uslegalforms.com/d/dillons-rule)
The constitutional foundation is narrow: \*\*Article XII, § 2\*\*
authorizes counties and incorporated cities to "make and enforce, within
its limits, all such local police, sanitary and other regulations as are
not in conﬂict with its charter or with the general laws." \[Idaho
Secretary of State\](https://sos.idaho.gov/elect/stcon/article_XII.html)
\[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-idaho/)
This is not broad home rule—it is limited police power delegation.

Statutory municipal powers under \*\*Idaho Code Title 50\*\* provide
additional authority. \*\*§ 50-302\*\* permits cities to make ordinances
"not inconsistent with the laws of the state of Idaho" to maintain
peace, good government, and welfare. \[Idaho State Legislature\]
(https://legislature.idaho.gov/statutesrules/idstat/title50/t50ch3/sect50-302/)
Idaho courts apply a "reasonable relationship" test: if a local
government can argue a reasonable position, the ordinance will generally
be upheld. \[Bristol George\]
(https://macomberlaw.com/constitutional/police-power/) However, courts
resolve all doubt \*\*against\*\* the municipality under Dillon's Rule.
\[Uslegalforms\](https://legal-resources.uslegalforms.com/d/dillons-rule)
Idaho has \*\*no county home-rule provision\*\*—county oﬃcials are
limited to programs speciﬁcally established by the legislature, making
county-level ordinances functionally nonviable for this campaign.

\### 2. Firearms preemption dominates the preemption landscape

\*\*Idaho Code § 18-3302J\*\* (enacted 2008) declares the legislature's
intent to "\*\*wholly occupy the ﬁeld of ﬁrearms regulation\*\*"
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-idaho/)
and prohibits any political subdivision from adopting any law "which
regulates in any manner the sale, acquisition, transfer, ownership,
possession, transportation, carrying or

storage of ﬁrearms." \[Idaho State
Legislature\](https://legislature.idaho.gov/statutesrules/idstat/title18/t18ch33/sect18-3302j/)
The only local exception is discharge regulation.
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-idaho/)
This statute is the campaign's most dangerous obstacle—opponents will
argue that restricting city assistance to "armed" federal personnel
constitutes ﬁrearms regulation by proxy.

No express preemption of municipal election-protection measures exists
under \*\*Title 34\*\* (election code), which creates a
negative-inference opening: the legislature has preempted ﬁrearms and
has moved to preempt LGBTQ nondiscrimination ordinances (\*\*HB 557\*\*,
2026 session) but has not preempted municipal election-integrity
measures. However, the Secretary of State's comprehensive authority
under \*\*§ 34-201\*\* and county clerks' supervisory role under \*\*§
34-206\*\* \[Idaho Secretary of State\]
(https://sos.idaho.gov/elections/publications/election_laws.pdf) support
an implicit ﬁeld-preemption argument. \*\*Idaho Constitution Article I,
§ 11\*\* further reinforces the pro-gun framework by prohibiting
licensure, registration, or special taxation on ﬁrearms. \[Gun Policy
Research\](https://gunpolicy.org/ﬁrearms/citation/quotes/7708)

The active threat of \*\*HB 557\*\*—a bill to preempt local
nondiscrimination ordinances, authorize AG lawsuits against
non-compliant cities, and which passed the House Local Government
Committee 13-3— \[Idaho
Press\](https://www.idahopress.com/news/local/bill-targeting-protections-for-sexual-orientation-gender-identity-passes-idaho-house-committee/article_3d7d07aa-debb-46ab-a091-6d7f2c02d7d2.html)
demonstrates the legislature's current willingness to weaponize
preemption against progressive municipal action.

\### 3. Constitutional basis and strategic framing

The ordinance's federal foundation is strong. \*\*18 U.S.C. § 592\*\*
makes it a felony for any person "in the civil, military, or naval
service of the United States" to order, bring, keep, or control "any
troops or armed men" at any election site. \[Legal Information
Institute\] (https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/592) \[U.S.
House of Representatives\](https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?
req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section592&num=0&edition=prelim) The
DOJ's own Justice Manual conﬁrms that prosecutors have \*\*"no authority
to send FBI special agents or U.S. marshals to polling places."\*\*
\[Brennan Center for Justice\]
(https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/voters-should-not-be-intimidated)
The anti-commandeering doctrine (\*Printz\*, 1997; \*Murphy\*, 2018)
protects municipalities from being compelled to assist federal
operations, \[Teaching American History\]
(https://teachingamericanhistory.org/document/printz-v-united-states/)
\[Legal Information Institute\]
(https://www.law.cornell.edu/constitution-conan/amendment-10/anti-commandeering-doctrine)
and the Ninth Circuit's validation of California's SB 54 conﬁrms that a
locality's refusal to assist federal enforcement is constitutionally
protected even when it makes federal operations "more diﬃcult."
\[NPR\](https://www.npr.org/2019/04/18/714882333/federal-appeals-panel-upholds-california-sanctuary-state-law)

Idaho's state constitutional provisions offer modest additional support.
\*\*Article I, § 19\*\* guarantees the right of suffrage, and
\*\*Article VI, § 1\*\* establishes voter qualiﬁcations. The ordinance
should be framed exclusively as a \*\*city resource-allocation measure
enforcing existing federal criminal law\*\*—never as ﬁrearms regulation.
The preamble should quote § 592 verbatim and cite the DOJ's own policies
conﬁrming the statute's applicability.

\### 7. Litigation risk assessment

The \*\*Idaho Supreme Court\*\* scores 7.0 on Ballotpedia's
Republican-control scale, with 4 of 5 justices appointed by Republican
governors. The court has applied \*\*minimal rational-basis scrutiny\*\*
to voting rights cases. A state-court challenge would face an
unfavorable tribunal. However, federal challenges would reach the
\*\*Ninth Circuit\*\* via the U.S. District Court for the District of
Idaho—a signiﬁcant advantage, as the Ninth Circuit validated
California's SB 54 sanctuary framework.

The strongest legal argument against the ordinance is that it
constitutes de facto ﬁrearms regulation under § 18-3302J's
ﬁeld-preemption language. \*\*Best response\*\*: The ordinance regulates
city resource allocation, not ﬁrearms; it directs how municipal

employees and equipment are deployed, which is squarely within
police-power authority. The ordinance does not regulate possession,
sale, ownership, or carrying of any ﬁrearm—it directs city operations to
comply with federal criminal law.

Preliminary injunction likelihood: \*\*MODERATE-HIGH\*\* in state court;
\*\*LOW-MODERATE\*\* in federal court.

\### 8. County-level viability: not viable

Idaho has no county home-rule provision. Counties are under stricter
Dillon's Rule constraints than cities. Ada County (Boise area) rescinded
its nondiscrimination ordinance in December 2024, signaling rightward
political drift. \*\*County-level ordinances are not a viable pathway in
Idaho.\*\*

\### 9. Legislative pathway: nonexistent

With a 90-15 Republican supermajority, state legislation is completely
nonviable. The legislature is actively pursuing preemption of
progressive local ordinances. The municipal route is the only option,
and even that route faces near-certain legislative retaliation.

\## II. INDIANA

\### 1. Statutory home rule with signiﬁcant carve-outs

Indiana transitioned from Dillon's Rule to \*\*statutory home rule in
1980\*\* through \*\*IC 36-1-3\*\* (Acts 1980, P.L.211). This was a
legislative grant—not a constitutional amendment—making it vulnerable to
erosion. The core provision, \*\*IC 36-1-3-5\*\*, creates
negative-implication home rule: "a unit may exercise any power it has to
the extent that the power: (1) is not expressly denied by the Indiana
Constitution or by statute; and (2) is not expressly granted to another
entity."

However, \*\*IC 36-1-3-8\*\* enumerates critical powers speciﬁcally
\*\*withheld\*\* from municipalities. For this campaign, three
provisions are paramount: \*\*(7)\*\* no power to regulate conduct
regulated by a state agency; \*\*(8)\*\* no power to prescribe penalties
for conduct constituting a crime or infraction under statute; and
\*\*(12)\*\* "the power to order or conduct an election, except as
expressly granted by statute." This last provision is the most
signiﬁcant preemption risk—election administration is comprehensively
controlled by state law under \*\*IC Title 3\*\*.

Both cities and counties have home rule under IC 36-1-3. Indianapolis
operates under a unique consolidated city-county government
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/City_elections_in_Indianapolis,\_Indiana\_(2024))
("\*\*Unigov\*\*") established in 1970 \[Wikipedia\]

(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indianapolis_City-County_Council) under
IC 36-3-1, meaning an Indianapolis ordinance effectively covers all of
Marion County (~977,000 people). The Indiana Supreme Court, despite
all-Republican appointments, has been characterized as composed of
"ﬂaming moderates" due to the merit-selection Judicial Nominating
Commission process. \[The Indiana Lawyer\]
(https://www.theindianalawyer.com/articles/ﬂaming-moderates-many-say-indiana-courts-remain-centrist-despite-heavy-gop-tilt)

\### 2. Aggressive preemption with enforcement teeth

Indiana's preemption landscape is the most developed among the four
target states. \*\*Firearms preemption\*\* under \*\*IC 35-47-11.1-2\*\*
invalidates all local ﬁrearms ordinances beyond state parameters and
creates a \*\*civil cause of action with treble damages and attorney
fees\*\* (SB 292, 2011).
\[Dixonmoseleylaw\](https://dixonmoseleylaw.com/indiana-gun-laws/sb-292-preemption/)
This enforcement mechanism has effectively deterred local
action—Giffords Law Center notes no signiﬁcant case law interpreting
these statutes because cities simply do not challenge them.
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-indiana/)

\*\*Immigration/sanctuary city preemption\*\* provides the most directly
relevant precedent. Indiana banned sanctuary policies in 2011, \[Indiana
Capital
Chronicle\](https://indianacapitalchronicle.com/briefs/ag-rokita-threatens-legal-action-against-locals-with-sanctuary-city-laws-on-the-books/)
then in 2024 passed \*\*Senate Enrolled Act 181\*\* empowering AG Rokita
to \*\*enforce\*\* the ban and sue non-compliant municipalities. Rokita
sent letters to East Chicago, Gary, West Lafayette, and Monroe County
demanding repeal of "welcoming city" ordinances.
\[WFYI\](https://www.wfyi.org/programs/indiana-week-in-review/radio/todd-rokita-threatens-to-sue-sanctuary-cities)
\*\*Gary initially resisted but ultimately complied\*\* in August 2024—
\[Purdue Exponent\]
(https://www.purdueexponent.org/city_state/politics/west-lafayettes-sanctuary-city-policy-remains/article_66d0a5a8-dc16-11ef-b8b2-8bce22c8e4bc.html)
a cautionary precedent.

\*\*IC 36-1-3-8(12)\*\*'s withholding of election-ordering power from
municipalities is the primary preemption threat. The campaign's
rebuttal: the ordinance does not "order or conduct an election"—it
directs city resource allocation to comply with federal law. Additional
preemption risks include \*\*IC Title 3\*\* (comprehensive election code
as implied ﬁeld preemption) and \*\*IC 36-1-3-8(7)\*\* (if state
agencies are deemed to regulate polling-place security).

A critical pending bill—\*\*HB 1343 (2026 session)\*\*—would establish a
"military police force" giving the governor broad authority to activate
it with statewide police powers, \[Common
Cause\](https://www.commoncause.org/indiana/) directly relevant to
concerns about armed personnel near polling places.

\### 3. Constitutional basis strengthened by Supremacy Clause argument

Beyond the shared federal foundation (§ 592, anti-commandeering,
sanctuary precedent), Indiana's state constitution provides additional
support. \*\*Article 2, § 1\*\* establishes that "all elections shall be
free and equal." The ordinance's framing as enforcement of existing
federal criminal law is particularly potent in Indiana because the
state's own home-rule statute permits any power not "expressly
denied"—and no statute expressly denies municipalities the power to
direct city employees not to assist in federally criminal activity.

The \*\*Supremacy Clause defense\*\* is strong: if the state attempts to
require municipal cooperation with armed federal presence at polling
places, that state action would conﬂict with 18 U.S.C. § 592 and would
itself be preempted by federal law. This creates a constitutional
shield: the municipality is caught between federal criminal law (§ 592,
prohibiting armed federal presence at polls) and any state mandate to
cooperate—and federal law wins.

\## IV. SOUTH DAKOTA

\### 1. Dillon's Rule severely constrains municipal authority

South Dakota is a \*\*strict Dillon's Rule state\*\* where
municipalities are "political subdivisions of the State, created as
convenient agencies for exercising such of the governmental powers of
the State as may be entrusted to them." Municipal powers are limited to
those (1) expressly granted, (2) necessarily implied, or (3) absolutely
essential. The presumption runs against municipal authority.

\*\*Article IX\*\* of the SD Constitution addresses local government but
provides no broad home-rule charter authority comparable to North
Dakota's framework. Former \*\*Article X\*\* ("Municipal Corporations")
was repealed in 1972.

\*\*SDCL Title 9\*\* governs municipal government with enumerated,
conditional powers. The South Dakota Municipal League acknowledges that
municipal powers "almost always" come with "conditions and
restrictions." Courts construe municipal authority \*\*narrowly\*\*.
Counties operate under the same Dillon's Rule constraints via \*\*SDCL
Title 7\*\* (Chapter 7-18A for ordinances). The one procedural
advantage: \*\*Article IX, § 2\*\* and \*\*SDCL 9-20-1\*\* mandate
initiative and referendum for ordinances in all cities—providing a
direct-democracy pathway that bypasses council reluctance.

\### 2. Multi-layered preemption with AG enforcement mandate

South Dakota has enacted \*\*overlapping ﬁrearms preemption\*\* across
all local-government levels: \*\*SDCL § 9-19-20\*\* (municipalities),
\*\*§ 7-18A-36\*\* (counties), \*\*§ 8-5-13\*\* (townships), and \*\*§
11-5-9\*\* (HOAs). The statutes prohibit any local ordinance that
"restricts or prohibits, or imposes any tax, licensure requirement, or
licensure fee on the possession, storage, transportation, purchase,
sale, transfer, ownership, manufacture, or repair of ﬁrearms." The
\*\*AG is mandated\*\* to issue cease-and-desist orders and bring
injunctive relief, with courts required to award reasonable expenses,
costs, and attorney fees to defendants.

\*\*HB 1218 (signed March 2025)\*\* further expanded ﬁrearms preemption
by prohibiting restrictions on concealed carry by government employees
in government buildings—directly relevant because polling places are
often in government-owned facilities. \*\*SB 100 (2025)\*\* allows
enhanced-permit holders to carry on college campuses.

Election law under \*\*SDCL Title 12\*\* is administered at the state
and county level—not by municipalities—creating a jurisdictional
argument that municipalities lack authority to regulate
election-adjacent activities. The \*\*Riot Boosting Act\*\* (SB 189,
2019) demonstrates the state's willingness to deploy punitive
legislation against progressive activism, though the ACLU secured a
permanent settlement blocking its unconstitutional provisions.

South Dakota passed \*\*11 laws from 2018 to 2024 restricting direct
democracy\*\*—the most of any state. Pending \*\*HJR 5003\*\* would
raise the constitutional amendment threshold to 60%. Bills shortening
petition windows and adding geographic requirements continue to advance.
This systematic assault on popular sovereignty makes ballot-initiative
pathways increasingly precarious.

\### 3. Constitutional basis relies heavily on federal framing

South Dakota's state constitutional provisions offer limited independent
support. The ordinance's viability rests almost entirely on the federal
framework: § 592's prohibition, the anti-commandeering doctrine, and
sanctuary-city precedent. The \*\*Supremacy Clause\*\* argument—that a
municipality cannot be compelled to violate federal criminal law—is the
most defensible position. The SD Constitution's Declaration of Rights
(Article VI) includes standard due-process and equal-protection
provisions, but the conservative state judiciary is unlikely to
interpret these expansively for election protection purposes.

\### Four-state sequencing strategy

\*\*Wave 1 — Q2-Q3 2026: Foundation building\*\*

Begin simultaneously in \*\*Indiana\*\* and \*\*North Dakota\*\*. In
Indiana, launch coalition engagement with Common Cause Indiana's "All IN
for Democracy" network and ACLU-IN. Conduct legal drafting workshops at
IU Maurer School of Law and begin formal advocacy with Bloomington's
Common Council. In North Dakota, convene the tribal coalition (NDNV,
NARF, Standing Rock, Spirit Lake, Turtle Mountain, MHA Nation) and begin
drafting a Fargo municipal ballot initiative with Reform Fargo's
infrastructure. The North Dakota ordinance should explicitly cite NDCC §
62.1-01-03.1 as structural precedent.

\*\*Wave 2 — Q3-Q4 2026: First passage attempts\*\*

Push for \*\*Bloomington council vote\*\* in September-October 2026,
timed before November elections to maximize media attention and
voter-mobilization synergy. File the \*\*Fargo municipal ballot
initiative\*\* for inclusion on the November 2026 ballot (or June 2027
municipal election, depending on ﬁling deadlines). Begin preliminary
coalition work in \*\*Idaho\*\* (ACLU-ID, Association of Idaho Cities)
and \*\*South Dakota\*\* (Four Directions, NDN Collective, ACLU-SD, USD
School of Law).

\*\*Wave 3 — Q1-Q2 2027: Expansion and defense\*\*

Following Bloomington passage (expected), immediately launch the
\*\*Indianapolis campaign\*\*—using Bloomington as proof of concept and
leveraging the City-County Council's 19-6 Democratic supermajority.
Prepare for state legislative backlash in both Indiana and North Dakota
(monitoring 2027 legislative sessions for reactive preemption bills). In
Idaho, introduce the ordinance to the \*\*Boise City Council\*\* with
support from the Association of Idaho Cities. In South Dakota, work with
\*\*Vermillion\*\* city council and USD law faculty.

\*\*Wave 4 — Q3-Q4 2027: Consolidation\*\*

Pursue passage in \*\*Indianapolis\*\*, \*\*Fargo\*\* (if not achieved
in 2026), \*\*Boise\*\*, and \*\*Vermillion\*\*. Defend against
preemption challenges through coordinated litigation strategy. File any
necessary federal court actions to establish precedent under § 592 and
the anti-commandeering doctrine. Expand to secondary targets
(\*\*Moscow\*\* in Idaho, \*\*Grand Forks\*\* in ND, \*\*South Bend\*\*
in Indiana) as resources permit.

\### Unifying legal strategy

The four-state campaign should operate under a \*\*single legal
theory\*\* with state-speciﬁc adaptations. The core ordinance template
should:

1\. \*\*Quote 18 U.S.C. § 592 verbatim\*\* in the preamble, establishing
the ordinance as federal-law enforcement

2\. \*\*Direct city employee conduct\*\* (not regulate ﬁrearms), framing
every provision as a resource-allocation and employee-direction measure

3\. \*\*Cite the anti-commandeering doctrine\*\* (\*Printz\*,
\*Murphy\*, sanctuary-city precedent) as constitutional authority

4\. \*\*Include a savings clause\*\* preserving individual federal
employees' right to vote at their assigned polling places (mirroring §
592's own exception)

5\. \*\*Reference DOJ policies\*\* conﬁrming that federal prosecutors
lack authority to send armed agents to polling places 6. \*\*Avoid any
language\*\* that could be characterized as regulating ﬁrearms
possession, carry, or transport

In North Dakota speciﬁcally, the ordinance should cite \*\*NDCC §
62.1-01-03.1\*\* as structural precedent—the state has already embraced
the non-cooperation-with-federal-enforcement principle. In Indiana, the
ordinance should be explicitly framed under \*\*IC 36-1-3-5\*\*'s
negative-implication home rule: the power to direct city employees is
not "expressly denied" by any statute.

Litigation should be coordinated across states to build a body of
favorable precedent. Prioritize \*\*federal court ﬁlings\*\* where
possible (Seventh Circuit for Indiana, Ninth Circuit for Idaho, Eighth
Circuit for Dakotas) to establish § 592 enforcement authority. If any
ordinance is enjoined in state court, immediately reﬁle federal claims
under the Supremacy Clause.

\### Durable strategic value beyond ordinance survival

Even where ordinances face eventual preemption, the campaign generates
lasting value through four mechanisms:

\*\*Narrative establishment.\*\* Each municipal debate forces public
discussion of 18 U.S.C. § 592—a federal statute most Americans have
never heard of. Media coverage of council votes, tribal coalition
advocacy, and AG threats educates voters about the existing federal
prohibition on armed troops at polling places. This awareness persists
regardless of whether the ordinance survives legal challenge.

\*\*Coalition infrastructure.\*\* The campaign builds durable
organizational relationships—between tribal nations and urban
progressives in the Dakotas, between university law clinics and
municipal governments in Indiana and Idaho, between civil-liberties
organizations and election-protection networks. These coalitions can be
activated for future voting-rights campaigns, redistricting battles, and
federal legislative advocacy.

\*\*Legal precedent development.\*\* Even unsuccessful litigation
clariﬁes the boundaries of municipal authority, anti-commandeering
doctrine, and § 592's scope. A favorable federal court ruling in any
single state creates persuasive authority nationwide. The Indianapolis
symbolic-ordinance precedent demonstrates that even preempted measures
can exist without triggering litigation if cities decline to enforce.

\*\*Political cost imposition.\*\* When state attorneys general issue
cease-and-desist orders against election-protection ordinances, they
publicly position themselves as opposing protections for voters at
polling places. This creates political accountability: voters learn that
their AG actively fought to allow armed federal personnel at their
voting locations. In states where AGs face election (all four), this
record becomes campaign material.

The campaign's fundamental strategic insight is that \*\*the ordinance
enforces existing federal criminal law\*\*. Every opponent must explain
why they oppose local compliance with a federal statute that protects
voters. That framing advantage persists whether the ordinance survives a
single legislative session or endures for decades.
