\# Six-state municipal election protection ordinance: legal and
political analysis

\*\*Four of the six Paciﬁc and Mountain West states present Tier 1
conditions for passing municipal ordinances enforcing 18 U.S.C. §
592\*\*, with California, Oregon, and Washington offering the strongest
combined legal frameworks and political environments in the nation for
this campaign. Hawaii's unique four-county structure provides
extraordinary eﬃciency. Alaska and Montana, while constitutionally
favorable, face hostile state executives who will aggressively challenge
local action. The critical strategic ﬁnding across all six states: the
"enforce existing federal law" framing holds up under each state's
preemption framework, the anti-commandeering doctrine protects
non-assistance ordinances in every jurisdiction,
\[Lawfare\](https://www.lawfaremedia.org/article/can-the-u.s.-government-compel-states-to-enforce-immigration-law)
\[Tenth Amendment
Center\](https://tenthamendmentcenter.com/2021/01/04/the-anti-commandeering-doctrine-an-introduction/)
and the three Paciﬁc Coast states each have existing sanctuary law
precedent that directly supports the ordinance's legal structure.
Vote-by-mail systems in Oregon, Washington, and Hawaii require careful
ordinance drafting to deﬁne "polling places" broadly enough to cover
ballot drop boxes, voting centers, and counting facilities.

\## CALIFORNIA: The model jurisdiction with unmatched legal
infrastructure

\### Home rule authority

California offers the \*\*strongest charter city doctrine in the
nation\*\*. Article XI, § 5(a) of the California Constitution grants
charter cities plenary authority to "make and enforce all ordinances and
regulations in respect to municipal affairs."
\[CA\](https://debtguide-api.treasurer.ca.gov/guide-pages/chapter-1-legally-incurring-debt-state-law-restrictions-on-public-agency-debt-and-revenues/1-5-charter-cities/1-5-1-charter-city-powers-municipal-affairs-vs-statewide-concerns)
Critically, Article XI, § 5(b) explicitly enumerates both "conduct of
city elections" and "regulation of the city police force" as
constitutional municipal affairs \[Costamesaca\]
(http://ftp.costamesaca.gov/costamesaca/council/agenda/2012/2012-01-10-S/Attachment-4_California-League-of-Cities-Charter-Primer.pdf)
— the two domains this ordinance squarely occupies.

\*\*Approximately 121–125 charter cities\*\* exist in California,
including all major target municipalities. Home rule is self-executing:
charter cities derive authority directly from the Constitution, not from
legislative delegation. The California Supreme Court in \*Johnson v.
Bradley\* (1992) established the four-part test for charter city
authority and conﬁrmed that election conduct is a constitutionally
enumerated municipal affair. \[US Gov Cloud
API\](https://mccmeetingspublic.blob.core.usgovcloudapi.net/losaltosca-meet-f16c5b7000f0412a905a80c49e06af70/ITEM-Attachment-001-7e6406aea94443a981176cb7ffb9f51d.pdf)
\[Lawpipe\] (https://www.lawpipe.com/California/Johnson_v_Bradley.html)
\*California Federal Savings v. City of Los Angeles\* (1991) established
the "ﬂuid" and "dialectical" nature of the municipal affairs doctrine.
\[Californiacityﬁnance\]
(https://www.californiacityﬁnance.com/CharterCitiesandGeneralLaw.pdf)
\[Paso Robles\]
(https://www.prcity.com/DocumentCenter/View/25607/January-15-2019-City-Council-Agenda-Item-13A-PDF)
\*City of Riverside v. Inland Empire Patients Health & Wellness Center\*
(2013) conﬁrmed that even general law cities exercise broad police power
under Article XI, § 7. \[US Gov Cloud

\### Preemption landscape

ordinance. The state dramatically reformed ﬁrearms preemption in 2022:
AB 1594/SB 1327 declared that local gun safety regulations can only be
preempted by explicit statements in state law, not implied preemption,
provided local regulations are at least as stringent as state law.
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-california/)
This is the most local-government-friendly ﬁrearms preemption framework
in the nation.

\*\*AB 2642, the PEACE Act\*\* (signed September 24, 2024), created a
legal presumption of intimidation for anyone openly carrying a ﬁrearm
while observing voters or election workers, authorized private rights of
action for voter intimidation, and empowered the AG to sue. \[Caguns\]

(https://caguns.net/articles/california-ﬁrearm-laws-the-2026-outlook-2025-recap.12/)

\### Constitutional basis and strategic framing

The anti-commandeering doctrine receives its strongest state-level
application through the Ninth Circuit's SB 54 ruling. Article II of the
California Constitution protects voting rights; Article I guarantees
inalienable rights to "safety, happiness, and privacy." The ordinance's
non-assistance structure mirrors the exact legal framework upheld in SB
54 litigation.

The combination of (1) constitutionally enumerated municipal affairs
over elections and police, (2) the PEACE Act establishing
ﬁrearms-near-elections as presumptively intimidating, (3) SB 54
precedent for non-cooperation, and (4) the 2022 ﬁrearms preemption
reform creates a \*\*virtually impregnable legal position\*\*.

\## OREGON: The nation's strongest home rule meets its oldest sanctuary

\### Home rule authority

Oregon possesses \*\*the strongest home rule tradition in the United
States\*\*. Article XI, Section 2 of the Oregon Constitution (adopted
1906) grants cities plenary power: "The legal voters of every city and
town are hereby granted power to enact and amend their municipal
charter, subject to the Constitution and criminal laws of the State of
Oregon."
\[Gazettetimes\](https://gazettetimes.com/news/local/as-we-see-it-home-rule-is-no-more-in-oregon/article_119f20ee-2506-5fca-9d54-27cfd75266a2.html)
This is self-executing — all \*\*241 Oregon cities\*\* operate under
constitutional home rule authority.
\[Orcities\](https://www.orcities.org/application/ﬁles/4617/1700/1308/Home-Rule-101-updated-8-18-23.pdf)
County home rule under Article VI, Section 10 provides authority
"substantially equal to that of home rule cities." \[Oregon
Legislature\](https://www.oregonlegislature.gov/bills_laws/ors/anc011.html)

Justice Hans Linde's opinion in \*City of La Grande v. Public Employees
Retirement Board\* (1978) established Oregon's preemption framework: the
Legislature may enact substantive policies of statewide concern that
override local ordinances, but cannot enact or amend city charters.
\[Orcities\](https://www.orcities.org/download_ﬁle/1168/1852)
\[Orcities\]
(https://www.orcities.org/application/ﬁles/9817/2106/3885/WhitePaper-OriginsEvolutionandFutureofMunicipalHomeRule-updated5-23.pdf)
The critical distinction for this campaign: Oregon courts apply a
\*\*two-tier preemption analysis\*\*. For civil/regulatory measures
(which this ordinance is), there is a \*\*strong presumption against
preemption\*\*. For criminal measures, courts presume preemption.
\[Orcities\]
(https://www.orcities.org/application/ﬁles/5317/2798/0405/Handbook\_-\_Chapter_2_Home_Rule_and_Its_Limits.pdf)
\[Orcities\] (https://www.orcities.org/download_ﬁle/1168/1852) The
non-assistance ordinance is civil/regulatory, triggering the favorable
presumption.

\### The vote-by-mail factor

Oregon has been an \*\*all-mail voting state since 2000\*\*. There are
no traditional polling places. Election infrastructure consists of
county elections oﬃces, oﬃcial ballot drop sites (hundreds statewide,
open 24/7), and privacy booths at county oﬃces. \[Multnomah County\]
(https://multco.us/info/voting-oregon-vote-mail) \[Clatsop
County\](https://www.clatsopcounty.gov/elections/page/vote-mail-frequently-asked-questions)

(https://oregon.public.law/statutes/ors_260.695) The ordinance must
deﬁne "polling places" to include county elections oﬃces, ballot drop
sites, ballot processing facilities, and any location where voters
physically interact with election infrastructure. This deﬁnitional
challenge is \*\*manageable through careful drafting\*\* and actually
creates an opportunity to establish forward-looking precedent as more
states adopt vote-by-mail.

\## WASHINGTON: Constitutional language tailor-made for this ordinance

\### Home rule authority

Washington provides robust home rule through both constitutional charter
cities (Article XI, § 10) and the statutory code city framework (RCW
Title 35A). The code city system is Washington's most important
framework: \*\*RCW 35A.11.020\*\* declares that code cities have "all
powers possible for a city or town to have under the Constitution" and
those "not speciﬁcally denied to code cities by law." \[Washington State
Legislature\](https://app.leg.wa.gov/RCW/default.aspx?cite=35A.11.020)
The \*\*liberal construction clause\*\* (RCW 35A.11.050) mandates that
code city powers "shall be construed liberally in favor of such cities."
\[MRSC\](https://mrsc.org/stay-informed/mrsc-insight/august-2016/washington-cities-have-more-powers-than-we-think)
\[Washington State
Legislature\](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?
cite=35a.11&full=true)

First-class charter cities include Seattle, Tacoma, Spokane, and
Bellingham. \[Wikipedia\]
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/City_government_in_Washington\_(state))
Six charter counties exist, including \*\*King County\*\* (home rule
charter since 1968). Both charter cities and code cities have broad
authority unless the legislature has expressly denied a speciﬁc power.
\[MRSC\](https://mrsc.org/stay-informed/mrsc-insight/august-2016/washington-cities-have-more-powers-than-we-think)

\### The constitutional provision that changes everything

\*\*Article I, Section 19 of the Washington Constitution\*\* states:
"All Elections shall be free and equal, and \*\*no power, civil or
military, shall at any time interfere to prevent the free exercise of
the right of suffrage.\*\*"
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/constitution/washington/constitution-1.html)
This is \*\*the single most powerful state constitutional provision
identiﬁed across all six target states\*\*. It explicitly prohibits
"civil or military" interference with elections — directly mirroring 18
U.S.C. § 592. A municipality can argue its ordinance enforces both
federal law and the state constitution simultaneously.

\### Preemption landscape — already favorable precedent

Washington \*\*already enacted RCW 9.41.284\*\*, which prohibits
possessing ﬁrearms, dangerous weapons, air guns, and stun guns at ballot
counting centers, voting centers, student engagement hubs, and county
elections oﬃces. \[Handgunlaw\]
(https://www.handgunlaw.us/states/washington.pdf) \[Washington State
Legislature\](https://app.leg.wa.gov/rcw/default.aspx?cite=9.41.284)
This demonstrates that \*\*state policy already recognizes the need to
keep weapons away from election infrastructure\*\*. The proposed
ordinance extends this principle to armed federal operations.

Firearms preemption under \*\*RCW 9.41.290\*\* creates "full ﬁeld

Ct.) held that this preemption does not cover a municipal employer's
authority to regulate employee possession of ﬁrearms on the job —
supporting

the argument that municipal resource allocation decisions fall outside

Maui County, Hawaii County (Big Island), and Kauai County. This makes
Hawaii extraordinarily eﬃcient for the campaign — covering 100% of the
state's population requires only four actions.

Article VIII of the Hawaii Constitution provides the home rule
framework. Section 2 (amended 1968) elevates charter provisions
regarding executive, legislative, and administrative structure above
statute. However, home rule is \*\*not self-executing\*\* — \*Fasi v.
City & County of Honolulu\* (1968) held that county charters are "no
more than a statutory charter, subject to continuing legislative
control." HRS § 46-1.5(13) grants counties power to enact ordinances
protecting "health, life, and property" and preserving "order and
security" not inconsistent with state statute \[Gun Policy
Research\](https://gunpolicy.org/ﬁrearms/citation/quotes/7707) — the
primary statutory hook for the ordinance.

The preemption test under \*Richardson v. City & County of Honolulu\*
(1994) asks: (1) Does the ordinance cover the same subject matter as a
comprehensive state scheme? (2) Does it conﬂict with statute? Since no
comprehensive state scheme governs county cooperation with armed federal
personnel at voting locations, the ordinance should survive.

\### Unifying legal strategy and drafting principles

Five principles should govern ordinance drafting across all six states:

\*\*Non-assistance, never obstruction.\*\* Every ordinance must be
structured as a municipal resource allocation decision — the city will
not deploy its police, facilities, or personnel to assist armed federal
operations near election infrastructure. No ordinance should prohibit,
block, or actively interfere with federal operations. This distinction,
validated by the Ninth Circuit in \*United States v. California\* and
cautioned by \*United States v. King County\*, is the single most
important legal bright line.

\*\*Enforce federal law, don't resist it.\*\* The ordinance's preamble
and operative text should explicitly reference 18 U.S.C. § 592 and frame
the municipal action as local enforcement supporting an existing federal
prohibition. This is the critical distinction from sanctuary-type
measures and inoculates against federal preemption arguments.

\*\*Deﬁne election infrastructure broadly.\*\* For vote-by-mail states
(Oregon, Washington, Hawaii), deﬁne protected locations to include
ballot drop boxes, voting centers, ballot counting centers, student
engagement hubs, county elections oﬃces, and any location where voters
physically interact with election infrastructure. Track existing state
statutory terminology (e.g., Washington's RCW 9.41.284 categories).

\*\*Avoid ﬁrearms regulation territory.\*\* No ordinance should regulate
who may carry ﬁrearms, where ﬁrearms may be carried, or any aspect of
ﬁrearms possession. The operative mechanism is purely municipal resource
allocation — directing city employees and assets. This is essential to
avoid triggering state ﬁrearms preemption statutes in Oregon (ORS
166.170), Washington (RCW 9.41.290), Alaska (AS 29.35.145), and Montana
(MCA 45-8-351).

\*\*Include state constitutional hooks.\*\* Each state's ordinance
should reference its own constitutional voting protections: California's
Article XI, § 5(b) (elections as municipal affairs); Oregon's Article I,
§ 26 ("all elections shall be free and equal"); Washington's Article I,
§ 19 ("no power, civil or military, shall interfere"); Hawaii's Article
II (suffrage); Alaska's Article V (suffrage) and Article I, § 22
(privacy); Montana's Article II, §§ 10 (privacy), 13 ("all elections
shall be free and open"), and 33 (importation of armed persons).

Montana's Article II, § 33 (importation of armed persons) provides a

The campaign should launch in Wave 1 with San Francisco, Berkeley,
Portland, and Eugene — cities where passage is near-certain and legal
frameworks are strongest. Each subsequent wave expands geographic and
political reach while building on the precedent established by earlier
successes. The critical risk across all six states is not legal

framing are well-established) but rather political messaging: ensuring
the "enforce federal law" frame dominates over opponents' inevitable
