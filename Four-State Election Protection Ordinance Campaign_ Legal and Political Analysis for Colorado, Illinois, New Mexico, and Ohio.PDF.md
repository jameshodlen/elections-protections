\# Four-state election protection ordinance campaign: Legal and
political analysis

\*\*Colorado, Illinois, New Mexico, and Ohio collectively represent the
second wave of the national campaign to pass municipal ordinances
enforcing 18 U.S.C. § 592, the federal ban on armed troops at polling
places.\*\* Three of the four states — Colorado, Illinois, and New
Mexico — rank as Tier 1, offering some of the most favorable legal and
political terrain in the nation. Ohio, while presenting the highest risk
due to its Republican trifecta and a state supreme court actively
considering the demolition of its home rule framework, paradoxically
offers one of the most constitutionally robust home rule shields
available. This batch delivers \*\*14–18 viable target
municipalities\*\* across four states, covering an estimated population
of over 12 million residents, with Illinois emerging as potentially the
single most favorable jurisdiction in the nation for this campaign.

The core legal architecture remains the same across all four states:
these ordinances direct that municipal resources shall not be deployed
to assist armed federal personnel near polling places, framing this as
local enforcement of existing federal law rather than sanctuary-type
resistance. The anti-commandeering doctrine (Printz, Murphy, Brackeen)
provides the constitutional backbone, while 18 U.S.C. § 592's
unambiguous criminal prohibition supplies the federal statutory
foundation. Critically, the 2025–2026 sanctuary city litigation has
produced a robust body of law conﬁrming that "refusing to help is not
the same as impeding" — and this ordinance stands on even stronger
ground than sanctuary policies because it declines to assist activities
that themselves violate federal law.

\## I. Federal legal foundation underpinning all four states

\*\*18 U.S.C. § 592\*\* provides the bedrock. The statute makes it a
federal crime — punishable by up to \*\*ﬁve years imprisonment\*\* and
disqualiﬁcation from federal oﬃce — for any person "in the civil,
military, or naval service of the United States" to order, bring, keep,
or have under their control "any troops or armed men at any place where
a general or special election is held." The sole exception is force
"necessary to repel armed enemies of the United States," which legal
scholars at the Brennan Center and Penn Law interpret as applying
exclusively to armed foreign invasion, not domestic law enforcement
operations.

The statute's scope is broader than many realize. The phrase "other
person in the civil, military, or naval service" encompasses civilian
federal agents — ICE, CBP, DHS, FBI, U.S. Marshals — not merely
uniformed military. Congress deliberately removed the original "keep the
peace" exception over a century ago, foreclosing the very "election
security" justiﬁcation a future administration might invoke.

The \*\*anti-commandeering doctrine\*\* is robust and intact. Printz v.
United States (521 U.S. 898, 1997) held that the federal government
cannot "command the States' oﬃcers, or those of their political
subdivisions, to administer or enforce a federal regulatory program."
Murphy v. NCAA (584 U.S. 453, 2018) expanded this, holding the federal
government cannot even prohibit states from repealing their own laws.
Haaland v. Brackeen (599 U.S. 255, 2023) did not narrow the doctrine —
rather, it clariﬁed that commandeering applies when Congress directs
states speciﬁcally, leaving the core holdings fully intact.

The \*\*non-assistance versus obstruction distinction\*\* is now
well-settled. In United States v. California (921 F.3d 865, 9th Cir.
2019), the Ninth Circuit held: "California has the right, pursuant to
the anticommandeering rule, to refrain from assisting with federal
efforts." Even the conservative Fifth Circuit, in City of El Cenizo v.
Texas (890 F.3d 164, 2018), acknowledged that "the Tenth Amendment
prevents Congress from compelling Texas municipalities to cooperate."
The Trump administration's 2025 lawsuits against sanctuary jurisdictions
have largely failed — a federal judge dismissed the suit against
Illinois in July 2025 in a 64-page ruling citing anti-commandeering
principles.

The proposed ordinance occupies \*\*stronger legal ground than any
sanctuary policy\*\*. Sanctuary policies require municipalities to argue
they have a right not to cooperate with enforcement of valid federal
law. This ordinance argues municipalities are declining to assist with
activities that themselves violate federal law. The Supremacy Clause
cannot be invoked against an ordinance that moves in the same direction
as federal statute.

\## II. COLORADO

\### 1. Home rule authority: Article XX and the strongest tradition in
the West

Colorado's home rule framework, rooted in \*\*Article XX of the Colorado
Constitution\*\* (adopted 1902, expanded 1912), is among the most robust
in the nation. Section 6 vests municipalities of 2,000+ inhabitants with
"the full right of self-government in both local and municipal matters,"
and Section 9 declares the article "in all respects self-executing." The
enumeration of speciﬁc powers is illustrative, not exhaustive — and
Section 8 renders conﬂicting constitutional provisions "inapplicable."

\*\*108 Colorado municipalities\*\* hold home rule status. The
constitutional framework distinguishes charter (home rule) cities from
statutory cities (11 total) and statutory towns (159 total). County home
rule is authorized under C.R.S. § 30-35-101 et seq. and Article XIV, §
16, with Pitkin and Weld Counties holding charters and Denver and
Broomﬁeld operating as consolidated city-counties.

The Colorado Supreme Court's framework from \*\*City of Northglenn v.
Ibarra\*\* (62 P.3d 151, Colo. 2003) organizes regulatory authority into
three categories: matters of local concern (municipality prevails),
matters of statewide concern (state prevails), and matters of mixed
concern (coexistence unless conﬂict, in which case state prevails). A
ﬁve-factor totality test applies: need for statewide uniformity,
extraterritorial impact, historical regulation patterns, constitutional
commitment, and need for governmental cooperation.

Critically, the Colorado Municipal League itself has argued — in
opposing extension of the Colorado Voting Rights Act to municipalities
via SB25-001 — that "Article XX, Section 6 explicitly grants
municipalities control and power to regulate all matters pertaining to
municipal elections," citing People ex rel. Tate v. Prevost (134 P. 129,
Colo. 1913) and Kingsley v. City & County of Denver (247 P.2d 805, Colo.
1952). \*\*This establishes that municipal elections are matters of
purely local concern\*\* — a powerful precedent for the election
protection ordinance.

\### 2. Preemption landscape: Uniquely favorable after 2021 ﬁrearms
preemption repeal

Colorado underwent a seismic shift in 2021 when \*\*SB 21-256\*\* made
it the ﬁrst state in the nation to broadly repeal its ﬁrearms preemption
statute. The amended C.R.S. § 29-11.7-103 now permits local governments
to enact ﬁrearms ordinances "unless otherwise expressly prohibited
pursuant to state law." More directly relevant, \*\*C.R.S. §
29-11.7-104\*\* already prohibits carrying ﬁrearms "within any polling
location, or within one hundred feet of a drop box or any building in
which a polling location is located" on election day — demonstrating
legislative recognition that armed presence near polling places is a
legitimate regulatory concern.

The proposed ordinance does not regulate ﬁrearms at all — it regulates
city resource allocation — but even if characterized adjacent to ﬁrearms
regulation, Colorado's preemption landscape is uniquely permissive.

Colorado's \*\*sanctuary state legislation\*\* provides a direct
template. HB19-1124 (2019) prohibits law enforcement from arresting or
detaining individuals solely on civil immigration detainers. SB21-131
(2021) blocks state employees from sharing information with immigration
enforcement without a warrant. HB23-1100 (2023) bans local government
contracts for federal immigrant detention. SB25-276 (2025) extended
these restrictions to all local government employees. Denver's Executive
Order 142 and Ordinance 940-17 already prohibit the use of "any city
funds or resources to assist in the enforcement of federal immigration
laws." Governor Polis has explicitly invoked the anti-commandeering
doctrine, stating: "Colorado, like many states, will not allow the
federal government to commandeer our public safety resources."

\*\*The negative inference is powerful\*\*: Colorado has enacted express
preemptions where it intended them (original 2003 ﬁrearms preemption,
rent control ban in § 38-12-301) and has not preempted local ordinances
regarding deployment of city resources near polling places. The Colorado
Voting Rights Act (SB25-001, signed May 12, 2025) further demonstrates
the legislature's direction of travel — toward expanding, not
restricting, election protection.

\### 3. Constitutional basis

Colorado Constitution Article VII establishes voting rights and election
procedures. Article II, § 10 protects free speech, § 6 protects
assembly, and § 1 guarantees equality and inalienable rights. The
anti-commandeering doctrine is already operationalized through
Colorado's sanctuary legislation. The election protection ordinance
follows the identical legal architecture — extending the non-cooperation
principle from immigration enforcement to 18 U.S.C. § 592 enforcement.
The ordinance does not create ﬁrearms regulations; it directs
city/county employee conduct and resource allocation in support of
existing federal law.

\## III. NEW MEXICO

\### 1. Home rule authority: Limited framework, liberal construction,
unique constitutional asset

New Mexico's home rule derives from \*\*Article X, § 6\*\* of the NM
Constitution (added 1970). Section 6(D) provides that a charter
municipality "may exercise all legislative powers and perform all
functions not expressly denied by general law or charter." Section 6(E)
contains a critical directive: "The purpose of this section is to
provide for maximum local self-government. \*\*A liberal construction
shall be given to the powers of municipalities.\*\*"

The framework operates as a limitation model — charter cities look to
state law for express restrictions, not grants. \*\*12
municipalities\*\* hold home rule charters: Alamogordo, Albuquerque,
Clovis, Gallup, Grants, Hobbs, Las Cruces, Las Vegas (NM), Rio Rancho,
Santa Fe, Los Alamos (consolidated city-county), and others.
\*\*Bernalillo County\*\* also holds a home rule charter (approved by
voters, amended 2022).

Key case law establishes protective standards. \*\*Apodaca v. Wilson\*\*
(525 P.2d 876, N.M. 1974) held that "a home rule municipality no longer
has to look to the legislature for a grant of power to act, but only
looks to legislative enactments to see if any express limitations have
been placed on their power." \*\*State ex rel. Haynes v. Bonem\*\* (845
P.2d 150, N.M. 1992) established that state statutes can override home
rule only if they relate to a "matter of statewide concern." \*\*New
Mexicans for Free Enterprise v. City of Santa Fe\*\* (126 P.3d 1149,
N.M. Ct. App. 2005) upheld Santa Fe's minimum wage ordinance and
rejected implied preemption, requiring clear intent to preempt.

NM's home rule is more limited than Colorado's or Illinois's due to the
"expressly denied by general law" standard, penalties capped at petty
misdemeanor level, and the civil/private law exclusion. But the liberal
construction mandate and the statewide-concern requirement for
preemption provide meaningful protection.

\### 2. Preemption landscape: Constitutional ﬁrearms preemption demands
careful drafting

The most signiﬁcant preemption risk in New Mexico is \*\*constitutional
in origin\*\*. Article II, § 6 states: "No municipality or county shall
regulate, in any way, an incident of the right to keep and bear arms."
This is stronger than mere statutory preemption and demands
\*\*meticulous ordinance drafting\*\*. The ordinance must be framed
strictly as municipal resource allocation and non-cooperation with
federal enforcement — not as ﬁrearms regulation. The "incident of the
right to keep and bear arms" language is broad, but directing which
federal agencies city employees cooperate with is categorically distinct
from regulating private ﬁrearms possession.

On election law, Article VII, § 1 vests the legislature with power to
"regulate the manner, time and places of voting," suggesting state-level
authority. However, the ordinance does not regulate elections — it
governs municipal resource deployment. The negative inference argument
is available: the legislature has enacted speciﬁc preemptions (ﬁrearms,
taxation) but has NOT preempted municipal action on local government
resource allocation near polling places.

The \*\*Immigrant Safety Act (HB 9, 2025)\*\* provides direct precedent.
The NM Senate passed this legislation 24–15, prohibiting state/local
governments from entering 287(g) agreements and banning use of public
land for immigration detention. This demonstrates the state's
willingness to legislate local non-cooperation with federal enforcement
— the identical legal architecture the ordinance employs.

\### 3. Constitutional basis: Article II, § 17 is a unique weapon

New Mexico possesses a uniquely powerful constitutional provision.
\*\*Article II, § 17\*\* states: "All elections shall be free and open,
and \*\*no power, civil or military, shall at any time interfere to
prevent the free exercise of the right of suffrage.\*\*" This language —
explicitly barring both civil and military interference with voting —
directly mirrors the concerns addressed by 18 U.S.C. § 592 and provides
a state constitutional mandate for the ordinance. No other state in this
batch has language this directly on point.

Article VII, § 1 mandates the legislature "enact such laws as will
secure the secrecy of the ballot and the purity of elections and guard
against the abuse of elective franchise." Article VII, § 3 provides
super-entrenched protection against restriction of voting rights based
on religion, race, language, or color — requiring three-fourths of
statewide voters and two-thirds in each county to amend.

\## IV. ILLINOIS

\### 1. Home rule authority: The 1970 Constitution revolutionized
municipal power

Article VII, Section 6 of the \*\*Illinois Constitution (1970)\*\*
provides what is arguably the strongest home rule framework in the
nation. The provision was deliberately revolutionary — the 1970
Constitutional Convention intended to reverse Dillon's Rule and
fundamentally restructure state-local relations. Section 6(a) states: "a
home rule unit may exercise any power and perform any function
pertaining to its government and affairs including, but not limited to,
the power to regulate for the protection of the public health, safety,
morals and welfare."

Illinois grants \*\*automatic home rule\*\* to every municipality with a
population exceeding \*\*25,000\*\* — no charter adoption required.
Smaller municipalities may opt in by referendum. As of 2024, \*\*224
Illinois municipalities\*\* are home rule units. Cook County is the only
home rule county (having an elected chief executive oﬃcer). No charter
is needed; the constitutional text is self-executing.

Key case law has consistently construed these powers broadly.
\*\*Kanellos v. County of Cook\*\* (53 Ill. 2d 161, 1972) established
that the 1970 Constitution "was designed to drastically alter the
relationship which previously existed between local and state
government." \*\*Kalodimos v. Village of Morton Grove\*\* (103 Ill. 2d
483, 1984) upheld a complete municipal handgun ban, articulating a
three-factor test and holding that home rule "is predicated on the
assumption that problems in which local governments have a legitimate
and substantial interest should be open to local solution." \*\*Scadron
v. City of Des Plaines\*\* (153 Ill. 2d 164, 1992) established the
"speciﬁcally limited" standard — the General Assembly must speciﬁcally
limit home rule concurrent exercise for preemption to apply. \*\*City of
Evanston v. Create, Inc.\*\* (85 Ill. 2d 101, 1981) conﬁrmed that "the
local governing body can create an ordinance speciﬁcally suited for the
unique needs of its residents."

The preemption mechanism under Article VII, § 6 requires aﬃrmative
legislative action. Section 6(g) requires a three-ﬁfths vote to deny or
limit home rule powers. Section 6(h) requires speciﬁc legislative
declaration for exclusive state exercise. Section 6(i) permits
concurrent exercise unless the General Assembly "speciﬁcally limit\[s\]"
it or "speciﬁcally declare\[s\]" state exclusivity. This structure
creates a strong presumption favoring local authority.

\### 2. Preemption landscape: TRUST Act provides exact structural
precedent

\*\*Firearms preemption\*\* exists under 430 ILCS 66/90 (Firearm
Concealed Carry Act), which declares regulation of handguns and
ammunition "exclusive powers and functions of the State" and explicitly
invokes Section 6(h) as a denial of home rule powers. However, the
election protection ordinance does not regulate ﬁrearms — it governs
municipal resource deployment. The Protect Illinois Communities Act
(2023, banning assault weapons) notably does NOT preempt home rule
municipalities, allowing stricter local bans to remain — demonstrating
the state's comfort with layered local authority on public safety
matters adjacent to ﬁrearms.

The \*\*Illinois TRUST Act (2017)\*\* and \*\*Way Forward Act (2021)\*\*
provide the exact structural precedent this ordinance requires. The
TRUST Act prohibits state and local law enforcement from participating
in immigration enforcement, detaining individuals on immigration
detainers, or eliciting citizenship information from persons in custody.
The Way Forward Act prohibits state/local government contracts for
federal immigrant detention and requires annual compliance reports to
the AG. Both are non-cooperation ordinances that refuse to deploy local
resources to assist federal enforcement — precisely the same legal
architecture the election

protection ordinance employs. The DOJ sued Illinois over these
provisions in 2025; a federal judge \*\*dismissed the suit\*\* in July
2025 in a 64-page ruling citing anti-commandeering principles.

\*\*Election law preemption\*\* under the Illinois Election Code (10
ILCS 5/) governs election administration, but the ordinance does not
regulate election procedures — it governs municipal resource allocation
regarding armed federal personnel. Illinois courts require express
preemptive intent; there is no applicable ﬁeld preemption doctrine, and
the General Assembly has not "speciﬁcally limited" home rule authority
over local law enforcement resource allocation near polling places.

\### 3. Constitutional basis

Illinois Constitution Article III, § 3 mandates free and equal
elections. Article I provides due process and equal protection (§ 2),
freedom of speech (§ 4), and right to assemble and petition (§ 5).
Article VII, § 10 authorizes intergovernmental cooperation, including
with the United States — framing the ordinance as a resource allocation
decision within the municipality's recognized authority to manage its
own intergovernmental relationships. AG Kwame Raoul has expressly cited
Printz anti-commandeering principles in defending the TRUST Act,
establishing the doctrinal framework within Illinois jurisprudence.

\## V. OHIO

\### 1. Home rule authority: The 1912 Amendment as the sole shield in
hostile territory

\*\*Article XVIII of the Ohio Constitution\*\* (the 1912 Home Rule
Amendment) provides one of the theoretically strongest home rule
frameworks in the nation, adopted during the Progressive Era.
\*\*Section 3\*\* states: "Municipalities shall have authority to
exercise all powers of local self-government and to adopt and enforce
within their limits such local police, sanitary and other similar
regulations, as are not in conﬂict with general laws." \*\*Section 7\*\*
provides: "Any municipality may frame and adopt or amend a charter for
its government."

The critical structural detail is that \*\*Section 3 contains three
distinct clauses\*\*, and they operate differently. Clause 1 grants
power to exercise "all powers of local self-government" — this power
\*\*stands alone\*\* and is not modiﬁed by the conﬂict clause. Clause 2
grants police power concurrently with the state. Clause 3 (the conﬂict
clause) modiﬁes \*\*only Clause 2\*\*, not Clause 1. \*\*This
distinction is mission-critical\*\*: ordinances framed as exercises of
local self-government (Clause 1) — such as management of city resources
and personnel — are generally immune from state preemption, while police
power ordinances (Clause 2) may be preempted by conﬂicting general laws.

Ohio home rule is \*\*self-executing\*\* (Perrysburg v. Ridgway, 108
Ohio St. 245, 1923). Over \*\*300 municipalities\*\* have adopted home
rule charters; all target cities — Columbus, Cleveland, Cincinnati,
Dayton — are charter cities. County home rule is extremely limited; only
\*\*Cuyahoga County\*\* (adopted 2009) and \*\*Summit County\*\* hold
charters. There is \*\*no supremacy clause\*\* in the Ohio Constitution,
making preemption narrower than its federal counterpart.

The \*\*Canton test\*\* (City of Canton v. State, 2002-Ohio-2005) is the
primary analytical framework for preemption. A state statute qualiﬁes as
a "general law" capable of preempting local ordinances only if it: (1)
is part of a statewide and comprehensive legislative enactment; (2)
applies to all parts of the state alike and operates uniformly; (3) sets
forth police, sanitary, or similar regulations; and (4) prescribes a
rule of conduct upon citizens generally. If a statute fails any prong,
it cannot preempt. Additional key cases: \*\*American Financial Services
Ass'n v. City of Cleveland\*\* (2006) established that "a statement by
the General Assembly of its intent to preempt a ﬁeld of legislation does
not trump the constitutional authority of municipalities"; \*\*City of
Dayton v. State\*\* (2017-Ohio-6909) successfully challenged state
preemption provisions that failed Canton test prong 3; \*\*Fondessy
Enterprises v. Oregon\*\* (1986) held that municipal constitutional
authority "cannot be extinguished by a legislative provision."

\### 2. Preemption landscape: The most dangerous in the four-state batch

\*\*ORC § 9.68\*\* is among the most aggressive ﬁrearms preemption
statutes in the nation. It declares the right to keep and bear arms a
"fundamental individual right," preempts ALL local ﬁrearms regulation,
provides \*\*mandatory attorney fees\*\* against noncompliant cities,
and as of April 2025 (SB 58) prohibits ﬁrearm liability insurance
requirements. Pending legislation (February 2026 hearings) would add
\*\*punitive/exemplary damages\*\* against cities enacting local gun
ordinances. The ordinance MUST NOT trigger § 9.68 — it must be framed
strictly as local self-government resource allocation, not as ﬁrearms
regulation.

\*\*The existential threat\*\*: AG Dave Yost is \*\*actively petitioning
the 6-1 Republican Ohio Supreme Court to overrule the Canton test\*\* in
a case involving Columbus tobacco regulation (R.C. 9.681). The Ohio
Mayors Alliance calls this "the nuclear option for home rule rights." If
the Canton test falls, virtually all municipal ordinances become
vulnerable to state preemption, fundamentally destabilizing Ohio's home
rule framework.

\*\*Anti-sanctuary legislation\*\* is advancing. \*\*HB 26 (Protecting
Ohio Communities Act, 2025–2026)\*\* requires state and local
authorities to cooperate with federal immigration enforcement, mandates
participation in federal veriﬁcation programs, and imposes

\*\*10% funding reductions\*\* for noncompliance. The bill has an
emergency clause for immediate effect. Seventeen Republican co-sponsors.
While HB 26 targets immigration cooperation speciﬁcally, its template
could be extended to require cooperation with federal law enforcement
operations near polling places.

Additional preemption battleﬁelds: ﬂavored tobacco (R.C. 9.681,
legislature overrode DeWine's veto), traﬃc cameras (state repeatedly
attempted preemption; Dayton fought back successfully), oil and gas
drilling (Morrison v. Beck Energy, 2015 — municipality could not
regulate under home rule when state had enacted comprehensive general
regulation).

\### 3. Constitutional basis

rights. Article I (Bill of Rights) provides protections for assembly (§
3) and speech/press (§ 11). \*\*Article XVIII, § 3, Clause 1\*\* is the
primary constitutional shield. The anti-commandeering doctrine applies

ordinance's framing as supporting 18 U.S.C. § 592 avoids the "conﬂict"
that triggers preemption under Ohio's narrow conﬂict test — because the
ordinance and federal law move in the same direction.

\### The unifying legal strategy demands ﬁve drafting principles

First, \*\*frame as local self-government, not police power\*\*. In
every state, the ordinance should be drafted as an exercise of the
municipality's inherent authority to manage its own resources and direct
its own employees — not as a regulation of ﬁrearms, elections, or law
enforcement conduct. This avoids ﬁrearms preemption triggers in all four
states and, in Ohio speciﬁcally, places the ordinance under Article
XVIII, § 3, Clause 1 (local self-government) rather than Clause 2
(police power), potentially immunizing it from the Canton test entirely.

Second, \*\*enforce rather than resist federal law\*\*. Every ordinance
must explicitly reference 18 U.S.C. § 592 and frame the municipality's
action as supporting enforcement of an existing federal criminal
prohibition. The preamble should recite the statute's text and
legislative history, note that Congress deliberately removed the "keep
the peace" exception, and declare that the municipality acts in
furtherance of federal law. This neutralizes Supremacy Clause challenges
— there is no conﬂict when local and federal law point in the same
direction.

Third, \*\*maintain the non-assistance/non-cooperation structure\*\*.
The ordinance directs what municipal employees and resources will and
will not do. It does not obstruct, impede, or interfere with federal
operations. It does not authorize municipal employees to physically
prevent federal agents from acting. The line between non-cooperation and
obstruction is well-established in sanctuary litigation and must not be
crossed.

Fourth, \*\*include severability and narrow tailoring provisions\*\*.
Each ordinance should contain robust severability clauses so that if any
provision is invalidated, the remainder survives. Provisions should be
narrowly tailored to apply only within municipal boundaries, only to
municipal resources, and only to the speciﬁc circumstance of armed
federal personnel near polling places during election periods.

Fifth, \*\*build in sunset and review mechanisms\*\*. Including a sunset
provision (e.g., automatic renewal every four years) and a reporting
requirement (annual report to city council on any instances of armed
federal presence near polling places) demonstrates good governance,
provides ongoing data for litigation defense, and makes the ordinance
harder to attack as a permanent expansion of municipal authority.

\## Conclusion: A favorable batch with one high-stakes gamble

This four-state batch is substantially more favorable overall than many
possible combinations. Three Tier 1 states — Colorado, New Mexico, and
Illinois — offer strong home rule, favorable political environments,
supportive AGs, and established non-cooperation legal frameworks.
Illinois in particular may be the single most favorable jurisdiction in
the nation, combining the strongest home rule provisions, a Democratic
supermajority, ongoing TRUST Act defense creating real-time
anti-commandeering precedent, and the possibility of covering 5+ million
residents through a single Cook County ordinance.

Ohio is the deliberate gamble — and the one that could matter most. The
1912 Home Rule Amendment was written precisely for moments when state
power is wielded against municipal self-governance. The amendment exists
because Progressive Era Ohioans understood that cities needed
constitutional shields against hostile legislatures. The campaign in
Ohio is not just about election protection ordinances — it is about
defending the principle of municipal self-governance itself, at a moment
when the Attorney General is asking the state supreme court to gut the
Canton test and thereby dismantle over a century of home rule
jurisprudence. Coordinating with the Ohio Mayors Alliance and the Ohio
Municipal League on Canton test defense should be treated as a parallel
strategic objective of equal importance to passing the ordinances
themselves.

The recommended total budget split across four states: \*\*Illinois
20–25%\*\*, \*\*Ohio 25–30%\*\*, \*\*Colorado 12–15%\*\*, \*\*New Mexico
10– 12%\*\*, with the remaining 20–25% held as cross-state reserves for
litigation defense, communications, and federal legislative
coordination. The campaign should move fastest in Illinois and Colorado
(Wave 1), establish NM and expand CO/IL (Waves 2–3), and treat Ohio as
the high-stakes anchor requiring the most legal investment and the most
careful operational security to avoid triggering retaliatory legislation
before multiple cities have enacted ordinances.

Across all four states, the legal framing remains the campaign's most
powerful asset: \*\*this ordinance enforces federal law — it does not
resist it.\*\* That single distinction transforms the legal and
political calculus, placing the campaign on fundamentally stronger
ground than any sanctuary measure and aligning municipal action with the
plain text of a 160-year-old federal criminal statute that Congress has
only strengthened over time.
