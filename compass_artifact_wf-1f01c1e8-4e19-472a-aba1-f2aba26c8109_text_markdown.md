# Protecting elections from ICE: A municipal policy guide for Minnesota and Wisconsin

**Minnesota and Wisconsin cities have significant but distinct legal pathways to insulate elections from federal immigration enforcement.** Minnesota municipalities operate in a permissive environment with no state preemption and supportive Attorney General opinions, while Wisconsin cities face constraints from a 2017 anti-sanctuary law requiring detainer compliance. The core legal reality is this: cities cannot prevent ICE from operating in public spaces including near polling places, but they can refuse to assist federal enforcement, protect local data, and create policies that reduce the chilling effect on immigrant communities.

The Trump administration rescinded all "sensitive location" protections on January 20, 2025—and critically, **polling places were never federally designated as sensitive locations** under any prior policy. This means election-specific protections must be built at the state and local level using existing constitutional authorities, primarily the anti-commandeering doctrine upheld repeatedly by federal courts.

---

## The legal foundation for municipal action

Municipal authority to limit cooperation with federal immigration enforcement rests on three pillars of constitutional law that have survived extensive litigation.

The **anti-commandeering doctrine**, established in *Printz v. United States* (1997) and strengthened in *Murphy v. NCAA* (2018), prevents the federal government from compelling state and local officials to enforce federal programs. Justice Scalia wrote that "the Federal Government may neither issue directives requiring the States to address particular problems, nor command the States' officers...to administer or enforce a federal regulatory program." This principle has protected sanctuary policies across the country.

Courts have consistently upheld sanctuary policies when challenged. In *United States v. California* (2019), the Ninth Circuit ruled that California's Values Act was constitutional because "California has the right, pursuant to the anticommandeering rule, to refrain from assisting with federal efforts." The Supreme Court declined to hear the appeal. Similarly, Chicago's Welcoming City Ordinance survived challenges through the Supreme Court, and on **July 25, 2025**, a federal judge dismissed a DOJ lawsuit against Illinois, Chicago, and Cook County, ruling their sanctuary policies "reflect a decision to not participate in enforcing civil immigration law—a decision protected by the Tenth Amendment."

**What cities can legally refuse to do:**
- Honor ICE detainer requests without judicial warrants
- Inquire about immigration status of residents
- Allow ICE access to interview detained persons without warrant
- Provide office space, equipment, or personnel for immigration enforcement
- Share information beyond what 8 U.S.C. § 1373 narrowly requires
- Enter 287(g) agreements with ICE

## Minnesota's permissive legal landscape

Minnesota provides one of the most favorable legal environments for municipal immigration protections in the Midwest. **No state law prohibits sanctuary policies**, and recent Attorney General opinions affirmatively support non-cooperation with ICE.

On **February 6, 2025**, Attorney General Keith Ellison issued a formal legal opinion finding that Minnesota law **prohibits** holding individuals based on immigration detainers. The opinion determined that continued detention constitutes an "arrest" under both the Fourth Amendment and the Minnesota Constitution, requiring probable cause that ICE detainers do not provide. A **December 12, 2025** opinion further clarified that sheriffs cannot unilaterally enter 287(g) agreements—authority rests with county boards.

Minneapolis operates under a **Separation Ordinance** (Chapter 19) first enacted in 2003 and unanimously strengthened in December 2025. The ordinance prohibits city employees from inquiring about immigration status, prevents police from taking enforcement action "for the sole purpose of detecting the presence of undocumented persons," and bars use of city resources including surveillance technology and parking facilities for immigration enforcement. St. Paul maintains similar protections under Chapter 44 of its Administrative Code.

Minnesota's **107 home rule charter cities**—including Minneapolis and St. Paul—possess broad authority under the state constitution to adopt local governance structures and policies. State statute also grants both statutory and home rule cities general welfare authority to legislate for "protection of the health, morals, peace, and good order of the community."

**Current legal exposure:** The DOJ filed suit against Minnesota, Minneapolis, St. Paul, and Hennepin County on September 29, 2025, challenging sanctuary policies. On January 12, 2026, the state and cities filed a counter-lawsuit against the federal government to block "Operation Metro Surge" deployments. This litigation is ongoing but does not prevent municipalities from maintaining or strengthening existing policies.

## Wisconsin's constrained but navigable terrain

Wisconsin presents a more challenging legal environment. In **2017, the state legislature passed a law requiring local law enforcement to comply with federal immigration detainer requests** and prohibiting municipalities from adopting policies that prevent cooperation with federal authorities. Violations can result in state funding penalties.

However, the law's scope is narrower than it appears. It focuses on law enforcement detention cooperation, not broader welcoming policies or election protections. Cities like Madison and Milwaukee have maintained community trust policies that work within legal limits by not asking about immigration status during routine interactions, even while technically complying with detainer requirements.

**Madison** has operated as effectively a sanctuary city since 1983, when it declared sanctuary for Central American refugees. Current police department policy states officers "will not arrest or detain anyone solely for a suspected violation of immigration laws" and prohibits self-initiated contact based on suspected immigration status. Cooperation with ICE requires commanding officer approval and is limited to terrorism, espionage, violent felonies, or previously deported felons.

**Milwaukee County** enacted a 2017 ordinance prohibiting county law enforcement from inquiring about immigration status or assisting federal immigration actions. Both Milwaukee County and Dane County appear on the DHS "sanctuary jurisdictions" list published in May 2025.

**Opportunities within constraints:** Wisconsin cities can still adopt policies protecting voter information, designating election workers as non-cooperating with ICE inquiries, restricting use of city election facilities, and creating community trust policies that don't directly contradict the 2017 law. The Wisconsin Elections Commission's **December 2025 vote** to deny DOJ voter data requests demonstrates state-level resistance that cities can build upon.

## The sensitive locations gap around elections

**Federal policy provides no protection for polling places.** The Trump administration rescinded the 2021 "Protected Areas" guidance on January 20, 2025, but even under that Obama/Biden-era policy, polling places, election offices, and voter registration sites were never specifically designated as sensitive locations. The historical list protected schools, healthcare facilities, places of worship, and public demonstrations—but not elections.

ICE has stated it is "not planning operations targeting polling locations" but explicitly reserves discretion: "If a dangerous criminal alien is near a polling location, they may be arrested." Current policy directs agents to use "common sense" with "case-by-case determinations"—no locations are categorically protected and no prior approval is required for enforcement actions.

**Pending federal legislation** (H.R. 1061, the "Protecting Sensitive Locations Act") would codify polling places as sensitive locations with a **1,000-foot buffer zone**, but it has not advanced in Congress.

Cities cannot compel ICE to respect locally-designated sensitive locations. However, they can take meaningful action through three channels:

1. **Restricting local assistance** near election sites
2. **Protecting election worker conduct** through training and policy
3. **Limiting data sharing** that could facilitate enforcement

Federal law provides backup protections: **18 U.S.C. § 592** prohibits stationing "troops or armed men" at polling places, though its application to ICE agents is legally untested. The **Voting Rights Act Section 11(b)** prohibits intimidation, threats, or coercion of voters—plaintiffs need only prove intimidation, not intent—and has been used to address enforcement activity that creates chilling effects.

## Protecting voter registration data from federal access

Voter data protection represents the most concrete area for immediate municipal action. Both Minnesota and Wisconsin maintain statutory frameworks distinguishing public from confidential voter information, and cities can layer additional protections.

**Minnesota Statute 201.091** explicitly limits use of voter information to "elections, political activities, or law enforcement" and prohibits internet publication or transfer to unauthorized parties. Sensitive data—driver's license numbers, Social Security numbers, full dates of birth—are confidential. Secretary of State Steve Simon declined DOJ requests for unredacted voter lists, and the state was sued on September 25, 2025; the lawsuit is pending with a motion to dismiss filed December 23, 2025.

**Wisconsin Statute 6.36** similarly protects dates of birth, driver's license numbers, and Social Security numbers as confidential. The bipartisan Wisconsin Elections Commission voted **5-1 on December 11, 2025** to deny DOJ's demand, directing federal officials to purchase only public data through the state's Badger Voters portal. Wisconsin was sued December 18, 2025.

**Model municipal data protection elements based on California SB 54 and sanctuary ordinances:**
- Prohibit city resources from facilitating immigration enforcement related to election data
- Bar direct or indirect sharing of election records with immigration authorities
- Prevent uploading election data to databases accessible to immigration agencies
- Require documentation and annual reporting on any federal data requests
- Train election workers on proper responses to federal inquiries
- Create exception only for judicial warrants or criminal investigations distinct from civil immigration enforcement

**Colorado's SB 25-276** (May 2025) provides the most recent comprehensive model, explicitly extending prohibitions to municipalities and preventing disclosure of personally identifying information that is not "publicly available" for immigration enforcement purposes.

## Actionable policies from other jurisdictions

Several jurisdictions have established court-tested frameworks that Minnesota and Wisconsin cities can adapt for election protection.

**Chicago's Welcoming City Ordinance** (Municipal Code Chapter 2-173), repeatedly upheld through Supreme Court litigation, prohibits city agencies from stopping, arresting, or detaining based on immigration status or administrative warrants. It bars transfer of individuals to ICE custody, prohibits ICE access to detained persons or city facilities, and eliminates immigration status questions from city forms. The ordinance replaced "citizen" terminology with "person" or "resident" throughout city documents.

**California's Values Act (SB 54)** prevents state and local law enforcement from using resources for federal immigration enforcement, creates safe zones around public institutions, and prohibits sharing personal information with ICE unless already public. The Ninth Circuit upheld the law and the Supreme Court declined to hear the Trump administration's appeal.

**Illinois' TRUST Act** (SB 31), signed by Republican Governor Bruce Rauner in 2017 with bipartisan support including the State Police and Sheriffs Association, prohibits detention based solely on immigration status and bars detainer compliance without criminal warrant. A federal judge dismissed the DOJ's challenge in July 2025.

**New Jersey's Immigrant Trust Act package**, passed in January 2026, includes a Safe Communities Act requiring Attorney General guidance on sensitive locations and a Privacy Protection Act limiting government data sharing—these represent the newest model legislation.

**Key elements for election-specific ordinances:**
- Designate polling places, early voting sites, election offices, and voter registration locations as local sensitive areas
- Prohibit election workers from inquiring about or recording immigration status
- Direct local law enforcement to not cooperate with ICE within specified distance of voting locations (model: 1,000 feet per H.R. 1061)
- Protect voter registration drives as civic activity
- Require judicial warrant for any immigration-related inquiry involving election infrastructure

## Implementation pathway for 2026 elections

Cities in Minnesota and Wisconsin should pursue a layered strategy matching policy ambition to legal constraints.

**For Minnesota cities (Minneapolis, St. Paul, Duluth, Rochester, others):**
- Expand existing separation ordinances to explicitly cover election facilities, workers, and data
- Adopt resolutions designating polling places as local "community trust locations" where cooperation with federal immigration enforcement is restricted
- Train poll workers and election officials on non-inquiry policies and appropriate responses to ICE contact
- Enact data protection ordinances limiting sharing of any election-related information with immigration authorities
- Coordinate with county election officials on consistent policies
- Document and publicly report any federal requests for election data or access to election facilities

**For Wisconsin cities (Madison, Milwaukee, Kenosha, Green Bay, others):**
- Strengthen community trust policies within limits of state law, focusing on election workers and facilities rather than law enforcement detention
- Adopt data protection ordinances similar to Colorado's SB 25-276 framework
- Work with Wisconsin Elections Commission to maintain unified resistance to federal data demands
- Create buffer zone policies for city-owned or -operated early voting sites and election facilities
- Train election workers on privacy protections and proper response protocols
- Advocate for state-level legislation (safe haven bills are in the 2025-27 budget proposal)

**For both states:**
- Partner with state Attorneys General on legal strategy and defense resources
- Coordinate with immigrant advocacy organizations (ACLU, NILC, Immigrant Law Center of Minnesota, ACLU of Wisconsin) for model language and legal support
- Establish rapid response protocols for documenting any ICE activity near election sites
- Create community communication plans in multiple languages to counter chilling effects
- Connect election protection efforts to existing sanctuary/welcoming city infrastructure

## Conclusion: Building durable local protection

The legal framework strongly supports municipal action to protect elections from immigration enforcement, but cities must work within real constraints. Minnesota municipalities have clear authority and supportive state-level allies; Wisconsin cities operate in a more constrained environment requiring creative approaches focused on data protection and community trust rather than direct non-cooperation with detention requests.

**Three insights should guide 2026 strategy:**

First, the gap in federal policy presents both risk and opportunity. Because polling places have never been federally protected sensitive locations, cities cannot rely on federal restraint—but they also have space to establish local norms without federal preemption.

Second, data protection represents the most actionable and legally defensible category for municipal action. Both Minnesota and Wisconsin have strong state-level frameworks limiting voter data disclosure; cities can layer additional protections and procedural requirements that slow or prevent federal access.

Third, the constitutional anti-commandeering doctrine provides robust legal protection for non-cooperation policies, but cities cannot prevent federal agents from operating in public spaces. The most effective protection comes from reducing the chilling effect on immigrant voters through community communication, worker training, and visible policy commitments—not from attempting to exclude ICE from the vicinity of polling places.

Cities that act before the 2026 election cycle will establish precedents, train workers, and build community trust that passive jurisdictions will lack. The legal tools exist; implementation requires political will and thoughtful policy design.
