\# Five-state municipal election protection ordinance campaign analysis

\*\*The Northeastern corridor of Rhode Island, Massachusetts, Maryland,
Delaware, and Vermont represents the most uniformly favorable
multi-state batch yet analyzed for the Protections for Elections
campaign.\*\* All ﬁve states feature Democratic trifectas (or
near-trifectas), allied Attorneys General, and established
non-cooperation frameworks with federal agencies. Three states (Rhode
Island, Massachusetts, Maryland) qualify as Tier 1 — highly viable;
Vermont rates Tier 2 due to Dillon's Rule constraints offset by its
unique town meeting tradition; and Delaware rates Tier 2 for a statewide
legislative pathway despite Tier 3 municipal constraints. Unlike
previous batch analyses (Kansas/Missouri/Nebraska/Iowa; Southeast
states), every state here features an AG who is a likely \*ally\* rather
than \*adversary\*, fundamentally transforming the risk calculus. The
critical legal challenge across all ﬁve states is careful drafting that
frames the ordinance as municipal resource allocation — avoiding both
ﬁrearms preemption triggers and election regulation prohibitions — while
anchoring ﬁrmly to 18 U.S.C. § 592's federal statutory mandate.

\## RHODE ISLAND

\### 1. Home rule authority

Rhode Island operates under \*\*Article XIII of the RI Constitution\*\*
(the Home Rule Amendment, adopted 1986), which grants every city and
town the right to self-government in all local matters. Section 2
permits municipalities to adopt charters and enact local laws "not
inconsistent with this constitution and laws enacted by the general
assembly."
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/constitution/rhode-island/C13.html)
However, the General Assembly retains sweeping power under Section 4 to
legislate on municipal matters through general laws applicable to all
municipalities
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/constitution/rhode-island/C13.html) or
through special legislation approved by local voters.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/constitution/rhode-island/C13.html)
Section 5 prohibits municipalities from levying taxes or borrowing
except as authorized by the General Assembly. \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/constitution/rhode-island/C13.html)

Rhode Island's home rule is best characterized as \*\*constitutional but
weak\*\* — a hybrid between Dillon's Rule and full home rule where the
General Assembly maintains dominant authority. Currently \*\*36 of 39
municipalities\*\* have adopted home rule charters (7 of 8 cities, 29 of
31 towns). \[Ri\]
(https://municipalﬁnance.ri.gov/sites/g/ﬁles/xkgbur546/ﬁles/documents/resources/Home_Rule_Charter_Publication.pdf)
The state has no county government whatsoever; all government functions
operate at the municipal level.

Key case law establishes the boundaries of municipal authority.
\*\*Bruckshaw v. Paolino, 557 A.2d 1221 (R.I. 1989)\*\* held that
employee pension regulation was a "local matter" and struck down a
Providence-speciﬁc act not submitted to voters, establishing that the
General Assembly cannot intrude on charter city local matters except
through general law or voter-approved special legislation. \[Leagle\]
(https://www.leagle.com/decision/19891778557a2d122111756) \*\*Westerly
Residents v. Brancato, 565 A.2d 1262 (R.I. 1989)\*\* similarly upheld
home rule authority over sewer infrastructure.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/cases/rhode-island/supreme-court/1989/565-a-2d-1262.html)
Most critically for this campaign, \*\*Sullivan v. Town of Coventry &
Town of Middletown (2010)\*\* held that municipalities properly used
authority under \*\*§ 45-6-1(a)\*\* to regulate ﬁrearm discharge as "a
matter of local concern," ﬁnding that "regulating ﬁrearms in the
interest of public safety has traditionally been the domain of
municipalities."
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-rhode-island/)

Governance structures include strong mayor-council systems (Providence,
Pawtucket, Cranston, Warwick, Woonsocket), town council/town manager
models (most towns), and ﬁnancial town meetings in some smaller
communities. This diversity provides multiple procedural pathways for
ordinance passage.

\### 2. Preemption landscape

\*\*Firearms preemption\*\* is the primary legal risk. \*\*R.I. Gen.
Laws § 11-47-58\*\* provides: "The control of ﬁrearms, ammunition, or
their component parts regarding their ownership, possession,
transportation, carrying, transfer, sale, purchase, purchase delay,
licensing, registration, and taxation shall rest solely with the state."
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2013/title-11/chapter-11-47/section-11-47-58)
However, \*Sullivan v. Town of Middletown\* (2010) found that the
legislature's 1999 re-amendment of § 45-6-1 without removing its ﬁrearms
language suggested concurrent municipal authority on public safety
matters survives. \[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-rhode-island/)
Giffords Law Center reports no signiﬁcant cases further interpreting §
11-47-58, leaving its scope somewhat open.
\[Giffords\](https://lawcenter.giffords.org/local-authority-to-regulate-ﬁrearms-in-rhode-island/)

The ordinance can be carefully drafted to \*\*avoid triggering ﬁrearms
preemption entirely\*\*. It does not regulate ownership, possession,
carrying, or any enumerated activity. It regulates municipal resource
allocation — directing city personnel and equipment not to assist armed
federal operations near polling places.

\*\*Election law preemption\*\* under Title 17 creates a secondary risk.
The Board of Elections administers elections statewide, and \*\*§
17-19-21\*\* governs polling place arrangements.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/rhode-island/2021/title-17/chapter-17-19/section-17-19-21/)
However, the ordinance does not regulate election administration; it
governs municipal resource deployment — a fundamentally different
category.

The \*\*sanctuary infrastructure\*\* provides powerful precedent. Rhode
Island, Providence, and Central Falls were placed on the Trump DOJ's
"sanctuary jurisdiction" list in May/July 2025. Providence already has
an active sanctuary ordinance (the "Providence Community– Police
Relations Act"), and the City Council ﬁnalized limits on police
cooperation with ICE in November 2025. \[The Public's Radio\]
(https://thepublicsradio.org/immigration/providence-holds-off-on-stronger-immigrant-legal-protections-for-now/)
The ACLU of Rhode Island is actively developing model sanctuary
language. \[The Public's
Radio\](https://thepublicsradio.org/immigration/providence-holds-off-on-stronger-immigrant-legal-protections-for-now/)
Rhode Island has \*\*no anti-sanctuary laws\*\* and no state mandates on
immigration enforcement cooperation. This entire framework maps directly
onto the proposed ordinance's non-assistance structure.

\### 3. Constitutional basis for the ordinance

\*\*RI Constitution Article II, Section 1\*\* establishes a state
constitutional right to vote for every citizen 18 or older with 30 days'
residency.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/constitution/rhode-island/C02.html)
Article I (Declaration of Rights) establishes foundational rights
including due process (§ 2), security against unreasonable searches (§
21), and — notably — \*\*does not contain an explicit right to keep and
bear arms\*\*, reducing 2A-based opposition arguments.

The \*\*anti-commandeering doctrine\*\* has been expressly invoked by AG
Neronha in a January 2025 joint statement with 10 other state AGs: "It
is well-established—through longstanding Supreme Court precedent—that
the U.S. Constitution prevents the federal government from commandeering
states to enforce federal laws... the federal government cannot 'impress
into its service—and at no cost to itself— the police oﬃcers of the 50
States.'" \[Rhode Island Attorney General's
Oﬃce\](https://riag.ri.gov/press-releases/joint-statement-attorney-general-neronha-and-10-state-attorneys-general-state-and)
This statement applies directly to the proposed ordinance.

\*\*18 U.S.C. § 592\*\* criminalizes any federal oﬃcer who "orders,
brings, keeps, or has under his authority or control any troops or armed
men at any place where a general or special election is held" with up to
ﬁve years imprisonment and disqualiﬁcation from oﬃce. The only exception
is repelling "armed enemies of the United States." \[Brennan Center for
Justice
+2\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/federal-and-state-election-laws-ban-federal-forces-polling-places)
The Brennan Center's February 2025 analysis conﬁrmed that the removal of
the "keep the peace" exception over 100 years ago means the current
administration "can't justify sending federal forces to polling places
by claiming that state and local police are simply failing to maintain
order." \[Brennan Center for Justice\]
(https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/federal-and-state-election-laws-ban-federal-forces-polling-places)

\## MASSACHUSETTS

\### 1. Home rule authority

The \*\*Massachusetts Home Rule Amendment (Article 89)\*\*, adopted
1966, fundamentally restructured municipal authority. Section 6
provides: "Any city or town may, by the adoption, amendment, or repeal
of local ordinances or by-laws, exercise any power or function which the
general court has power to confer upon it, which is not inconsistent
with the constitution or laws enacted by the general court."
\[Mass.gov\](https://www.mass.gov/doc/home-rule/download) \[Gun Policy
Research\] (https://gunpolicy.org/ﬁrearms/citation/quotes/7717) The
complementary \*\*Home Rule Procedures Act (M.G.L. Chapter 43B)\*\*
establishes implementation mechanisms.
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-massachusetts/)

\*\*Section 7 contains a critical limitation\*\*: municipalities may not
"(1) regulate elections other than those prescribed by sections three
and four." \[Massachusetts Municipal
Association\](https://www.mma.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/article89_0.pdf)
This is the \*\*most signiﬁcant legal risk\*\* in Massachusetts. The
ordinance must be framed exclusively as municipal resource
allocation/police power — never as election regulation.

Massachusetts transitioned from full Dillon's Rule to home rule with
Article 89. \[Mass.gov\](https://www.mass.gov/doc/home-rule/download)
Under M.G.L. Ch. 43B, § 13, ordinance authority is self-executing —
municipalities do not need enabling legislation provided they avoid
conﬂict with state law.
\[Handgunlaw\](https://www.handgunlaw.us/states/massachusetts.pdf) The
AG's Municipal Law Unit reviews town bylaws for consistency
\[Mass.gov\](https://www.mass.gov/doc/home-rule/download) (city
ordinances do not require AG review). Governance structures span open
town meetings, representative town meetings, and city councils, with
unique forms like Cambridge's Plan E (council-manager with proportional
representation). \[Massachusetts Municipal Association\]
(https://www.mma.org/members/msa/massachusetts-select-board-handbook/chapter-4-organization-of-town-government/resources-3/)

Key case law favors municipal authority. \*\*Bloom v. Worcester, 363
Mass. 136 (1973)\*\* affords municipalities "considerable latitude" in
self-governance,
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/cases/massachusetts/supreme-court/volumes/488/488mass319.html)
requiring that preemptive legislative intent "must be clear."
\[Lexington,
MA\](https://www.lexingtonma.gov/DocumentCenter/View/6839/Town-Counsel-Memo-on-Potential-Legal-Concerns-PDF)
\*\*Town of Amherst v. Attorney General, 398 Mass. 793 (1986)\*\* — a
landmark directly on point — upheld a local ﬁrearm discharge bylaw,
holding that "the existence of legislation on a subject is not
necessarily a bar to the enactment of local ordinances... on the same
subject." \[Lexington,
MA\](https://www.lexingtonma.gov/DocumentCenter/View/6839/Town-Counsel-Memo-on-Potential-Legal-Concerns-PDF)
\[LawShun\](https://lawshun.com/article/can-local-laws-conﬁlct-with-state-laws-in-massachusetts)
\*\*Grace v. Brookline, 379 Mass. 43 (1979)\*\* requires a "sharp
conﬂict" before invalidating local regulation. \[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-massachusetts/)
\*\*Connors v. City of Boston, 430 Mass. 31 (1999)\*\* established the
modern three-part preemption test (express, ﬁeld, conﬂict). \[Gun Policy
Research\] (https://gunpolicy.org/ﬁrearms/citation/quotes/7717)

\### 2. Preemption landscape

\*\*Massachusetts has no express state preemption of local ﬁrearms
regulation.\*\*
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/gun-laws/policy-areas/other-laws-policies/preemption-of-local-laws/)
This is conﬁrmed by Giffords Law Center and multiple sources. The 2024
Act Modernizing Firearms Laws (H.4885), signed by Governor Healey on
July 25, 2024, signiﬁcantly strengthened state gun laws
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gun_laws_in_Massachusetts)
but did not add ﬁrearms preemption. Under H.4885, ﬁrearms are prohibited
in government buildings (M.G.L. c. 269, § 10(k)),
\[Handgunlaw\](https://www.handgunlaw.us/states/massachusetts.pdf) which
actually \*supports\* the ordinance's framework.

Massachusetts has \*\*no anti-sanctuary laws\*\*. The state trends
strongly protective: the \*\*Safe Communities Act\*\* (H.2580/S.1681
\[Progressive Mass\](https://www.progressivemass.com/issues/sca-action/)
in the 194th General Court), sponsored by Sen. Jamie Eldridge and Rep.
Manny Cruz/Rep. Priscila Sousa, would limit local cooperation with
federal immigration enforcement. \[Progressive
Mass\](https://www.progressivemass.com/issues/sca-action/) The
\*\*Boston Trust Act (2014)\*\* limits police involvement with ICE and
is currently being defended against Trump administration challenge.
Cambridge declared itself a sanctuary city in 1985 (reaﬃrmed 2006);
Northampton issued a 2014 executive order on ICE detainers; Amherst
passed a 2017 sanctuary bylaw at Town Meeting. The SJC ruled in 2017
that Massachusetts law does not authorize holding people solely on
federal immigration detainer requests.

The SJC preemption framework (\*Connors\*) requires: (1) express
legislative intent to forbid local activity, (2) legislation so
comprehensive that local action would frustrate it, or (3) "sharp
conﬂict" between local and state law. \[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-massachusetts/)
\[Gun Policy Research\]
(https://gunpolicy.org/ﬁrearms/citation/quotes/7717) The proposed
ordinance satisﬁes none of these triggers.

\## DELAWARE

\### 1. Home rule authority

Delaware is a \*\*strict Dillon's Rule state\*\* — among the most
constrained in the nation for municipal authority. Local governments may
only exercise powers expressly granted by the state, necessarily
implied, or absolutely essential to declared purposes. Any doubt is
resolved against local authority. The Delaware Constitution contains
\*\*no separate article granting home rule\*\* to municipalities;
Article IX addresses business corporations, not municipal governance.

Limited home rule exists through \*\*Del. Code Title 22, Chapter 8\*\*
(Municipal Home Rule Act). Municipalities with population exceeding
1,000 may amend their charters through a supermajority process: either a
resolution with \*\*3/4 concurrence\*\* of all legislative body members,
or a charter commission elected via petition bearing \*\*10% of qualiﬁed
voters' signatures\*\*. After local referendum approval, the charter
amendment must be ﬁled with the Governor and legislative leaders. The
General Assembly then has \*\*30 calendar days to negate the amendment
by 2/3 vote of each house\*\* — a legislative veto system making home
rule contingent on state acquiescence.

\*\*Title 22 § 835(a)\*\* explicitly prohibits charter amendments that
would contravene any general state statute, change voter qualiﬁcations,
change municipal election dates, alter boundary procedures, or
\*\*restrict or license ﬁrearms\*\*. Wilmington operates under a special
legislative charter as Delaware's only city exceeding 50,000 population,
with 13 council members. Counties derive power from Title 9 and operate
differently from municipalities.

\### 2. Preemption landscape

\*\*Firearms preemption is a critical barrier.\*\* \*\*Del. Code Title
22, § 111(a)\*\* provides: "The municipal governments shall enact no
law, ordinance or regulation prohibiting, restricting or licensing the
ownership, transfer, possession or transportation of ﬁrearms or
components of ﬁrearms or ammunition except that the discharge of a
ﬁrearm may be regulated." \*\*Title 22, § 835(a)(6)\*\* prohibits
charter amendments restricting ﬁrearms. \*\*Title 9, § 330(c)\*\*
extends identical preemption to county governments. Municipalities may
restrict ﬁrearms only in police stations and municipal buildings with
conspicuous signage and exemptions for permit holders and law
enforcement.

The ordinance does not regulate ﬁrearms ownership, transfer, possession,
or transportation — it directs municipal resources. This distinction is
legally sound but will be aggressively challenged. No signiﬁcant case
law interprets these preemption provisions, creating both uncertainty
and the absence of adverse precedent.

Delaware's \*\*non-cooperation infrastructure\*\* provides strong
precedent. \*\*HB 182\*\* (signed July 14, 2025) prohibits Delaware law
enforcement from entering 287(g) agreements with ICE. Governor Meyer
directed state police not to cooperate with ICE in most situations in
February 2025. New Castle County has operated under a sanctuary-type
executive order since 2017. Delaware has \*\*no anti-sanctuary laws\*\*.

\### 3. Constitutional basis

\*\*Delaware Constitution Article I, § 3\*\* guarantees free elections
and every citizen's entitlement to vote. \*\*Article V\*\* governs
elections at the state level. \*\*Article I, § 20\*\* contains a right
to keep and bear arms provision, but the ordinance does not restrict
this right. The anti-commandeering doctrine provides federal
constitutional support, and HB 182's 287(g) ban establishes the
non-cooperation framework at the state level.

\## VERMONT

\### 1. Home rule authority

Vermont is a \*\*Dillon's Rule state\*\* — one of only 11 whose
constitution does not grant municipal home rule. \*City of Montpelier v.
Barnett, 191 Vt. 441 (2012)\* conﬁrmed: "We have adopted Dillon's Rule,

speciﬁcally authorized by the legislature, and such additional functions
as may be incident, subordinate, or necessary to the exercise thereof.'"

or prohibit the use or discharge, but not possession of, ﬁrearms" (with
consistency requirement to § 2295); (14) power to deﬁne public nuisances
and provide for their abatement "as the public health, safety, or
welfare may require"; and (15) power to provide penalties for

ordinance violations. Charter cities (Burlington, Montpelier, South
Burlington, Winooski, Brattleboro) may have \*\*broader authority\*\*
than general-law towns. \*SBC Enterprises v. City of South Burlington\*
held that a charter can provide authorization independent of § 2291.
Critically, \*\*Ferry v. City of Montpelier (2023)\*\* upheld noncitizen
voting in local elections under charter authority, demonstrating an
expansive view of charter powers for local governance.

Vermont's \*\*town meeting tradition\*\* provides a transformative
strategic advantage. Annual town meetings held on the \*\*ﬁrst Tuesday
in March\*\* (a state holiday) constitute direct democracy where
registered voters speak and vote. A petition signed by just \*\*5% of
registered voters\*\* (17 V.S.A. § 2642) places an article on the town
warning. In Montpelier, this means approximately 200 signatures; in
Winooski, approximately 365; in Burlington, approximately 2,250.
Articles can proceed by open ﬂoor vote or Australian ballot. Special
meetings require the same 5% petition with 30-40 days' notice.

\### 2. Preemption landscape

\*\*Firearms preemption\*\* under \*\*24 V.S.A. § 2295\*\* provides: "no
town, city, or incorporated village... shall directly regulate... the
possession, ownership, transportation, transfer, sale, purchase,
carrying, licensing, or registration of traps, ﬁrearms, ammunition, or
components of ﬁrearms or ammunition." Exceptions allow municipalities to
regulate "use or discharge" (but not possession) under § 2291(8).
\*Hunters, Anglers & Trappers Ass'n v. Winooski Valley Park District\*
held § 2295's "except as otherwise provided by law" clause creates
additional exceptions. A VT AG opinion ruled § 2295 doesn't prevent
voluntary background checks because they don't "directly regulate"
ﬁrearms.

The ordinance does not directly regulate ﬁrearms. It directs municipal
employees and resources — a resource allocation decision fundamentally
different from ﬁrearms regulation. The analogy to sanctuary policies,
which Vermont already practices extensively, is precise.

Vermont's \*\*sanctuary infrastructure\*\* is highly developed. The
statewide \*\*Fair and Impartial Policing Policy (FIPP)\*\* prohibits
local police from investigating immigration status or holding people on
ICE detainers. Nine jurisdictions adopted strengthened "No Polimigra"
policies. Burlington, Montpelier, and Winooski were identiﬁed as
"sanctuary jurisdictions" by DHS in May 2025. Montpelier designated
itself a sanctuary city in 2016. Vermont has \*\*no anti-sanctuary
laws\*\*.

\### 3. Constitutional basis

\*\*Vermont Constitution Chapter I, Article 16\*\* provides an
extraordinarily powerful foundation: "the people have a right to bear
arms for the defence of themselves and the State — \*\*and as standing
armies in time of peace are dangerous to liberty, they ought not to be
kept up; and that the military should be kept under strict subordination
to and governed by the civil power.\*\*" Article 17 further restricts
martial law. These provisions \*\*directly align with 18 U.S.C. § 592's
purpose\*\* of preventing armed federal forces at polling places.

\*\*Article 8\*\* guarantees "all elections ought to be free and without
corruption" and voters' rights to "elect oﬃcers, and be elected into
oﬃce." Articles 13 (free speech) and 9 (citizens' duties) provide
additional protections. The anti-commandeering doctrine (\*Printz\*,
\*Murphy\*) ﬁrmly supports the non-assistance framework. Vermont's own
Governor has stated the state's response "DOES defend the State against
federal overreach" based on the 10th Amendment.

\### 7. Litigation strategy

\### 8. Legislative pathway

Statewide legislation is viable but faces the \*\*Governor Scott veto
risk\*\*. The legislature could: (1) pass standalone election protection
legislation (veto likely, override uncertain with reduced majority), or
(2) grant explicit municipal authority to enact polling place protection
ordinances, solving the Dillon's Rule problem. Key allies include Senate
President Pro Tem \*\*Phil Baruth (D)\*\*, Speaker \*\*Jill Krowinski
(D-Burlington)\*\*, and Progressive Party members. The 2026 session
convenes in January.

The recommended strategy prioritizes \*\*municipal charter-city
ordinances\*\* (avoiding both Dillon's Rule and gubernatorial veto)
while pursuing enabling legislation as a secondary track.

\### Unifying legal strategy and drafting principles

Five principles must govern ordinance drafting across all ﬁve states:

First, \*\*resource allocation framing is mandatory\*\*. Every ordinance
must be structured as directing municipal personnel, equipment,
facilities, and information systems — never as regulating ﬁrearms,
elections, or federal personnel conduct. The operative language should
prohibit "deploying, assigning, or authorizing city/county
\[personnel/resources\] to assist, support, facilitate, or provide
logistical support to armed federal personnel operating within \[X
feet\] of designated polling places during election periods."

Second, \*\*18 U.S.C. § 592 must anchor every ﬁndings section\*\*. The
ordinance enforces existing federal law; it does not create new
restrictions. The whereas clauses should quote the statute, cite its
criminal penalties (up to 5 years imprisonment), note the narrow "armed
enemies" exception, and reference the Brennan Center analysis conﬁrming
that the "keep the peace" exception was removed over a century ago.

Third, \*\*anti-commandeering doctrine provides the constitutional
shield\*\*. Cite \*Printz v. United States, 521 U.S. 898 (1997)\* and
\*Murphy v. NCAA, 584 U.S. 453 (2018)\* explicitly. Note that each
state's AG has already invoked this doctrine in the immigration context
— the ordinance extends the same principle to election protection.

Fourth, \*\*non-assistance language must be precise and narrow\*\*. The
ordinance prohibits assistance — not obstruction. Municipal employees
are not directed to interfere with federal operations; they simply do
not participate. Model on the TRUST Act framework (Maryland), Boston
Trust Act (Massachusetts), and FIPP (Vermont) language already upheld or
unchallenged in these jurisdictions.

Fifth, \*\*state-speciﬁc constitutional hooks should be
incorporated\*\*. Maryland's Free Elections Clause (Art. 7) and absence
of a state RKBA provision; Vermont's Article 16 (standing armies
dangerous to liberty); Rhode Island's absence of an explicit RKBA
provision; Massachusetts' narrow \*Davis\* interpretation of Article
XVII. Each strengthens the local ordinance against state-level
challenge.

\### What each state uniquely contributes to the national campaign

\*\*Maryland\*\* delivers the \*\*scale and precedent model\*\*. Charter
county ordinances covering Montgomery County (~1 million) and Prince
George's County (~967,000) demonstrate that election protection
non-assistance can operate at population scales comparable to many
entire states. The TRUST Act provides the cleanest legal precedent for
non-cooperation framing. Maryland's proximity to Washington, D.C.
guarantees national media coverage.

\*\*Massachusetts\*\* provides the \*\*legal and academic
infrastructure\*\*. The Harvard Election Law Clinic's involvement
legitimizes the campaign's legal framework. The absence of ﬁrearms
preemption creates the strongest legal environment for establishing
precedent. Cambridge's 80-year proportional representation tradition and
Amherst's landmark \*Town of Amherst v. AG\* (1986) case demonstrate
that Massachusetts municipalities pioneered and sustained exactly this
type of local governance innovation.

\*\*Vermont\*\* offers the \*\*direct democracy narrative\*\*. Town
meeting passage transforms the campaign from "politicians passing
ordinances" to "citizens directly protecting their own elections." The
constitutional framing under Article 16 — standing armies are "dangerous
to liberty" and must be "subordinate to civil power" — provides the most
powerful rhetorical foundation of any state. Burlington's 40-year
Progressive Party tradition adds historical depth.

\*\*Rhode Island\*\* demonstrates \*\*comprehensive coverage
feasibility\*\*. With no county government and only 39 municipalities, a
successful municipal campaign could achieve statewide coverage through
sequential municipal passage — something impossible in larger states. AG
Neronha's explicit anti-commandeering statements provide
executive-branch validation. The state's small size enables rapid
expansion from initial passage to statewide adoption.

\*\*Delaware\*\* proves the \*\*statewide legislative pathway\*\*. If HB
182's 287(g) ban framework is successfully adapted to election
protection, Delaware demonstrates that states can achieve comprehensive,
uniform coverage through a single legislative act — a model for states
where municipal authority is constrained. Governor Meyer's personal
history (authoring the 2017 NCC sanctuary executive order before
becoming governor) creates an unusually direct pipeline from municipal
innovation to statewide policy.

Together, these ﬁve states present an interlocking demonstration that
the election protection ordinance framework functions across every
governance structure: strong charter counties (Maryland), home rule
cities (Massachusetts, Rhode Island), town meetings (Vermont), executive
orders (Delaware/New Castle County), and statewide legislation (all
ﬁve). This diversity is the batch's singular contribution to the
national campaign — proving that \*\*18 U.S.C. § 592 local enforcement
works everywhere\*\*, regardless of state legal architecture.
