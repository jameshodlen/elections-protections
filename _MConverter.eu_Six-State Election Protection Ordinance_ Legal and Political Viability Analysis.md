\# Six-state election protection ordinance: legal and political viability analysis

\*\*The proposed municipal ordinance enforcing 18 U.S.C. § 592 faces dramatical y different legal landscapes across the six target states, with Virginia offering the strongest near-term pathway through state legislation, Florida providing the best municipal-level legal framework, and Tennessee, Alabama, and South Carolina presenting near-prohibitive barriers.\*\* Only Virginia's new Democratic trifecta creates a realistic state legislative pathway; al other states require a purely municipal strategy in hostile or ambivalent state environments. \# II. FLORIDA

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Florida possesses the \*\*strongest municipal home rule framework\*\* of any target state. \*\*Article VIII, Section 2\(b\) of the Florida Constitution\*\* grants municipalities "governmental, corporate and proprietary powers to enable them to conduct municipal government, perform municipal functions and render municipal services, and may exercise power for municipal purposes except as otherwise provided by law." \[Palm Springs\]\(https://www.vpsfl.org/489/History-of-Home-Rule\) Florida abandoned Dil on's Rule with the 1968

Constitutional revision \[Palm Springs\]\(https://www.vpsfl.org/489/History-of-Home-Rule\) and the 1973 Municipal Home Rule Powers Act \(MHRPA\). 

\*\*Florida Statutes Chapter 166\*\* \(MHRPA\) guarantees broad municipal authority. \[Florida Senate\]

\(https://www.flsenate.gov/Session/Bil /2019/3/Analyses/h0003a.BPS.PDF\) Section 166.021\(3\) limits municipal power only for subjects expressly preempted by the Constitution or general law, and §166.021\(4\) mandates liberal construction "to secure for municipalities the broad exercise of home rule powers." \[Florida Senate\]\(https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2018/Chapter166/Al \) Charter counties receive paral el authority under Article VIII, §1\(g\): "al powers of local self-government not inconsistent with general law." \[My Florida Legal\]\(https://www.myfloridalegal.com/ag-opinions/home-rule-and-special-legislation\) \[vLex\]\(https://case-law.vlex.com/vid/sarasota-al -

for-fair-885385420\)

Critical case law strongly favors the ordinance. In \*\*Sarasota Al iance for Fair Elections v. Browning, 28 So.3d 880 \(Fla. 2010\)\*\*, the Florida Supreme Court held that \*\*the Florida Election Code does not preempt the field of elections law\*\* \[Findlaw\]

\(https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/fl-supreme-court/1507783.html\) — the single most important precedent for this campaign. In \*\*City of Hol ywood v. Mul igan, 934 So.2d 1238 \(Fla. 2006\)\*\*, the Court established that ambiguity on preemption must be resolved in favor of local government. \[The Florida Bar\]\(https://www.floridabar.org/the-florida-bar-journal/the-effectiveness-of-home-rule-a-preemption-and-conflict-analysis/\) \*\*State v. City of Sunrise, 354 So.2d 1206 \(Fla. 1978\)\*\* confirmed that municipalities are no longer dependent on the legislature for authority; statutes only determine \*limitations\*. \[Fl-counties\]\(https://faca.fl-counties.com/sites/default/files/2021-09/Preemption.Whitepaper.61421%20FINAL.pdf\)

Municipalities can partial y act on election-adjacent matters. While §97.0115 preempts "al matters set forth in chapters 97–105," \[Florida DOS\]\(https://files.floridados.gov/media/706922/binder1-florida-election-code-2023.pdf\) the proposed ordinance regulates \*local government resource al ocation and police conduct near pol ing places\* — not election administration itself — potential y placing it outside the preempted field. 

\#\# 2. Preemption landscape

\(https://www.flsenate.gov/Laws/Statutes/2018/Chapter97/Al \) This covers election administration but does not expressly address local resource deployment, cooperation with federal law enforcement, or enforcement of federal criminal statutes like 18 U.S.C. § 592. 

\*\*SB 168 \(2019\)\*\*, codified at §§908.101–.109, is Florida's anti-sanctuary law requiring local law enforcement to use "best efforts" to support federal immigration enforcement. \[State Regs Today\]\(https://www.stateregstoday.com/politics/state-immigration-enforcement-and-cooperation-with-sanctuary-cities-in-florida\) \[Florida Governor's Office\]\(https://www.flgov.com/eog/news/press/2019/governor-ron-desantis-signs-sb-168-federal-immigration-enforcement\) This establishes that the legislature is wil ing to \*\*mandate\*\* local cooperation with federal enforcement — a direct analogue that opponents wil invoke. DeSantis\* produced mixed results; the 11th Circuit reversed on standing grounds in 2023. \[CBS News\]\(https://www.cbsnews.com/miami/news/court-rejects-chal enge-to-sanctuary-cities-law/\)

\*\*The Election Code does NOT comprehensively occupy the field\*\* per the Florida Supreme Court in \*Sarasota Al iance\*. \[Findlaw\]

\(https://caselaw.findlaw.com/court/fl-supreme-court/1507783.html\) The key argument is that the ordinance addresses local resource al ocation and law enforcement conduct — not the election administration matters covered in chapters 97–105. 

\*\*Weaponizable statutes\*\*: §97.0115 \(express election preemption\), the §790.33 model for personal liability, \[Fl-counties\]\(https://faca.fl-counties.com/sites/default/files/2021-09/Preemption.Whitepaper.61421%20FINAL.pdf\) the §908 anti-sanctuary framework, §166.044/

\#\# 3. Constitutional basis

The anti-commandeering doctrine \(\*Printz v. United States\*, 521 U.S. 898; \[Tenth Amendment Center\]

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Virginia is one of the \*\*most restrictive Dil on's Rule states in the nation\*\*. The Virginia Supreme Court in \*City of Winchester v. 

Redmond\* \(1896\) adopted the principle that localities possess only expressly granted powers, necessarily implied powers, and essential/indispensable powers, \[Weebly\]\(https://578125292684560794.weebly.com/dil ons-rule.html\) with "any fair, reasonable, substantial doubt concerning the existence of power...resolved by the courts \*\*against\*\* the corporation" \[Cvil epedia\]

\(https://www.cvil epedia.org/Dil on\_Rule\) \[Public Health Law Center\]

\(https://www.publichealthlawcenter.org/sites/default/files/resources/Dil ons-Rule-Home-Rule-Preemption.pdf\) \(\*County Board of Arlington v. Brown\*, 329 S.E.2d 468, 470 \(Va. 1985\)\). 

The 1971 Constitutional Convention considered and \*\*rejected\*\* a home rule amendment. \[VPM\]\(https://www.vpm.org/news/2025-03-05/curious-commonwealth-john-dil on-rule-virginia-locality-law-why\) \*\*Va. Code § 15.2-1102\*\* grants cities broad language including "al powers...pertinent to the conduct of the affairs and functions of the municipal government," \[Justia\]

\(https://law.justia.com/codes/virginia/title-15-2/chapter-11/section-15-2-1102/\) but Virginia courts consistently apply Dil on's Rule to narrow this. The Virginia Supreme Court in \*Alford v. City of Newport News\*, 260 S.E.2d 241 \(Va. 1979\) validated municipal police power for public health ordinances, and \*City of Virginia Beach v. Murphy\*, 239 Va. 353 \(1990\) upheld police power for public nuisances. 

\*\*There is no express grant of authority\*\* in the Code of Virginia for localities to regulate federal personnel near pol ing places, restrict city resources from cooperating with federal law enforcement on election matters, or enforce federal election law. However, Virginia's 38

independent cities operate under individual charters with "uniform charter powers" under Va. Code § 15.2-1100 et seq. \[Virginia Code\]

\(https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacodeful /title15.2/chapter11/article1/\) and have somewhat broader practical authority because they function as both city and county government. 

\#\# 2. Preemption landscape

Title 24.2 comprehensively regulates elections. \*\*§24.2-604\*\* extensively regulates pol ing place activities \[Virginia Code\]

\(https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title24.2/chapter6/section24.2-604/\) and \*\*explicitly exempts law enforcement officers\*\* from the prohibition on carrying weapons within the 40-foot zone \[Virginia Code\]

\(https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacode/title24.2/chapter6/section24.2-604/\) — the General Assembly has specifical y addressed armed persons near pol ing places. \*\*§24.2-606\*\* provides that election officers, with law enforcement consent, may designate officers to attend pol ing places. \[Virginia Code\]\(https://law.lis.virginia.gov/vacodeful /title24.2/chapter6/\) This comprehensive scheme creates a strong \*\*implied preemption\*\* argument. 

Virginia does \*\*not\*\* currently have an anti-sanctuary preemption statute. Governor Youngkin signed Executive Order 47 \(Feb. 2025\) directing state cooperation with ICE, but EOs don't bind localities directly. Past anti-sanctuary bil s were vetoed by Governor Northam.

\*states\* from \*federal\* overreach, not necessarily localities from state/federal action. However, since Virginia has not \*required\*

\# VII. LOUISIANA

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Louisiana's home rule framework is \*\*stronger than the other hostile states\*\*. \*\*La. Const. Art. VI, § 5\*\* authorizes local governmental subdivisions to draft and adopt home rule charters. Home rule operates as the \*\*reverse of Dil on's Rule\*\* — home rule governments can exercise al powers not explicitly denied. Currently \*\*26 of 64 parishes\*\* and approximately 30 municipalities operate under home rule charters. 

\*\*La. Const. Art. VI, § 6\*\* prohibits the legislature from enacting laws that change the "structure and organization or the particular distribution and redistribution of the powers and functions" of home rule charter entities. In \*\*City of New Orleans v. State, 426 So.2d 1318 \(La. 1983\)\*\*, the Louisiana Supreme Court held that the 1974 Constitution "sought to expand rather than retract local autonomy." 

Louisiana's \*\*civil law system\*\* \(French/Spanish traditions\) has specific implications: legislation is primary \(Civil Code Article 2\), 

\*\*jurisprudence constante\*\* \(not stare decisis\) means a series of consistent decisions — not a single ruling — form binding authority, and the prohibition on ordinances "governing private or civil relationships" \(Art. VI, §9\(A\)\(2\)\) is broadly defined. Custom is a secondary source only where legislation is silent \(Civil Code Article 3\). 

Key home rule charter municipalities: \*\*New Orleans\*\* \(home rule since 1954, pre-1974 Constitution\), \*\*East Baton Rouge Parish\*\*

\(consolidated government, first home rule charter in Louisiana since 1946\), \*\*Shreveport\*\*, \*\*Lafayette Parish\*\* \(consolidated government\), and \*\*Lake Charles\*\*. 

\#\# 2. Preemption landscape — express statutory preemption is the critical obstacle

\*\*La. R.S. 18:1\(B\)\*\* is the single most formidable statutory barrier:

> "The Louisiana Election Code shal regulate the conduct of elections and \*\*political subdivisions shal be prohibited from adopting any law, resolution, or ordinance relative to elections and the conduct thereof\*\*, including campaign finance, \*\*except as otherwise specifical y authorized in this code.\*\*" 

This comprehensive express preemption, enacted in 1989 \(Acts 1989, No. 56, §1\), voided al existing local election laws and prohibits even \*\*resolutions\*\* — broader than most states' preemption. The Louisiana Election Code \(Title 18\) comprehensively covers pol ing places, voter intimidation \(R.S. 18:1461\), election offenses \(R.S. 18:1461.4\), and a 600-foot campaign-free zone \(R.S. 18:1462\). 

Louisiana's \*\*anti-sanctuary laws\*\* are among the most aggressive in the nation:

- \*\*Act 314 of 2024 \(SB 208\)\*\*, codified at La. R.S. 33:81–85, prohibits sanctuary policies and requires compliance with ICE detainers. 

- \*\*SB 15 \(effective August 1, 2025\)\*\* criminalizes refusal to cooperate with federal immigration enforcement with penalties of \*\*up to 10

years in prison\*\* — the first state law in the US to criminalize interference with immigration enforcement. 

- \*\*Operation GEAUX \(May 2025\)\*\*: Formal Louisiana-ICE partnership. 

AG Liz Murril has already aggressively targeted New Orleans' sanctuary policies, filing motions to eliminate OPSO sanctuary practices, demanding NOPD cooperate with ICE, and threatening felony prosecution of non-compliant officials. 

\#\# 3. Constitutional basis

The ordinance's strongest framing is as a \*\*resource al ocation directive\*\*, not an election regulation — directing how local police resources are deployed, similar to policies about when officers assist other agencies. In Louisiana's civil law system, legislation is strictly construed. If the ordinance is careful y framed as a police resource al ocation measure, there is an argument it fal s outside the scope of R.S. 18:1\(B\)'s prohibition on regulations "relative to elections." However, Louisiana courts interpreting civil law tend to look at \*\*purpose and effect\*\*, not just form. 

Home rule charter municipalities have a \*\*marginal y stronger basis\*\* under Art. VI, §6's protection from legislative interference with structure and powers, but this protection relates to organization, not substantive policy areas preempted by state law.

\# VIII. Cross-state strategic recommendations

\#\# The federalism paradox at the campaign's center

The most powerful rhetorical and legal asset across al six states is that this ordinance \*\*enforces existing federal law\*\*. Unlike sanctuary city policies that resist federal enforcement, the election protection ordinance asks local governments to align with 18 U.S.C. §

592 — a criminal statute enacted to prevent voter intimidation by armed federal personnel. This framing inoculates against "anti-law enforcement" attacks and distinguishes the campaign from sanctuary precedents. Every state-specific drafting effort should lead with this distinction.

\*\*Phase 1 \(Months 1–6\)\*\*: Virginia state legislative push and Florida municipal ordinances in St. Petersburg and Gainesvil e. Virginia's Democratic trifecta window is \*\*time-limited and urgent\*\* — pursue a statewide bil in the 2026 or 2027 General Assembly session.

\#\# The dual-track vehicle strategy

Across al states, the campaign should pursue \*\*two paral el vehicles\*\*:

Frame as resource al ocation directives, include robust severability clauses, define precise pol ing place buffer zones, include emergency safe harbors, and avoid firearms-specific language.

These operate below the legislative radar, are harder to preempt, and can be implemented immediately. Madison's own approach included police department SOP changes alongside the ordinance.
