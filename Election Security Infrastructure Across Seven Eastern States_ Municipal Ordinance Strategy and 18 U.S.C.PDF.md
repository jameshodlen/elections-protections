\# Election security infrastructure across seven eastern states

\*\*West Virginia stands out as the highest-priority target for
municipal ordinance strategy\*\*, with no polling place ﬁrearms
prohibition, constitutional carry, strong state preemption blocking
local action, and no AG election protection infrastructure. Virginia,
now under a Democratic trifecta since January 2026, presents an
immediate legislative opportunity. The remaining ﬁve states —
Massachusetts, Maryland, Delaware, Rhode Island, and Vermont — form a
strong coalition bloc, all having joined the 19-state lawsuit against EO
14248 and all possessing paper ballot systems, though Rhode Island
notably lacks a polling place ﬁrearms ban despite otherwise robust
protections. This research covers election administration,
cybersecurity, physical security, legal strategies, and key contacts for
all seven states, with cross-state comparative analysis and strategic
implications for municipal ordinance enforcement of 18 U.S.C. § 592.

\## State-by-state election authority and voting infrastructure

All seven states use \*\*paper ballots with optical scan tabulation\*\*,
providing strong audit trails. Massachusetts and Vermont additionally
mandate post-election audits by law, and Rhode Island pioneered
risk-limiting audits in 2017. \[Veriﬁed Voting\]
(https://veriﬁedvoting.org/auditlaw/rhode-island/)

\*\*Virginia\*\* elections are administered by the Department of
Elections (ELECT), currently led by Commissioner Susan Beals under the
oversight of a ﬁve-member State Board of Elections.
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virginia_Department_of_Elections)
The November 2025 elections produced a historic Democratic trifecta:
Governor Abigail Spanberger, AG Jay Jones (ﬁrst Black AG in state
history), and expanded legislative majorities.
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Virginia_gubernatorial_election)
Virginia decertiﬁed all DRE machines in 2017, making it an early leader
in paper ballot adoption. \[GovInfo\]
(https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CHRG-115hhrg30295/html/CHRG-115hhrg30295.htm)
Key statutes are in Title 24.2 of the Virginia Code. Virginia is a
\*\*strict Dillon Rule state\*\*,
\[Localmajority\](https://www.localmajority.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/VA.DillonRule.20190515.DR\_.pdf)
severely limiting municipal authority to act independently on election
security. \[University of Virginia School of
Law\](https://www.law.virginia.edu/news/202003/it-time-home-rule-virginia)
However, the 2026 legislative session includes \*\*HB 909\*\*, which
would expand the polling place ﬁrearms buffer from 40 to 100 feet.
\[NRA-ILA\]
(https://www.nraila.org/articles/20260115/virginia-more-gun-control-introduced-in-general-assembly)

\*\*Rhode Island\*\* operates under a unique dual-authority system:
Secretary of State Gregg Amore (D) serves as chief elections oﬃcial
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/Rhode_Island_Secretary_of_State)
under HAVA, while the independent Board of Elections (Executive Director
Miguel Nunez) regulates the elections process.
\[Ri\](https://elections.ri.gov/) The state uses ES&S DS200 optical
scanners across \*\*590 units statewide\*\* \[PR
Newswire\](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/rhode-island-updates-their-voting-technology-with-visionary-voting-products-from-ess-300302395.html)
and was a national pioneer in risk-limiting audits. Rhode Island's 36 of
39 municipalities
\[Ri\](https://municipalﬁnance.ri.gov/sites/g/ﬁles/xkgbur546/ﬁles/documents/resources/Home_Rule_Charter_Publication.pdf)
have adopted Home Rule Charters
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Home_rule_in_the_United_States)
under Article XIII of the RI Constitution, but election administration
remains governed by state law (Title 17).

\*\*Massachusetts\*\* elections are overseen by Secretary of the
Commonwealth William Francis Galvin (D), who has held the position since
1995.
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/Massachusetts_Secretary_of_State)
The landmark \*\*VOTES Act (2022)\*\* permanently established no-excuse
mail voting, expanded early voting, \[Common
Cause\](https://www.commoncause.org/massachusetts/work/the-votes-act/)

authority under \*\*Article LXXXIX\*\* of the state constitution,
\[Handgunlaw\](https://www.handgunlaw.us/states/massachusetts.pdf)
meaning municipalities can extend the 150-foot buffer zone and adopt
additional protective ordinances consistent with state law — making it
the most favorable environment for local election security action among
these seven states.

\[MDVRA\](https://www.mdvra.org/)

\[Delaware\](https://elections.delaware.gov/aboutagency.shtml) Delaware

\## Voter intimidation laws and enforcement tools

\*\*Maryland\*\* provides the most comprehensive voter intimidation
framework: EL § 16-201 imposes penalties up to \*\*\$5,000 and 5 years
imprisonment\*\* for intimidation, \[maryland\]
(https://elections.maryland.gov/press_room/documents/2024%20Voter%20Intimidation%20Guidance%20Memo.pdf)
and critically, EL § 16-201(c) references \*\*Courts Article §
5-106(b)\*\*,
\[Findlaw\](https://codes.ﬁndlaw.com/md/election-law/md-code-elec-law-sect-16-201.html)
creating an explicit \*\*private right of action\*\* for voters. The AG
can also seek injunctive relief under EL § 16-1004(a)(1). \[maryland\]
(https://elections.maryland.gov/press_room/documents/2024%20Voter%20Intimidation%20Guidance%20Memo.pdf)

\*\*Virginia\*\* similarly provides a \*\*statutory private right of
action\*\* under Va. Code § 24.2-1005(B), allowing any intimidated voter
to "obtain relief." \[Brennan Center for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/virginia-election-interference-law-handbook)
Conspiracy to hinder voting rights is a \*\*Class 5 felony\*\* under §
24.2-1015 \[Brennan Center for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/virginia-election-interference-law-handbook)
— one of the strongest penalties in this group. Virginia also requires
police oﬃcers at polling places to obey election judges' orders and has
robust paramilitary activity prohibitions under §§ 18.2-433.1-433.2.
\[Brennan Center for
Justice\](https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/virginia-protections-against-intimidation-voters-and-election-workers)

\*\*Massachusetts\*\* mandates a \*\*constable or police oﬃcer at every
polling place\*\* (M.G.L. ch. 54, §§ 65, 71) — the only state in this
group with such a requirement. Voter intimidation under M.G.L. ch. 56,
§§ 29-31 carries up to one year imprisonment. \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/massachusetts/2010/parti/titleviii/chapter56/section29/)
AG Campbell has issued speciﬁc advisories citing 18 U.S.C. § 592 and
maintains an active election protection program.
\[Mass.gov\](https://www.mass.gov/doc/ago-advisory-on-poll-monitoring-and-voter-intimidation-english/download)

\*\*Delaware\*\* stands out for its dedicated \*\*Chapter 53 (Military
or Other Interference with Elections)\*\*: § 5302 makes military
interference a \*\*felony\*\* punishable by \$1,000-\$10,000 and 1-5
years imprisonment, \[Delaware\]
(https://delcode.delaware.gov/title15/c051/sc02/index.html) with
permanent disenfranchisement. \[Delaware\]
(https://delcode.delaware.gov/title15/c053/index.html) Section 5303
provides a \*\*civil remedy for interference with voting\*\* — a
statutory basis for private enforcement.

\*\*Rhode Island\*\* treats voter intimidation as a \*\*felony\*\* under
§ 17-23-5 (with permanent loss of voting rights upon conviction),
representing strong criminal deterrence. However, no state-level private
civil right of action was identiﬁed.

\*\*Vermont\*\* has notably \*\*weak voter intimidation penalties\*\*: §
2017 imposes only a \$200 ﬁne for "undue inﬂuence" \[Vermont
Legislature\]
(https://legislature.vermont.gov/statutes/section/17/035/02017)
\[Vermont Legislature\]
(https://legislature.vermont.gov/statutes/section/17/035/02017) — the
lowest penalty in this group. The polling place ﬁrearms ban (13 V.S.A. §
4027) adds a meaningful enforcement tool with up to one year
imprisonment. \[Vermont Legislature\]
(https://legislature.vermont.gov/statutes/section/13/085/04027)

\*\*West Virginia\*\* classiﬁes voter intimidation as a \*\*misdemeanor
only\*\* (§§ 3-9-9, 3-9-10, max \$1,000/1 year), \[West Virginia
Legislature\] (https://code.wvlegislature.gov/3-9-10/) \[West Virginia
Legislature\](http://www.wvlegislature.gov/WVCODE/code.cfm?
chap=03&art=9&section=9) with no private right of action, no AG election
protection infrastructure, and no anti-paramilitary training statute.
Georgetown ICAP conﬁrms WV has an anti-militia law but \*\*not\*\* an
anti-paramilitary one \[Max Velocity Tactical\]
(https://maxvelocitytactical.com/virginia-anti-paramilitary-law-perspective-rationality/)

\## Strategic implications for municipal ordinance enforcement of 18
U.S.C. § 592

\### The federal statute and its enforcement gap

18 U.S.C. § 592 prohibits any person in the federal civil, military, or
naval service from ordering, bringing, or keeping troops or armed men at
any election polling place, with a narrow exception for repelling armed
enemies. \[U.S. House of Representatives +2\]
(https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section592&num=0&edition=prelim)
Penalties include \*\*up to ﬁve years imprisonment and permanent
disqualiﬁcation from federal oﬃce\*\*. \[U.S. House of Representatives\]
(https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section592&num=0&edition=prelim)
Despite its severity, the statute has seen virtually no modern
prosecutions and functions primarily as a deterrent. Only the U.S. DOJ
can prosecute violations — municipalities cannot independently enforce §
592 through criminal prosecution.

However, municipalities \*\*can\*\* adopt complementary ordinances
addressing gaps § 592 does not cover: private armed groups and militia
activity near polls, state/local law enforcement conduct at polling
places, weapons buffer zones, and election security coordination
protocols. The Supreme Court's \*Bruen\* decision (2022) explicitly
aﬃrmed that ﬁrearms restrictions at "historically sensitive places"
including polling places are constitutionally permissible, \[Rhode
Island Attorney General's Oﬃce\](https://riag.ri.gov/bruen_guidance)
\[PBS\]
(https://www.pbs.org/newshour/politics/more-states-move-to-restrict-guns-at-polling-sites-to-protect-workers-voters-from-threats)
providing strong legal footing for such ordinances. \[States United
Democracy
Center\](https://statesunited.org/resources/police-need-to-know-polls/)

\### Three-tier priority framework

\*\*Tier 1 — Critical Priority (Weakest Protections):\*\*

\*\*West Virginia\*\* demands immediate strategic attention. It combines
constitutional carry, no polling place ﬁrearms ban, strong state ﬁrearms
preemption blocking local action,
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-west-virginia/)
misdemeanor-only voter intimidation penalties, no anti-paramilitary
training statute, no AG election protection program, an unimplemented
cybersecurity framework, and a Second Amendment Preservation Act that
could complicate federal enforcement. The Dillon Rule further constrains
municipal action. \[University of Virginia School of
Law\](https://www.law.virginia.edu/news/202003/it-time-home-rule-virginia)
Strategy here must focus on \*\*state-level legislative advocacy\*\*
(since municipal ordinances face preemption) or \*\*federal enforcement
channels\*\*, though the current DOJ posture makes federal action
unlikely. Coalition building with the SOS oﬃce (which has shown some
independence from the governor on transparency issues) may offer an
entry point. \[West Virginia Secretary of State\]
(https://sos.wv.gov/news/Pages/04-03-25-A.aspx) Polling places in
schools and courthouses have incidental protections \[GIFFORDS\]

(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/location-restrictions-in-west-virginia/)
that could be highlighted to demonstrate the feasibility and
constitutionality of broader protections.

\*\*Tier 2 — Moderate Priority (Speciﬁc Gaps to Address):\*\*

\*\*Rhode Island\*\* needs a polling place ﬁrearms prohibition to close
its most signiﬁcant gap. Despite requiring permits for all carry, the
absence of a speciﬁc ban means legally armed individuals may enter
polling places. The state's strong home rule provisions, active AG, and
legislative interest (H7434 proposing 200-foot ICE buffer)
\[LegiScan\](https://legiscan.com/RI/bill/H7434/2026?
guid=6yevQ7MuLhqHKEv5frQEHh) suggest a receptive environment for either
state legislation or municipal ordinances. The dual-authority election
structure (SOS + Board of Elections) means engagement with both entities
is necessary.

\*\*Virginia\*\* presents a unique transitional opportunity. The January
2026 Democratic trifecta creates an immediate window for state-level
action.
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2025_Virginia_gubernatorial_election)
HB 909 (expanding the ﬁrearms buffer from 40 to 100 feet) is already in
the legislative pipeline.
\[NRA-ILA\](https://www.nraila.org/articles/20260115/virginia-more-gun-control-introduced-in-general-assembly)
However, the Dillon Rule means municipal ordinances require enabling
legislation. \[University of Virginia School of Law\]
(https://www.law.virginia.edu/news/202003/it-time-home-rule-virginia)
\[Localmajority\](https://www.localmajority.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/05/VA.DillonRule.20190515.DR\_.pdf)
Priority actions should include advocating for AG Jones to join the EO
14248 litigation, supporting HB 909's passage, and seeking Dillon Rule
carve-outs for municipal election security ordinances. Virginia's
paramilitary activity prohibitions (§§ 18.2-433.1-433.2) are already
strong \[Virginia Oﬃce of the Attorney General\]
(https://www.oag.state.va.us/ﬁles/Opinions/2020/Simon-20-046-issued.pdf)
and should be leveraged.

\*\*Vermont\*\* has resolved its core ﬁrearms gap through 13 V.S.A. §
4027 but retains weaknesses: a \$200 maximum ﬁne for voter intimidation,
no speciﬁc anti-paramilitary statute, no distance-based buffer zone, and
Dillon Rule constraints on local action plus statewide ﬁrearms
preemption (24 V.S.A. § 2295).
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gun_laws_in_Vermont)
Legislative advocacy to strengthen voter intimidation penalties and
establish a measured buffer zone would complement existing protections.

\*\*Tier 3 — Supportive Environments (Strong Existing Protections):\*\*

\*\*Massachusetts\*\* is the model state in this group: 150-foot total
ﬁrearms ban at polling places, \[Mass.gov
+3\](https://www.mass.gov/info-details/mass-general-laws-c269-ss-10)
police required at every polling place, strong home rule enabling
municipal action, venue state for EO 14248 injunction, very active AG,
and robust cybersecurity infrastructure. Municipal ordinances here can
serve as \*\*national models\*\* for other jurisdictions. The AG's
speciﬁc advisory citing 18 U.S.C. § 592 demonstrates existing
institutional awareness. \[Mass.gov\]
(https://www.mass.gov/doc/ago-advisory-on-poll-monitoring-and-voter-intimidation-english/download)

\*\*Maryland\*\* offers similarly strong protections with its 2023 Gun
Safety Act polling place ﬁrearms ban (upheld on appeal January 2026),
explicit private right of action, AG Federal Litigation Unit with
dedicated \[The
Banner\](https://www.thebanner.com/politics-power/state-government/maryland-attorney-general-anthony-brown-federal-lawsuits-AXS56S4QXFH3LEBNI6DAK5SL6Y/)
\$1.5M funding, and proximity to federal cyber assets. Charter counties
with home rule authority are well-positioned for model ordinance
adoption.

\*\*Delaware\*\* has the nation's strongest
military-election-interference statute (Chapter 53, felony penalties)
plus a 2023 polling place ﬁrearms ban
\[Safercarry\](https://safercarry.com/delaware-concealed-carry/) and a
statutory civil remedy for voting interference. These provisions
directly parallel and reinforce § 592's goals. Delaware should be
engaged as a model for statutory language.

\### Coalition opportunities and recommended actions

The ﬁve states that joined the EO 14248 lawsuit (MA, MD, DE, RI, VT)
form a natural coalition. \[CA
+4\](https://oag.ca.gov/news/press-releases/attorney-general-bonta-ﬁles-motion-preliminary-injunction-halt-implementation)
Virginia's new Democratic leadership creates a potential sixth partner.
Cross-state coordination through the AG coalition infrastructure already
built for the litigation provides an eﬃcient channel for election
security advocacy.

Priority actions for "Protections for Elections" should include:
drafting model municipal ordinances for Massachusetts and Maryland
(where home rule is strongest), advocating for Rhode Island to enact a
polling place ﬁrearms ban (a straightforward legislative lift in a blue
state), supporting Virginia's HB 909 and seeking broader enabling
legislation, building relationships with West Virginia's SOS oﬃce as an
independent voice, and documenting the CISA withdrawal's impact on small
states (Vermont and Rhode Island) to support federal funding advocacy.
The January 2026 West Virginia cybersecurity audit failure and Vermont's
"on our own" assessment \[vtdigger\]
(https://vtdigger.org/2026/02/12/on-our-own-vermont-is-getting-less-help-from-the-feds-to-keep-elections-secure/)
\[VTDigger\]
(https://vtdigger.org/2026/02/12/on-our-own-vermont-is-getting-less-help-from-the-feds-to-keep-elections-secure/)
provide powerful evidence for the argument that local and state action
is necessary when federal support evaporates.

\## Conclusion

The seven-state landscape reveals a clear \*\*protection gradient\*\*
running from Massachusetts and Maryland (comprehensive, layered
protections) through the mid-tier states (Virginia, Vermont, Delaware
with strong but incomplete frameworks) to the critical gap states (Rhode
Island's missing ﬁrearms ban and West Virginia's systemic
vulnerabilities). The CISA withdrawal has fundamentally altered the

election security calculus, forcing states to rely on their own
resources \[Santa Monica Daily
Press\](https://www.smdp.com/big-changes-to-the-agency-charged-with-securing-elections-lead-to-midterm-worries/)
\[Votebeat\](https://www.votebeat.org/2025/03/11/cisa-ends-support-election-security-nass-nased/)
precisely when threats are escalating \[Santa Monica Daily Press
+8\](https://smdp.com/government-politics-2/big-changes-to-the-agency-charged-with-securing-elections-lead-to-midterm-worries/)
— a dynamic that makes municipal-level action both more necessary and
more diﬃcult. West Virginia's combination of constitutional carry, no
polling place ﬁrearms ban, state preemption, and an unimplemented
cybersecurity framework represents the most acute vulnerability, but its
deeply conservative political environment makes legislative progress
unlikely without sustained external pressure or federal intervention.
The more productive near-term strategy focuses on strengthening
already-receptive states (Rhode Island's ﬁrearms gap, Virginia's
legislative window, Vermont's weak penalties) while building the
Massachusetts-Maryland model ordinance infrastructure that can be
deployed nationally. Delaware's Chapter 53
military-election-interference framework \[Delaware General
Assembly\](https://legis.delaware.gov/json/BillDetail/GenerateHtmlDocument?
legislationId=140510&legislationTypeId=1&docTypeId=2&legislationName=HB202)
deserves particular attention as a state-level complement to § 592 that
other states could replicate.
