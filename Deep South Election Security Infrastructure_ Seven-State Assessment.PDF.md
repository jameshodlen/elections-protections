\# Deep South election security infrastructure: seven-state assessment

\*\*The municipal ordinance strategy faces severe structural obstacles
across all seven Deep South states.\*\* Every state in this batch —
Florida, Georgia, Louisiana, South Carolina, Tennessee, Alabama, and
Mississippi — has enacted comprehensive ﬁrearms preemption laws that
strip municipalities of authority to regulate ﬁrearms, and all seven
have adopted constitutional/permitless carry since 2016. Only three
states (Georgia, South Carolina, and Louisiana) maintain explicit
statutory prohibitions on ﬁrearms at polling places; Alabama and
Tennessee have \*\*no such prohibition at all\*\*, representing the most
critical gaps. The political environment — all seven states have
Republican governors, attorneys general, and legislative supermajorities
— makes state-level reform unlikely and local ordinance campaigns
high-risk. This report provides the granular legal, cybersecurity, and
strategic analysis needed to identify viable pathways and prioritize
resources.

\## Part I: State-by-state election security proﬁles

\### Florida — Strong infrastructure, impenetrable preemption wall

\*\*Election Authority\*\*: Secretary of State \*\*Cord Byrd\*\*
(appointed by Governor DeSantis) oversees the Division of Elections.
Florida's 67 county Supervisors of Elections administer local elections.
\[Florida Department of
State\](https://dos.ﬂ.gov/historical/meetings-and-events/news-and-press-releases/view-release/?id=69472)
The \*\*Oﬃce of Election Crimes and Security (OECS)\*\*, created by SB
524 (2022), investigates alleged election violations. \[Florida
Department of
State\](https://dos.ﬂ.gov/communications/press-releases/2025/secretary-of-state-cord-byrd-announces-landmark-agreement-with-dhs-to-better-secure-elections/)
Key statutes are in \*\*Title IX, Chapters 97–106, Florida Statutes\*\*.

\*\*Voting Systems\*\*: Florida uses \*\*hand-marked paper ballots with
optical scan tabulators\*\* statewide (ES&S and Dominion systems).
Post-election audits are required under F.S. § 101.591, and systems are
air-gapped with a "trusted build" process. \[Florida Department of
State\](https://dos.ﬂ.gov/elections/voting-systems/about-voting-systems/)
Florida's voting infrastructure is among the most secure in the nation.

\*\*Firearms at Polling Places\*\*: Concealed carry is
\*\*prohibited\*\* at polling places under \*\*F.S. §
790.06(12)(a)(6)\*\* \[Everytown Law\]
(https://everytownlaw.org/report/election-protection/) (second-degree
misdemeanor). The 2023 constitutional carry law (HB 543) \[Florida
Governor's
Oﬃce\](https://www.ﬂgov.com/eog/news/press/2023/governor-ron-desantis-signs-hb-543-constitutional-carry)
did \*\*not\*\* remove polling places from the prohibited locations
list.
\[Valcarcellaw\](https://www.valcarcellaw.com/blog/what-gun-owners-should-know-about-ﬂoridas-new-permitless-carry-law/)
Open carry remains generally illegal under F.S. § 790.053. \[Musca Law\]
(https://www.muscalaw.com/blog/what-changed-ﬂoridas-open-carry-laws-2025)
\[Firearmlawsbystate\] (https://ﬁrearmlawsbystate.com/states/ﬂ.cfm)
However, there is no blanket ban on \*all\* ﬁrearms at polling places —
the prohibition is keyed to the concealed carry framework.

\*\*Preemption\*\*: \*\*F.S. § 790.33\*\* is one of the most aggressive
ﬁrearms preemption statutes in the country. The legislature has declared
it "occupies the whole ﬁeld" of ﬁrearms regulation. Local oﬃcials who
enact ﬁrearms ordinances face \*\*personal civil ﬁnes of up to
\$5,000\*\*.
\[Fl-counties\](https://faca.ﬂ-counties.com/sites/default/ﬁles/2021-09/Preemption.Whitepaper.61421%20FINAL.pdf)
Municipal ordinances enforcing 18 U.S.C. § 592 through ﬁrearms
restrictions at polling places would be immediately voided.

\*\*Home Rule\*\*: Florida's Constitution (Article VIII) and the
Municipal Home Rule Powers Act (Ch. 166) grant broad home rule, \[Palm
Springs\](https://www.vpsﬂ.org/489/History-of-Home-Rule) but this power
cannot be exercised in preempted ﬁelds. The ﬁrearms preemption is total
and eliminates any local pathway for polling place ﬁrearms ordinances.

\*\*Cybersecurity\*\*: Florida maintains robust infrastructure through
the Florida Digital Service and \*\*Cyber Florida\*\* (University of
South Florida), which runs the FirstLine program providing free
cybersecurity training. \[Cyber Florida at
USF\](https://cyberﬂorida.org/on-the-road-with-ﬁrstline-summer-2025/)
HAVA election security grants \[Bipartisan Policy
Center\](https://bipartisanpolicy.org/report/impact-

recent-grants-help-america-vote-act/) total approximately \*\*\$47.7
million\*\* (FY2018–FY2023). The loss of CISA/EI-ISAC support is
manageable given Florida's state-level capacity.

dos.ﬂ.gov/elections \| Voter Fraud Hotline: \*\*1-877-868-3737\*\*

\### Georgia — Strongest polling place ﬁrearms ban, but preemption
blocks local action

\*\*Election Authority\*\*: Secretary of State \*\*Brad
Raffensperger\*\* (R, running for Governor 2026) administers elections
through the Elections Division. The \*\*State Election Board\*\* has
regulatory and enforcement authority. Key statutes are in \*\*O.C.G.A.
Title 21, Chapter 2\*\* (Georgia Election Code).

\*\*Voting Systems\*\*: Georgia uses \*\*Dominion ImageCast X ballot
marking devices\*\* (BMDs) with paper printouts plus ImageCast Precinct
scanners across all \*\*159 counties\*\*. The system has been the
subject of signiﬁcant security litigation — Professor J. Alex Halderman
identiﬁed 9 vulnerabilities (unsealed in \*Curling v. Raffensperger\*,
June 2023), and the \*\*Coffee County breach\*\* (2021) constituted one
of the most serious election security incidents in U.S. history.
Risk-limiting audits are mandated under O.C.G.A. § 21-2-498.

\*\*Firearms at Polling Places\*\*: Georgia has the \*\*strongest
polling place ﬁrearms prohibition\*\* among these seven states.
\*\*O.C.G.A. § 21-2-413(i)\*\* prohibits ﬁrearms \*\*within 150 feet of
any active polling place\*\*, \[Everytown
Law\](https://everytownlaw.org/report/election-protection/) except for
security personnel (peace oﬃcers and certiﬁed security guards). This
survived the 2022 constitutional carry law (SB 319) and is reinforced by
\*\*O.C.G.A. § 16-11-127\*\*, which lists polling places among
prohibited carry locations.

\*\*Preemption\*\*: Despite this strong polling place ban, \*\*O.C.G.A.
§ 16-11-173\*\* broadly preempts local ﬁrearms regulation. The Georgia
Constitution (Art. IX, § II) explicitly \*\*excludes elections from the
home rule powers\*\* of counties and municipalities. \[New Georgia
Encyclopedia\](https://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/articles/government-politics/home-rule-and-ordinances/)
Local governments cannot impose additional ﬁrearms restrictions beyond
state law. A 2025 bill (SB 204) further strengthened ﬁrearms preemption.

\*\*SB 202 Litigation\*\*: Georgia's Election Integrity Act of 2021 has
survived every major legal challenge. The DOJ withdrew its lawsuit in
2025 under AG Bondi. The 11th Circuit aﬃrmed in \*Coalition for Good
Governance v. Raffensperger\* (January 2026). AG \*\*Chris Carr\*\* (R,
running for Governor 2026) has been the primary defender.

\*\*Cybersecurity\*\*: The \*\*Georgia Technology Authority (GTA)\*\*
leads state cybersecurity \[Georgia\](https://gta.georgia.gov/)
\[Georgia\]
(https://gta.georgia.gov/policies-and-programs/cybersecurity) under CISO
\*\*Steve Hodges\*\*. \[Georgia\]
(https://gta.georgia.gov/news/2023-01-09/steve-hodges-named-state-ciso)
The Georgia Cyber Center in Augusta is a major facility. HAVA funds
totaled approximately \*\*\$46.3 million\*\* (FY2018–2021).
\[Eac\](https://oig.eac.gov/reports/audit/audit-administration-help-america-vote-act-grants-awarded-state-georgia)
The loss of federal CISA support is signiﬁcant given Georgia's history
as an election security target.

\*\*Key Contacts\*\*: SoS Oﬃce: \*\*(404) 656-2871\*\* \| sos.ga.gov \|
AG Chris Carr: law.georgia.gov \| GTA cybersecurity: gta.georgia.gov.

\### Louisiana — Last state with paperless voting machines, approaching
critical transition

\*\*Election Authority\*\*: Secretary of State \*\*Nancy Landry\*\* (R,
elected November 2023) \[Ballotpedia\]
(https://ballotpedia.org/Louisiana_Secretary_of_State) oversees
elections. Governor \*\*Jeff Landry\*\* (R) took oﬃce January 2024. Key

statutes are in \*\*La. R.S. Title 18\*\* (Louisiana Election Code).

\*\*Voting Systems\*\*: Louisiana is the \*\*last state in the nation
still using paperless voting machines\*\* \[Veriﬁed Voting\]
(https://veriﬁedvoting.org/blog-executive-order-apr-2025/) for in-person
Election Day voting. The machines were purchased in 2005 and are no
longer manufactured.
\[Parlouisiana\](http://parlouisiana.org/wp-content/uploads/2022/09/PAR-Commentary-A-New-Voting-System.pdf)
SB 221 (2021) mandated new systems with auditable voter-veriﬁed paper
records, \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/louisiana/revised-statutes/title-18/rs-18-1362-1/)
and HB 924 (2022) required post-election audits — but neither can be
implemented until new equipment arrives. \[Veriﬁed
Voting\](https://veriﬁedvoting.org/auditlaw/louisiana/) Six vendors
demonstrated systems in August–September 2025, \[Louisiana
Illuminator\](https://lailluminator.com/2025/08/27/louisiana-vets-new-voting-systems-as-trump-doubles-down-on-false-fraud-claims/)
and a competitive procurement is underway targeting the 2026 midterms,
though the timeline remains uncertain.

\*\*Firearms at Polling Places\*\*: Concealed carry is
\*\*prohibited\*\* at polling places under \*\*R.S. 40:1379.3(N)(4)\*\*.
\[Bayou Shooter\]
(https://bayoushooter.com/threads/rs-40-1379-3n-5-meeting-place-of-the-governing-authority-of-a-political-subdivisio.151767/)
Louisiana adopted constitutional carry effective \*\*July 4, 2024\*\*
\[Vedder
Holsters\](https://www.vedderholsters.com/ccw-reciprocity-map/la-gun-laws/)
(SB 1), but the polling place prohibition survives. \[Alien Gear
Holsters\]
(https://aliengearholsters.com/blogs/news/louisiana-concealed-carry)
However, there is \*\*no explicit prohibition on open carry\*\* at
polling places separate from the concealed carry statute, creating a
potential gap.

\*\*Buffer Zone\*\*: Louisiana has the \*\*largest buffer zone\*\* among
these states at \*\*600 feet\*\* (R.S. 18:1462), with penalties of up to
\$500 ﬁne or 6 months imprisonment.

\*\*Preemption\*\*: \*\*R.S. 40:1796\*\* was signiﬁcantly strengthened
in 2024 to preempt all local ﬁrearms regulation. Existing local
ordinances are "null and void," \[Louisiana
Legislature\](https://legis.la.gov/legis/law.aspx?d=97860) and oﬃcials
face private lawsuits.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/louisiana/revised-statutes/title-40/rs-40-1796/)
Home rule authority exists for charter entities (New Orleans, Jefferson
Parish) under Article VI of the Constitution,
\[Bgr\](https://www.bgr.org/the-basics/) but ﬁrearms preemption
overrides this.

\*\*Cybersecurity\*\*: Louisiana has developed notable capacity through
\*\*Chief Cyber Oﬃcer Dustin Glover\*\*, \[Security Magazine\]
(https://www.securitymagazine.com/articles/98010-dustin-glover-named-louisiana-chief-cyber-oﬃcer)
the \*\*Louisiana Cyber Coordination Center (LC3)\*\* (\$1.5 million
facility),
\[StateScoop\](https://statescoop.com/louisiana-cyber-coordination-center-john-bel-edwards/)
and ESF-17 cyber emergency protocols. \[Louisiana Governor's
Oﬃce\](https://gov.louisiana.gov/page/cybersecurity-incident-resources-)
The state has assisted \*\*107 local agencies\*\* with cyber incidents
since 2019. \[Government Technology\]
(https://www.govtech.com/workforce/louisiana-ciso-changes-roles-to-focus-state-cyber-defenses)
HAVA funding: \*\*\$5.9M\*\* (FY2018), \[U.S. Election Assistance
Commission\](https://www.eac.gov/sites/default/ﬁles/havadocuments/LA_Narrative_Budget.pdf)
\*\*\$7.6M\*\* (FY2022 total). \[Issue
One\](https://issueone.org/articles/federal-funding-for-american-elections-hava-grants/)
The state is highly dependent on federal support, which makes CISA
cutbacks particularly concerning during the voting system transition.

\*\*Key Litigation\*\*: \*Louisiana v. Callais\* at SCOTUS (reargued
October 2025, decision pending) could fundamentally alter VRA Section
2's application to redistricting nationwide. \[NAACP Legal Defense
Fund\](https://www.naacpldf.org/case-issue/louisiana-v-callais/)

\*\*Key Contacts\*\*: SoS Elections Hotline: \*\*1-800-883-2805\*\*
\[Louisiana Secretary of State\]
(https://www.sos.la.gov/ElectionsAndVoting/GetElectionInformation/) \|
sos.la.gov \| AG Liz Murrill (R): \[Ballotpedia\]
(https://ballotpedia.org/Liz_Murrill) ag.state.la.us.

\### South Carolina — Best-positioned for existing protections, but
preemption still blocks local action

\*\*Election Authority\*\*: The \*\*South Carolina State Election
Commission (SEC)\*\* — not the Secretary of State — administers
elections. New Executive Director \*\*Jenny Wooten\*\* (appointed
December 2025, pending Senate conﬁrmation) replaced ﬁred director Howard

Knapp, who was arrested by SLED on charges of misconduct, embezzlement,
and wiretapping. \[South Carolina Public Radio\]
(https://www.southcarolinapublicradio.org/sc-news/2025-12-02/state-election-commission-interim-director-chosen-to-lead-sc-agency)
Key statutes are in \*\*SC Code Title 7\*\*.

\*\*Voting Systems\*\*: SC transitioned from paperless DREs to \*\*ES&S
ExpressVote BMDs\*\* paired with \*\*DS200 optical scanners\*\*
\[Essvote\](https://www.essvote.com/blog/industry-news/south-carolina-chooses-ess-paper-based-fully-auditable-voting-system/)
in 2019, at a cost of approximately \*\*\$51 million\*\*. \[South
Carolina Election Commission\](https://scvotes.gov/voters/how-to-vote/)
SC was the ﬁrst state to deploy ExpressVote universally.
\[Essvote\](https://www.essvote.com/blog/our-technology/south-carolina-voters-oﬃcials-applaud-expressvote-voting-machines/)
The system uses thermal paper summary cards with barcodes —
\*\*tabulators read the barcode, not the human-readable text\*\*,
\[Veriﬁed
Voting\](https://veriﬁedvoting.org/election-system/ess-expressvote/)
which election integrity advocates have ﬂagged as a concern.

\*\*Firearms at Polling Places\*\*: South Carolina has a \*\*total
prohibition\*\* on ﬁrearms at polling places on election days under
\*\*SC Code § 23-31-215(M)(3)\*\* and \*\*§ 16-23-20\*\*. \[Everytown
Law\](https://everytownlaw.org/report/election-protection/) The 2024
Constitutional Carry Act (H. 3594) explicitly maintained this
prohibition. \[Post and
Courier\](https://www.postandcourier.com/politics/sc-handgun-open-carry-law-2024/article_36687286-dc8c-11ee-8a84-33f028b71b07.html)
This is the clearest and most comprehensive polling place ﬁrearms ban in
this batch.

\*\*Buffer Zone\*\*: At \*\*500 feet\*\* (§ 7-25-180, expanded from 200
feet in 2022), South Carolina has the \*\*second-largest buffer zone\*\*
after Louisiana.

\*\*Preemption\*\*: \*\*§ 23-31-510\*\* preempts all local ﬁrearms
regulation. \[America's 1st Freedom\]
(https://www.americas1stfreedom.org/articles/2020/4/7/south-carolina-attorney-general-sues-city-over-anti-gun-ordinances/)
\[MASC\]
(https://www.masc.sc/uptown/02-2025/recent-laws-impact-carrying-concealable-weapons-public-property)
AG \*\*Alan Wilson\*\* (R, running for Governor 2026) actively enforces
this — he sued Columbia in 2020 for passing local gun ordinances.
\[America's 1st Freedom\]
(https://www.americas1stfreedom.org/articles/2020/4/7/south-carolina-attorney-general-sues-city-over-anti-gun-ordinances/)
Despite SC's abolition of Dillon's Rule (Article VIII, 1973
Constitution), \[South Carolina Association of Counties\]
(https://www.sccounties.org/sites/default/ﬁles/uploads/resources/home_rule_handbook_2024_ed.pdf)
ﬁrearms preemption prevents local polling place ﬁrearms ordinances.

\*\*Cybersecurity\*\*: The \*\*SC Critical Infrastructure Cybersecurity
(SC CIC)\*\* program,
\[Sc\](https://sccic.sc.gov/about/sc-cic-task-force) led by SLED
CIO/CISO \*\*Ryan Truskey\*\*,
\[Sc\](https://sccic.sc.gov/about/leadership) is a strong coordinating
body. SC uniquely has the \*\*125th Cyber Protection Battalion\*\* — one
of only ﬁve such units in the entire Army National Guard. \[Cybersc\]
(https://www.cybersc.us/page-18183) HAVA funding: approximately
\*\*\$6.4M\*\* (FY2020). \[Grantsoﬃce\]
(https://upstream.grantsoﬃce.com/Home/tabid/172/vw/g/vg/60882/2022-HAVA-Election-Security-Funds-South-Carolina-.aspx)
SC does NOT distribute HAVA funds to counties,
\[Grantsoﬃce\](https://upstream.grantsoﬃce.com/Home/tabid/172/vw/g/vg/60882/2022-HAVA-Election-Security-Funds-South-Carolina-.aspx)
creating local funding gaps. \[Civil
Rights\](https://civilrights.org/resource/coalition-letter-supporting-federal-funding-for-election-administration-in-fy26/)

\*\*Key Contacts\*\*: SEC: \*\*(803) 734-9060\*\* \| scvotes.gov \| AG

\### Tennessee — Critical ﬁrearms gap, no polling place prohibition

\*\*Election Authority\*\*: Secretary of State \*\*Tre Hargett\*\* (R)
oversees the Division of Elections. \*\*State Coordinator of Elections
Mark Goins\*\* manages day-to-day operations (elected NASED Vice
President, February 2025). \[Tennessee Secretary of State\]
(https://sos.tn.gov/press-releases/tennessee-coordinator-of-elections-mark-goins-elected-vice-president-of-the-national)
The 7-member \*\*State Election Commission\*\* is elected by the General
Assembly. \[Tennessee Secretary of State\]
(https://sos.tn.gov/elections/guides/tennessee-state-election-commission)
Key statutes are in \*\*Tennessee Code Title 2\*\*.

\*\*Voting Systems\*\*: Tennessee uses a mix of optical scan, DRE with
VVPAT, and BMDs. \[Veriﬁed Voting\]
(https://veriﬁedvoting.org/auditlaw/tennessee/) \*\*Public Chapter 1144
(2022)\*\* required all voting machines to produce voter-veriﬁable paper
audit trails by January 1, 2024 \[Tennessee General
Assembly\](https://wapp.capitol.tn.gov/apps/BillInfo/default.aspx?
BillNumber=HB2331&GA=112) — all 95 counties have now complied.
\[Thinktennessee\](https://www.thinktennessee.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/05/thinktn-voter-ed-series-part-2_casting-ballots.pdf)
Post-election audits began in 2024 (Secretary randomly selects 3–6
counties). \[Veriﬁed
Voting\](https://veriﬁedvoting.org/auditlaw/tennessee/) Tennessee ranked
\*\*#1 in election integrity\*\* per the Heritage Foundation for four
consecutive years. \[Tennessee Secretary of
State\](https://sos.tn.gov/press-releases/secretary-of-state-tre-hargett-announces-newly-certiﬁed-administrators-of-elections)

\*\*Firearms at Polling Places\*\*: \*\*Tennessee has NO speciﬁc statute
prohibiting ﬁrearms at polling places.\*\* \[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/location-restrictions-in-tennessee/)
This is conﬁrmed by Giffords Law Center. Constitutional carry (2021,
T.C.A. § 39-17-1307(g)) allows permitless open and concealed carry for
those 21+. \[USCCA\]
(https://www.usconcealedcarry.com/resources/ccw_reciprocity_map/tn-gun-laws/)
\[Wikipedia\] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gun_laws_in_Tennessee)
While government entities can theoretically post polling places under
\*\*§ 39-17-1359\*\* to prohibit ﬁrearms, this requires providing metal
detectors and security oﬃcers at each entrance \[County Technical
Assistance
Service\](https://www.ctas.tennessee.edu/eli/guns-public-property) —
impractical for most polling locations. The August 2025 \*Hughes v.
Lee\* ruling recognized polling places as "sensitive places" in dicta,
\[NewsChannel 5\]
(https://www.newschannel5.com/news/state/tennessee/davidson-county/a-landmark-tennessee-court-ruling-now-allows-guns-in-state-parks-changes-arrest-powers)
but this has no statutory force.

\*\*Buffer Zone\*\*: \*\*100 feet\*\* (T.C.A. § 2-7-111). This is the
Tennessee case that produced the landmark Supreme Court ruling
\*\*Burson v. Freeman, 504 U.S. 191 (1992)\*\*, upholding buffer zones
under strict scrutiny.
\[Kslegresearch\](https://www.kslegresearch.org/KLRD-web/Publications/ElectionsEthics/electioneering-distances_2022-update.pdf)

\*\*Preemption\*\*: \*\*T.C.A. § 39-17-1314\*\* preempts the "entire
ﬁeld" of ﬁrearms regulation. \[Wikipedia\]
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gun_laws_in_Tennessee)
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/tennessee/title-39/chapter-17/part-13/section-39-17-1314/)
A 2021 strengthening allows adversely affected parties to sue local
governments for violations, with \*\*3x attorney's fees\*\* as damages.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/tennessee/title-39/chapter-17/part-13/section-39-17-1314/)
Home rule under Article XI, § 9 is limited — municipal charters cannot
be inconsistent with general state law. \[FindLaw\]
(https://codes.ﬁndlaw.com/tn/tennessee-constitution/tn-const-art-xi-sect-9/)

\*\*Law Enforcement\*\*: Uniquely, \*\*T.C.A. § 2-7-103(c)\*\* bars law
enforcement from coming within \*\*10 feet\*\* of a polling place
entrance except by request, to arrest, or to vote.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/tennessee/2010/title-2/chapter-7/2-7-103)
\*\*T.C.A. § 58-1-402\*\* explicitly prohibits state guard supervision
of elections.

\*\*Cybersecurity\*\*: \*\*CISO Curtis Clan\*\* leads a 31-person team
within Strategic Technology Solutions (STS). \[BOSS Magazine\]
(https://thebossmagazine.com/state-of-tennessee/) The \*\*Tennessee
Cybersecurity Advisory Council\*\* is co-chaired by the Governor's COO
and State CIO. \[Tennessee
Government\](https://www.tn.gov/cybersecurity/state-government-cybersecurity.html)
HAVA funding: approximately \*\*\$19–20 million\*\* cumulative. The
state used over \*\*\$11.4 million\*\* for the VVPAT transition alone.
\[Issue One\]
(https://issueone.org/articles/federal-funding-for-american-elections-hava-grants/)

\### Alabama — Weakest protections, most hostile environment for reform

\*\*Election Authority\*\*: Secretary of State \*\*Wes Allen\*\* (R,
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/Alabama_Secretary_of_State)
running for Lieutenant Governor \[WAKA Action 8
News\](https://www.waka.com/2025/06/26/campaign-2026-caroleene-dobson-announces-bid-for-

alabama-secretary-of-state/) 2026) is the chief election oﬃcial. County
Probate Judges serve as local election oﬃcials. \[1819 News\]
(https://1819news.com/news/item/tallapoosa-county-tests-es-s-voting-machines-in-preparation-for-midterms)
\[Alabama Secretary of
State\](https://www.sos.alabama.gov/sites/default/ﬁles/election-2022/2024%20Poll%20Worker%20Guide%20Complete.pdf)
Key statutes are in \*\*Alabama Code Title 17\*\*.

\*\*Voting Systems\*\*: Alabama uses \*\*hand-marked paper ballots with
ES&S DS200 optical scanners\*\* and ExpressVote BMDs for accessibility.
Act 2023-131 mandated that voting systems have no internet connectivity.
\[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/alabama/title-17/chapter-7/section-17-7-21/)
\*\*Alabama does NOT require post-election audits\*\* \[Veriﬁed
Voting\](https://veriﬁedvoting.org/auditlaw/alabama/) — Act 2021-446
authorized but did not mandate a one-time audit.

\*\*Firearms at Polling Places\*\*: \*\*Alabama has NO statute
speciﬁcally prohibiting ﬁrearms at polling places.\*\* Per Giffords Law
Center, Alabama "has no statutes prohibiting ﬁrearms in polling places."
The prohibited locations list (§ 13A-11-61.2) includes courthouses,
schools, and DA's oﬃces, but \*\*polling places are conspicuously
absent\*\*. Permitless carry (Act 2022-133, effective January 1, 2023)
allows concealed carry for adults 19+ without any permit or training.
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/concealed-carry-in-alabama/)
If a polling place is in a school, the federal Gun-Free School Zones Act
applies, but this is an incidental rather than deliberate protection.

\*\*Buffer Zone\*\*: Only \*\*30 feet\*\* (§ 17-9-50) — the \*\*smallest
in this batch\*\* and among the smallest in the nation.

\*\*Home Rule\*\*: Alabama has the \*\*weakest home rule in the
nation\*\*. The 1901 Constitution (reorganized but not substantively
changed in 2022) operates under strict Dillon's Rule. \[Encyclopedia of
Alabama\](https://encyclopediaofalabama.org/article/constitutional-reform/)
Only 7 of 67 counties have even limited home rule. The constitution has
been amended \*\*926+ times\*\* because local matters must be handled
through constitutional amendments or legislative acts. \[Bhamwiki\]
(https://www.bhamwiki.com/w/Alabama_Constitution_of_1901)

\*\*Preemption\*\*: \*\*§ 13A-11-61.3\*\* occupies "the entire ﬁeld" of
ﬁrearms regulation "to the complete exclusion" of local action.
Subsection (d) explicitly states home rule status does not create
ﬁrearms regulatory authority. The AG can bring enforcement actions

\## Part III: Municipal ordinance strategy under 18 U.S.C. § 592

\### The preemption problem is insurmountable through conventional
approaches

The central challenge for Protections for Elections in the Deep South is
stark: \*\*every single state in this batch has enacted comprehensive
ﬁrearms preemption that explicitly strips municipalities of authority to
regulate ﬁrearms\*\*. The speciﬁc mechanisms vary but all achieve the
same result:

\- \*\*Florida\*\* (§ 790.33): Personal ﬁnes of \$5,000 on local oﬃcials
\[Fl-counties\](https://faca.ﬂ-counties.com/sites/default/ﬁles/2021-09/Preemption.Whitepaper.61421%20FINAL.pdf)

\- \*\*Georgia\*\* (§ 16-11-173): Private right of action with
attorney's fees against violating localities \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2021/title-16/chapter-11/article-4/part-5/section-16-11-173/)

\- \*\*South Carolina\*\* (§ 23-31-510): AG Wilson has actively
litigated against local gun ordinances \[America's 1st Freedom\]
(https://www.americas1stfreedom.org/articles/2020/4/7/south-carolina-attorney-general-sues-city-over-anti-gun-ordinances/)

\- \*\*Tennessee\*\* (§ 39-17-1314): 3x attorney's fees against
violating localities
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/tennessee/title-39/chapter-17/part-13/section-39-17-1314/)

\- \*\*Louisiana\*\* (R.S. 40:1796): 2024 strengthening made all
existing local ordinances "null and void" \[Louisiana Legislature\]
(https://legis.la.gov/legis/law.aspx?d=97860)

\- \*\*Alabama\*\* (§ 13A-11-61.3): Preemption expressly overrides home
rule authority

\- \*\*Mississippi\*\* (§ 45-9-51): Personal \$1,000 ﬁne on oﬃcials;
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-mississippi/)
proposed further strengthening

A municipality that passes a ﬁrearms ordinance enforcing 18 U.S.C. § 592
would face \*\*immediate legal challenge\*\*, likely resulting in the
ordinance being struck down and the sponsoring oﬃcials facing personal
ﬁnancial penalties. The political environment ensures aggressive
enforcement of preemption by Republican AGs.

\### Home rule authority is insuﬃcient across the board

Even setting aside ﬁrearms preemption, home rule authority for
election-related ordinances is weak or nonexistent:

\- \*\*Alabama\*\*: Virtually no home rule. The 1901 Constitution (still
in effect) strips local governments of independent authority.
\[Princeton Legal
Journal\](https://legaljournal.princeton.edu/sweet-home-alabama-navigating-home-rule-status-through-state-federalism/)
Only 7 of 67 counties have even limited home rule.

\- \*\*Mississippi\*\*: § 21-17-5(2)(c) \*\*explicitly bars\*\*
municipalities from changing election requirements, practices, or
procedures. \[State\]
(https://billstatus.ls.state.ms.us/documents/2020/html/HB/0600-0699/HB0628IN.htm)
This is a direct prohibition on election-related municipal ordinances.

\- \*\*Georgia\*\*: The Constitution (Art. IX, § II) explicitly
\*\*excludes elections\*\* from the subject matters available to home
rule authority. \[New Georgia
Encyclopedia\](https://www.georgiaencyclopedia.org/articles/government-politics/home-rule-and-ordinances/)

\- \*\*Tennessee\*\*: Dillon's Rule applies;
\[MTAS\](https://www.mtas.tennessee.edu/reference/creation-merger-dissolution-cities)
home rule charters cannot be inconsistent with general state law.
\[FindLaw\](https://codes.ﬁndlaw.com/tn/tennessee-constitution/tn-const-art-xi-sect-9/)

\- \*\*Louisiana\*\*: Home rule exists for charter entities but is
subordinate to the state's police power (Art. VI, § 9(B)). \[Louisiana
State Legislature\](https://www.legis.la.gov/legis/Law.aspx?d=206520)

\- \*\*Florida\*\*: Broad home rule exists but is completely overridden
by preemption in both ﬁrearms and elections ﬁelds. \[Florida Senate\]
(https://www.ﬂsenate.gov/Session/Bill/2022/518/Analyses/2022s00518.pre.rc.PDF)

\- \*\*South Carolina\*\*: The strongest home rule framework (Dillon's
Rule abolished in 1973), \[Statehousereport\]
(https://www.statehousereport.com/2017/02/02/history-home-rule/) but
still cannot overcome ﬁrearms preemption.

\### Priority targeting for municipal ordinance campaigns

Given these constraints, the strategy must shift from conventional
ﬁrearms-at-polling-places ordinances to alternative approaches:

\*\*Highest priority (greatest gaps in state protection)\*\*:

1\. \*\*Alabama\*\* — No ﬁrearms prohibition at polling places, weakest
buffer zone (30 feet), weakest voter intimidation penalties, weakest
home rule

2\. \*\*Tennessee\*\* — No ﬁrearms prohibition at polling places, no
speciﬁc statutory protection, 100-foot buffer zone

\*\*Secondary priority (partial protections with gaps)\*\*:

3\. \*\*Mississippi\*\* — Ambiguous protections (enhanced permit holders
may be exempt; open carry gap for non-candidates) 4. \*\*Louisiana\*\* —
No explicit open carry prohibition at polling places

\*\*Lower priority (existing state protections are adequate)\*\*:

5\. \*\*Georgia\*\* — 150-foot ﬁrearms ban at polling places already in
state law 6. \*\*South Carolina\*\* — Total polling place ﬁrearms ban
already in state law

7\. \*\*Florida\*\* — Concealed carry prohibited; open carry already
illegal generally

\### Alternative strategic pathways

Given that conventional municipal ﬁrearms ordinances are not viable in
any of these seven states, Protections for Elections should consider:

\*\*Federal enforcement focus\*\*: Rather than municipal ordinances,
campaign for \*\*federal enforcement of 18 U.S.C. § 592\*\* itself. The
statute already prohibits armed forces at polling places. Advocate for
DOJ Civil Rights Division enforcement, particularly in states without
state-level prohibitions (AL, TN).

\*\*Resolution-based approach\*\*: Municipalities can pass
\*\*non-binding resolutions\*\* expressing support for enforcement of 18
U.S.C. § 592 without creating enforceable ordinances that would trigger
preemption challenges. While symbolic, these create political pressure
and public awareness.

\*\*Election administration pathway\*\*: Rather than ﬁrearms ordinances,
work through \*\*county election oﬃcials\*\* (who control polling place
operations) to exercise their existing statutory authority to maintain
order. In Tennessee, election commissions could post polling places
under § 39-17-1359. In Alabama, polling place location choices could
prioritize venues with existing ﬁrearms restrictions (schools, certain
government buildings with security).

\*\*Coalition opportunities\*\*: Partner with county Supervisors of
Elections (FL), county Probate Judges (AL), county election commissions
(TN), and local law enforcement to develop voluntary security protocols.
The League of Women Voters, NAACP state conferences, and Southern
Poverty Law Center are natural coalition partners. In Georgia,
organizations like Fair Fight Action have existing infrastructure.

\*\*State legislative campaigns\*\*: While politically challenging in
all seven states, the most viable long-term strategy is passing
state-level polling place ﬁrearms prohibitions in Alabama and Tennessee
— the two states without any such protection. Framing this as a public
safety issue rather than a gun control measure may ﬁnd limited
bipartisan support, particularly given that Georgia and South Carolina
(both conservative states) already have such protections.

\### The Deep South political reality

All seven states present fundamentally hostile environments for election
protection ordinances:

\- \*\*All seven\*\* have Republican governors, AGs, and legislative
supermajorities - \*\*All seven\*\* adopted constitutional/permitless
carry between 2016–2024

\- \*\*All seven\*\* have comprehensive ﬁrearms preemption -
\*\*None\*\* joined the 19-state lawsuit against EO 14248 - \*\*None\*\*
have state-level Voting Rights Acts

\- \*\*Five of seven\*\* were formerly covered by Section 5 of the VRA
(AL, GA, LA, MS, SC) — all freed from preclearance by \*Shelby County v.
Holder\*

The organizations most hostile to this work will be the NRA, state
ﬁrearms organizations, and allied Republican legislators. Any municipal
ordinance campaign in these states risks not only legal defeat but
\*\*retaliatory state legislation\*\* — as has occurred in Florida (§
790.33 personal penalties), Georgia (SB 204 strengthening preemption),
and Louisiana (2024 preemption expansion).

\## Conclusion: Strategic recalibration required

The Deep South batch reveals a fundamentally different landscape from
northeastern states where Protections for Elections may ﬁnd more
receptive conditions. \*\*The municipal ordinance model is structurally
non-viable in all seven states\*\* due to the interlocking barriers of
ﬁrearms preemption, weak home rule authority, explicit election
administration preemption, and hostile political environments. The two
most alarming ﬁndings are Alabama's and Tennessee's complete lack of any
polling place ﬁrearms prohibition — voters can legally carry ﬁrearms
into many polling places in both states — combined with the legal
impossibility of local governments addressing this gap.

The most productive reallocation of resources would focus on \*\*federal
enforcement of existing law\*\* (18 U.S.C. § 592), \*\*election
administration partnerships\*\* to use existing authority creatively,
and \*\*long-term state legislative campaigns\*\* in Alabama and
Tennessee modeled on the protections that already exist in Georgia and
South Carolina. The organization should also invest in \*\*litigation
capacity\*\* to bring federal claims under the VRA and § 1983 when armed
intimidation at polling places occurs, rather than attempting preventive
municipal ordinances that cannot survive preemption challenges. Georgia
and South Carolina demonstrate that conservative Deep South states
\*can\* maintain polling place ﬁrearms prohibitions — the challenge is
building political will in the states that lack them.
