\# Four-state municipal election protection ordinance: legal and
strategic assessment

\*\*The campaign to enforce 18 U.S.C. § 592 through municipal ordinances
faces a stratiﬁed landscape: Texas offers the strongest legal foundation
and coalition infrastructure, Oklahoma presents a viable secondary front
anchored by Tulsa's new progressive leadership, Utah provides a narrow
but defensible path through Salt Lake City, and Wyoming should be
deprioritized entirely.\*\* The "mirror argument" — that states which
adopted anti-commandeering legislation for ﬁrearms enforcement cannot
credibly deny municipalities the same principle for election protection
— is the campaign's most powerful legal weapon, deployable in Texas,
Oklahoma, and potentially Utah. Across all four states, the ordinance
must be meticulously framed as a municipal resource allocation decision
supporting existing federal law, not as ﬁrearms regulation, to survive
preemption challenges.

This report covers all ten research areas for each state, provides a
comparative assessment, and recommends a sequenced rollout strategy for
"Protections for Elections."

\## I. Texas: the strongest foundation for a high-stakes ﬁght

\### Home rule authority

Texas provides the most robust home rule framework of the four states.
\*\*Article XI, Section 5 of the Texas Constitution\*\* grants cities
with populations exceeding \*\*5,000\*\* the power to adopt charters
\[Austin
Texas\](https://www.austintexas.gov/page/charter-amendment-petitions)
conferring "full power of local self-government."
\[Tml\](https://www.tml.org/DocumentCenter/View/244/Types-of-Texas-Cities-PDF)
Texas operates a hybrid model: general law cities follow Dillon's Rule,
while the state's \*\*350+ home rule cities\*\* derive powers directly
from the constitution.

The foundational principle was established in \*Forwood v. City of
Taylor\*, 214 S.W.2d 282 (Tex. 1948): home rule cities possess "full
authority to do anything the legislature could theretofore have
authorized them to do." Courts look to legislative acts "not for grants
of power...but only for limitations on their powers."
\[TLG\](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution:Article_XI,\_Section_5)
This was reaﬃrmed in \*S. Crushed Concrete, LLC v. City of Houston\*,
398 S.W.3d 676 (Tex. 2013). \[TLG\]
(https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution:Article_XI,\_Section_5)
Home rule is \*\*self-executing\*\* — no enabling legislation is
required.
\[Tml\](https://www.tml.org/DocumentCenter/View/244/Types-of-Texas-Cities-PDF)
The preemption standard requires "unmistakable clarity" from the
legislature per \*Dallas Merchant's & Concessionaire's Ass'n\*, 852
S.W.2d 489 (Tex. 1993). \[Tml\]
(https://www.tml.org/DocumentCenter/View/2192/Home-Rule-Charters---2020-08-PDF)

Texas counties have \*\*no home rule authority whatsoever\*\*. Per
\*City of San Antonio v. City of Boerne\*, 111 S.W.3d 22 (Tex. 2003),
counties possess "only those powers expressly conferred by the Texas
Constitution and the Legislature." County-level action is limited to
non-binding resolutions, cooperative agreements, and sheriff's
discretionary policies.

\### Preemption landscape and the mirror argument

Three preemption regimes are relevant. \*\*Texas Government Code §
229.001\*\* preempts municipal ﬁrearms regulation \[Texas Statutes\]
(https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/pdf/LG.229.pdf) but includes
a critical exception: subsection (g) states the section "does not limit
the enforceability of any state or federal law." \[Texas
Statutes\](https://statutes.capitol.texas.gov/Docs/LG/htm/LG.229.htm)
The proposed ordinance does not regulate ﬁrearms — it governs city
resource allocation — and § 229.001(g) actually supports it since 18
U.S.C. § 592 is existing federal law.

\*\*HB 2622 (2021)\*\*, the Texas Second Amendment Sanctuary Act
\[Wikipedia\] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Amendment_sanctuary)
(codiﬁed at Tex. Penal Code § 1.10), is the linchpin of the mirror
argument. It prohibits any state or local agency from "contract\[ing\]
with or in any other manner provid\[ing\] assistance to a federal agency
or oﬃcial" enforcing certain federal ﬁrearms regulations \[Texas
Capitol\]

(https://capitol.texas.gov/tlodocs/87R/analysis/html/HB02622E.htm)
enacted after January 19, 2021. \[Sanctuary Counties\]
(https://sanctuarycounties.com/2021/06/17/texas-is-now-the-15th-second-amendment-sanctuary-state/)
This establishes that Texas law explicitly endorses municipalities
refusing to assist federal agents — the identical anti-commandeering
principle the proposed ordinance would invoke. \*\*Texas cannot credibly
argue that municipalities lack power to refuse federal cooperation when
the state has codiﬁed exactly that power for ﬁrearms purposes.\*\*

\*\*SB 4 (2017)\*\* (Tex. Gov't Code § 752.053) prohibits "sanctuary
city" policies regarding immigration enforcement. \[Baker Institute\]
(https://www.bakerinstitute.org/research/uncertain-future-texas-sb-4)
Its existence alongside HB 2622 creates a powerful \*\*negative
inference\*\*: where the legislature intended to mandate cooperation
with federal agents, it did so explicitly (immigration via SB 4). Its
silence on polling-place security cooperation suggests no preemption
exists.

\*\*HB 2127 (2023)\*\*, the "Death Star" law, preempts local regulation
in eight areas including business, labor, and ﬁnance — but \*\*not
elections or election security\*\*. Travis County Judge Maya Guerra
Gamble declared it unconstitutional in August 2023; litigation
continues. This gap further strengthens the negative inference.

Austin's history of preempted ordinances (plastic bag ban struck down
via \*Laredo Merchants Association v. City of Laredo\*, 2018; paid sick
leave overturned in \*Texas Ass'n of Business v. City of Austin\*, 2018;
ban-the-box preempted by HB 2127) teaches a critical lesson: ordinances
fail when they directly conﬂict with speciﬁc statutes. The proposed
ordinance occupies unpreempted territory and governs internal resource
allocation rather than private conduct.

\### Constitutional basis

\*\*Article I, Section 24\*\* of the Texas Constitution provides: "The
military shall at all times be subordinate to the civil authority."
\[Texas\] (https://tlc.texas.gov/docs/legref/TxConst.pdf) This has
appeared in every Texas constitution since 1836 \[TLG\]
(https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution:Article_I,\_Section_24)
and was cited in \*Cole v. Texas Army Nat'l Guard\*, 909 S.W.2d 535
(Tex. App.—Austin 1995).
\[TLG\](https://texaslegalguide.com/Texas_Constitution:Article_I,\_Section_24)
Article I, Section 19 prohibits disfranchisement "except by the due
course of the law of the land." \[Tarlton Law Library\]
(https://tarlton.law.utexas.edu/constitutions/texas-1876-en/preamble-article-1-bill-rights)

The anti-commandeering doctrine — \*Printz v. United States\*, 521 U.S.
898 (1997); \*Murphy v. NCAA\*, 584 U.S. 453 (2018); \*New York v.
United States\*, 505 U.S. 144 (1992)
\[LegalClarity\](https://legalclarity.org/what-is-the-anti-commandeering-doctrine/)
— provides the federal constitutional backbone. The ordinance should be
framed as: (1) an exercise of municipal resource allocation authority,
(2) local policy aligned with existing federal law (18 U.S.C. § 592),
(3) implementation of Texas Constitution Art. I, § 24, and (4) a mirror
of state-sanctioned anti-commandeering under HB 2622.

\## II. Oklahoma: the Tulsa opportunity and the SB 631 mirror

\### Home rule authority

Oklahoma operates under \*\*modiﬁed Dillon's Rule with constitutional
home rule provisions for charter cities\*\*. Article XVIII, Section 3(a)
of the Oklahoma Constitution provides \[Oxford
Academic\](https://academic.oup.com/book/41586/chapter-abstract/353231389?
redirectedFrom=fulltext) that any city with \*\*2,000+ inhabitants\*\*
may frame a charter "consistent with and subject to the Constitution and
laws of this State" through a freeholder charter process. Oklahoma has
\*\*86 charter municipalities\*\* and 508 general law municipalities.
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/Laws_governing_local_ballot_measures_in_Oklahoma)

Courts resolve doubts about municipal authority against the
municipality. The 1907 constitutional convention included
progressive-era home rule provisions, but the "consistent with and
subject to" language gives state law supremacy over conﬂicting charter
provisions. Counties have \*\*no home rule authority\*\* — Article XVII
governs county government with purely statutory powers. No counties have
initiative and referendum powers.
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/Laws_governing_local_ballot_measures_in_Oklahoma)

\### Preemption landscape and the SB 631 mirror

\*\*21 O.S. § 1289.24\*\* preempts the "entire ﬁeld of
legislation...touching in any way ﬁrearms...to the complete exclusion of
any order, ordinance, or regulation by any municipality."
\[ATF\](https://www.atf.gov/ﬁrearms/docs/undeﬁned/oklahoma-state-laws-and-published-ordinances/download)
Section D creates a \*\*private right of action\*\* allowing civil suits
against municipalities violating ﬁrearms preemption. \[Justia
+2\](https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/title-21/section-21-1289-24/)
The preemption targets municipal regulation of private ﬁrearms
possession/carrying; the proposed ordinance directs city resources
regarding cooperation with armed federal personnel — a critical
distinction.

\*\*SB 631 (2021)\*\*, the Oklahoma Second Amendment Sanctuary State Act
\[Wikipedia\] (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Amendment_sanctuary)
\[BillTrack50\](https://www.billtrack50.com/billdetail/1279656) (codiﬁed
at 21 O.S. § 1289.24e), creates the mirror argument. It directs state
and local agencies to refuse enforcement of federal ﬁrearms laws deemed
to infringe on Second Amendment rights.
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Amendment_sanctuary)
Section C declares: "It shall be the duty of the courts and law
enforcement agencies of this state to protect the rights of law-abiding
citizens to keep and to bear arms...from the infringement provided under
the provisions of this act." \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/oklahoma/2021/title-21/section-21-1289-24e/)
\*\*The identical anti-commandeering principle supports a municipal
ordinance directing local resources not to cooperate with armed federal
personnel at polling places\*\* — especially when such cooperation would
facilitate what 18 U.S.C. § 592 already prohibits.

Anti-sanctuary city legislation includes \*\*HB 4156 (2024)\*\* (which
included sanctuary city preemption, passed 77-20 in the House),
\[Oklahoma Council of Public
Affairs\](https://ocpathink.org/post/independent-journalism/illegal-immigration-bill-sent-to-governor)
\*\*SB 572 (2021)\*\* (which authorizes AG opinions declaring a local
government has a sanctuary policy, making it ineligible for state
funds),
\[FAIR\](https://www.fairus.org/legislation/state-and-local-legislation/sanctuary-policies/oklahoma-anti-sanctuary-bills-advance)
and \*\*SB 781 (2021)\*\* requiring compliance with federal immigration
detainers. The negative inference argument is strong: the legislature
preempted in ﬁrearms, immigration, and oil & gas — but \*\*not\*\* in
election security or cooperation with armed federal personnel at polling
places.
\[Ballotpedia\](https://ballotpedia.org/Laws_governing_local_ballot_measures_in_Oklahoma)

\## III. Utah: a narrow path through aggressive preemption

\### Home rule authority

Utah's framework rests on a landmark judicial decision. In \*\*State v.
Hutchinson\*\*, 624 P.2d 1116 (Utah 1980), the Utah Supreme Court
\*\*expressly abandoned Dillon's Rule\*\*, calling it "archaic,
unrealistic, and unresponsive to current needs." \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/cases/utah/supreme-court/1980/16087-0.html)
Article XI, Section 5 of the Utah Constitution grants charter cities
authority to "exercise all powers relating to municipal affairs...not in
conﬂict with the general law." \[Utah Legislature\]
(https://le.utah.gov/xcode/ArticleXI/UC_AXI_1800010118000101.pdf) The
court in \*Dairy Prod. Servs., Inc. v. City of Wellsville\*, 13 P.3d 581
(Utah 2000) aﬃrmed cities have "independent authority, apart from
speciﬁc grants of power" for regulations "reasonably and appropriately
related to" police power objectives.
\[Squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/50-States-UT-Home-Rule-2020-updates-ﬁnal.pdf)

However, this liberal construction is subject to \*\*express legislative
preemption\*\*, which the legislature has wielded aggressively. Counties
have weaker authority, though Salt Lake, Summit, and other counties
operate under home rule charters.

\### The "inversion" preemption model

Utah has the most aggressive preemption regime of the four states.
\*\*Utah Code § 76-10-500\*\* declares the legislature "occupies the
whole ﬁeld of state regulation of ﬁrearms."
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/utah/title-76/chapter-10/part-5/section-500/)
Subsection (6) prohibits any local directive "pertaining to ﬁrearms that
in any way inhibits or restricts the possession, ownership, purchase,
sale, transfer, transport, or use of ﬁrearms on either public or private
property." The \*\*Firearm Preemption Enforcement Act (HB 76, 2021)\*\*
(§§ 78B-6-2201 through 2204) creates a private cause of action with
attorney fees and damages against municipalities violating ﬁrearms
preemption — one of the most aggressive enforcement mechanisms
nationally.

The "inversion" extends beyond ﬁrearms: the legislature has preempted
local authority on immigration, land use (multiple housing/zoning
bills), short-term rentals, government structure (forcing Grand County
to change from nonpartisan to partisan form via HB 224, 2018), and
school district authority (HB 29, 2024).
\[Utahcitizenscounsel\](https://www.utahcitizenscounsel.org/balance-of-state-and-local-governing/)
Title 20A comprehensively governs elections, though it does not contain
express preemption of local election security measures.

\*\*The critical vulnerability\*\*: § 76-10-500(6)'s extraordinary
breadth — "in any way inhibits or restricts...use of ﬁrearms" — could
capture an ordinance that differentiates treatment based on whether
federal personnel are armed, even though the ordinance targets city
resource allocation rather than ﬁrearms regulation. This is the single
most dangerous legal exposure in Utah.

\### Constitutional basis — Article I, Section 17

Utah's strongest constitutional provision is \*\*Article I, Section
17\*\*: "All elections shall be free, and no power, civil or military,
shall at any time interfere to prevent the free exercise of the right of
suffrage." \[Utah Legislature\]
(https://le.utah.gov/xcode/ArticleI/UC_AI_1800010118000101.pdf) This
echoes 18 U.S.C. § 592 and provides a powerful state constitutional
anchor. Article I, Section 2 vests all political power in the people.

\### Target municipalities

\## IV. Wyoming: redirect resources elsewhere

\### The case against inclusion

Wyoming presents a near-perfect storm of obstacles. It is the \*\*most
Republican state by presidential vote share\*\* (Trump won 71.6% in
2024).
\[Wikipedia\](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teton_County,\_Wyoming) The
\*\*Wyoming Freedom Caucus holds 34+ of 62 House seats\*\* — the ﬁrst
state Freedom Caucus to control a legislative chamber nationally. The
Senate is 29-2 Republican. \[Cowboy State Daily\]
(https://cowboystatedaily.com/2022/11/18/teton-county-republicans-outnumbered-but-that-wasnt-always-so/)
Coalition infrastructure is negligible: the ACLU of Wyoming is a
tri-state chapter (WY/ND/SD) with limited standalone capacity, and no
signiﬁcant voting rights organizations exist.

\### Legal framework

\*\*Article XIII, Section 1\*\* of the Wyoming Constitution (amended
1972) grants cities and towns power to "determine their local affairs
and government," subject to "statutes uniformly applicable to all cities
and towns." \[Wyoming Extension\]
(https://wyoextension.org/publications/html/B1323/)
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Wyoming.pdf)
Section 1(d) mandates liberal construction "for the purpose of giving
the largest measure of self-government." \[Wyoming Extension\]
(https://wyoextension.org/publications/html/B1323/)
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Wyoming.pdf)
However, the Wyoming Supreme Court has \*\*substantially undermined this
mandate\*\*. In \*Cheyenne Airport Bd. v. Rogers\*, 707 P.2d 717 (Wyo.
1985), the court called municipalities "largely the creatures of the
sovereign state and in need of speciﬁc delegations of powers" — pure
Dillon's Rule language.
\[Squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Wyoming.pdf)
Scholar Thomas S. Smith argued in "No Home on the Range for Home Rule,"
31 Land & Water L. Rev. 791 (1996), that the court
"inexplicabl\[y\]...ignores" the home rule amendment.
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Wyoming.pdf)

\*\*Wyo. Stat. § 6-8-401(c)\*\* provides comprehensive ﬁrearms
preemption covering "sale, transfer, purchase, delivery, taxation,
manufacture, ownership, transportation, storage, use, carrying or
possession of ﬁrearms." \[Justia\]
(https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-6/chapter-8/article-4/section-6-8-401/)
The \*\*Second Amendment Protection Act (Wyo. Stat. § 9-14-203,
2022)\*\* imposes criminal penalties (up to 1 year imprisonment, \$2,000
ﬁne) on oﬃcials enforcing "unconstitutional" federal gun control —
Wyoming is one of only three states with "gold-rated" gun sanctuary
laws. \*\*HB 172 (2025)\*\* repealed gun-free zones and strengthened
preemption further. \[Wyoming
Legislature\](https://www.wyoleg.gov/2021/Introduced/SF0067.pdf) \*\*HB
133 (2025)\*\* banned sanctuary cities with private enforcement actions
against non-compliant oﬃcials.

\### One notable constitutional provision

\*\*Article 6, Section 27\*\* contains powerful language: "Elections
shall be open, free and equal, and no power, civil or military, shall at
any time interfere to prevent an untrammeled exercise of the right of
suffrage." \[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/constitution/wyoming/)
Article I, Section 31 provides the military "is at all times and in all
places subordinate to the civil power." These provisions are valuable as
\*\*legal research assets for briefs in other jurisdictions\*\*, not as
grounds for Wyoming municipal action.

(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teton_County,\_Wyoming)

swing county with narrow Democratic margins. \[Wikipedia\]
(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albany_County,\_Wyoming) Both face

\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/wyoming/title-6/chapter-8/article-4/section-6-8-401/)

\## VI. Sequenced rollout strategy and campaign architecture

\### Wave 1 (March–June 2026): Texas launch

Austin should be the ﬁrst ordinance introduced. The city's
all-Democratic council, experienced city attorney's oﬃce (already
litigating HB 2127), and favorable Travis County district courts make
passage most likely. San Antonio should ﬁle simultaneously or within
weeks. Houston provides scale as a third Wave 1 city. \*\*Timing is
critical\*\*: pass before November 2026 elections for maximum
voter-protection messaging impact and before the January 2027
legislative session establishes precedent.

Ordinance language must emphasize: (1) the city shall not expend
municipal resources to assist armed federal personnel in establishing or
maintaining a presence within a deﬁned perimeter of polling places
during election periods; (2) express ﬁndings citing Art. I, § 24 of the
Texas Constitution, 18 U.S.C. § 592, and the anti-commandeering
principles underlying HB 2622; (3) framing as a resource allocation and
public safety measure; (4) severability clause. Avoid the words
"ﬁrearms," "weapons," or "guns" entirely. Use "armed federal personnel"
as the operative category.

\### Wave 2 (June–September 2026): Oklahoma expansion

Tulsa should introduce its ordinance after the Oklahoma legislature
adjourns in late May 2026. Mayor Monroe Nichols's progressive mandate
and the city's strong-mayor charter provide institutional capacity. The
Tulsa Race Massacre centennial legacy gives unique narrative weight to
election protection. Norman should follow within weeks. Ordinance
language should parallel the Texas model with Oklahoma-speciﬁc ﬁndings
citing Art. II, § 4 (interference with suffrage prohibition), Art. II, §
14 (military subordination), \[Oklahoma
Senate\](https://oksenate.gov/sites/default/ﬁles/2022-05/oc2_1.pdf) and
SB 631's anti-commandeering framework.

\### Wave 3 (April–June 2026): Utah parallel track

Salt Lake City should introduce its ordinance after the Utah legislative
session ends in early March 2026. Drafting requires extreme precision to
avoid triggering § 76-10-500's "in any way inhibits or restricts...use
of ﬁrearms" language. The ordinance should focus exclusively on city
employee duties, resource allocation, and facility use — never
referencing ﬁrearms. Express ﬁndings should cite Art. I, § 17 ("no
power, civil or military, shall at any time interfere to prevent the
free exercise of the right of suffrage"). \*\*Salt Lake County\*\*
should be explored as a parallel or alternative vehicle, given the
county clerk's control over polling place administration.

\### Cross-cutting legal strategy

Retain experienced municipal law counsel in each state before any
introduction. Prepare defensive litigation strategies including
preliminary injunction responses. Coordinate amicus support from
national organizations (Brennan Center, States United Democracy Center,
Lawyers' Committee for Civil Rights). Engage law school clinics for
academic support: UT Austin, OU College of Law, University of Utah S.J.
Quinney College of Law.

The \*\*anti-commandeering mirror argument\*\* should be the centerpiece
of both legal defense and public communications. The messaging frame:
"These states told their cities they don't have to help the federal
government enforce new gun laws. We're simply applying the same
principle to protect voters from armed intimidation at the polls —
something federal law has banned since Reconstruction."

\## VII. Model ordinance architecture across jurisdictions

Every ordinance across all three active states should share a common
architecture while incorporating state-speciﬁc constitutional hooks. The
operative provision should direct that no city oﬃcer, employee, or
department shall use city funds, equipment, facilities, or personnel to
assist, support, or facilitate the activities of armed federal personnel
within a deﬁned buffer zone of any designated polling place during any
election period, consistent with 18 U.S.C. § 592. A "whereas" clause
section should cite: the applicable state constitutional provision on
free elections and military subordination; 18 U.S.C. § 592's existing
federal prohibition; the anti-commandeering doctrine; and, in Texas and
Oklahoma speciﬁcally, the state's own anti-commandeering legislation.

\*\*Key drafting principles\*\* that apply universally: frame as
resource allocation, not regulation; use "armed federal personnel"
rather than any ﬁrearms-related terminology; structure as non-assistance
rather than obstruction; include robust severability clauses; deﬁne
"election period" broadly to cover early voting and Election Day;
establish a clear geographic buffer measured from polling place
boundaries.

\## Conclusion

The four-state assessment reveals a stratiﬁed but actionable landscape.
\*\*Texas is the campaign's center of gravity\*\* — its strong home rule
framework, the HB 2622 mirror argument, deep coalition infrastructure,
and strategic timing window before the January 2027 session make it the
highest-return investment. Oklahoma's Tulsa opportunity under Mayor
Nichols offers a compelling secondary front with its own powerful
constitutional provisions and SB 631 mirror argument. Utah presents a
narrow, high-risk path constrained by the nation's most aggressive
ﬁrearms preemption enforcement mechanism, but Salt Lake City's
progressive leadership and Article I, Section 17 provide enough legal
foundation to justify investment. Wyoming should be excluded from active
campaign operations entirely.

The most novel strategic insight across all four states is the
\*\*asymmetric leverage of the anti-commandeering mirror argument\*\*:
Texas and Oklahoma have created a legal and rhetorical framework that,
by their own logic, supports the proposed ordinance. Forcing opponents
to distinguish between "good" anti-commandeering (refusing to enforce
federal gun laws) and "bad" anti-commandeering (refusing to assist armed
federal personnel at polls) creates a political burden that transcends
the legal merits. Even ordinances that face eventual preemption or
judicial defeat generate lasting value by establishing this framework in
public discourse, building coalition infrastructure, and forcing state
oﬃcials to publicly oppose election protection — a position that carries
signiﬁcant political cost in any electoral context.
