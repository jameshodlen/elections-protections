\# Election security infrastructure: Plains & Mountain West regional
batch

\*\*Six conservative-leaning states — Missouri, Montana, Idaho, North
Dakota, South Dakota, and Wyoming — present the most formidable
challenge for municipal ordinance strategies enforcing 18 U.S.C. §
592.\*\* All six are constitutional carry states with comprehensive
ﬁrearms preemption, Republican trifecta governments, and no
participation in the 19-state EO 14248 lawsuit. Only Missouri maintains
any statutory restriction on ﬁrearms at polling places. The combination
of Dillon's Rule governance (or weakened home rule), aggressive state
ﬁrearms preemption, and CISA's withdrawal from election security support
creates a landscape where municipal-level action faces
near-insurmountable legal barriers — yet also where the need for polling
place protections may be greatest.

This guide is prepared for \*\*Protections for Elections\*\*, a
non-partisan 501(c)(4) organization focused on passing municipal
ordinances that enforce the federal ban on armed troops at polling
places.

\### 3. Physical security and polling place protections

\*\*Buffer zone: 100 feet\*\* from any entrance to a polling place under
SDCL 12-18-3. Prohibited activities include campaign oﬃces, public
address systems, soliciting votes, petition signatures, and use of
communication/photographic devices that "repeatedly distracts,
interrupts, or intimidates." The general prohibition states: "No person
may engage in any practice which interferes with the voter's free access
to the polls." Violation is a \*\*Class 2 misdemeanor\*\* (maximum 30
days jail and/or \$500 ﬁne).

\*\*South Dakota has no state law prohibiting ﬁrearms at polling
places\*\* — conﬁrmed by Giffords Law Center. The state became a
constitutional carry state on July 1, 2019. \*\*South Dakota has among
the strongest ﬁrearms preemption laws in the nation\*\*: SDCL § 9-19-20
(municipalities), § 7-18A-36 (counties), and § 8-5-13 (townships) all
declare that no political subdivision "may pass any ordinance that
restricts or prohibits… the possession, storage, transportation,
purchase, sale, transfer, ownership, manufacture, or repair of ﬁrearms
or ammunition." Any violating ordinance is \*\*automatically null and
void\*\*. The AG is \*\*statutorily required\*\* to send
cease-and-desist orders and bring injunction actions against
non-compliant localities, with localities facing liability for legal
fees.

Historical voter intimidation in South Dakota, particularly against
\*\*Native American voters\*\*, is well documented. In \*Daschle v.
Thune\* (D.S.D. 2004), a federal court found that following voters to
polling places and recording license plates constituted illegal voter
intimidation. Federal judges have found "intimidation particularly
targeted at Native Americans in Charles Mix County."

\### 4. Legal strategies and constitutional protections

federal courts will enforce voter intimidation claims. \*\*South Dakota
has no state voting rights act.\*\*

\## Wyoming (WY)

\### 1. State election authority and legal framework

\*\*Secretary of State Chuck Gray (R)\*\* assumed oﬃce January 2023.
Gray has been among the most aggressive SOS oﬃcials in pushing "election
integrity" measures aligned with conservative priorities. The SOS
oversees 23 county clerks in Wyoming's decentralized system,

governed by \*\*Wyoming Statutes Title 22\*\* (Election Code of 1973, as
amended). Key provisions include W.S. 22-6-130 (post-election ballot
audit, enacted 2023), W.S. 22-15-109 (poll watchers), and W.S. 22-26-101
through 22-26-121 (offenses and penalties).

Wyoming has \*\*constitutional home rule\*\* since a 1972 amendment
(Article 13, Section 1), empowering cities and towns to "determine their
local affairs and government" with powers "liberally construed for the
purpose of giving the largest measure of self-government." However, the
Wyoming Association of Municipalities has noted that many consider
Wyoming municipalities to have been granted \*\*"home rule authority in
name only"\*\* — the provision is qualiﬁed as "subject to state
statutes," and the Wyoming Supreme Court has never broadly interpreted
it. Counties operate under Dillon's Rule principles. Comprehensive
ﬁrearms preemption (W.S. 6-8-401) renders municipal ﬁrearms ordinances
legally impossible.

The \*\*2025 legislature passed a sweeping conservative package\*\*: HEA
57 (documentary proof of citizenship for registration), HEA 61 (ban on
foreign funding of ballot measures), HEA 62 (voter roll maintenance
using DHS SAVE system), HEA 65 (ban on private election funds), HEA 71
(ranked choice voting prohibition), and critically, \*\*HB 172 — the
Wyoming Repeal Gun Free Zones Act\*\* (effective July 1, 2025), which
allows concealed carry in government meetings, public buildings, and
airports while strengthening ﬁrearms preemption. Governor Gordon allowed
HB 172 to become law without his signature. HB 245 would require all
counties to use paper ballots, targeting Laramie County's DRE
touch-screen system.

Nearly all 23 counties use hand-marked paper ballots with ES&S optical
scanners. \*\*Laramie County was the sole DRE holdout.\*\* Post-election
audits are mandated since 2023 (W.S. 22-6-130), requiring audit of
statewide contests with a 5% ballot sample.

\### 2. Cybersecurity infrastructure and capabilities

\*\*CISO Miguel Penaranda\*\* was appointed February 2025 within Wyoming
Enterprise Technology Services (ETS), led by CIO Jeff Clines (appointed
September 2024). The cybersecurity team of approximately \*\*5
people\*\* is responsible for 127 state agencies. Wyoming uses
CrowdStrike Falcon XDR as its primary platform and KnowBe4 for training.
Governor Gordon established the \*\*CARE Team\*\* (Cyber Assistance
Response Effort) by Executive Order 2023-02, uniting ETS, the Wyoming
Information Analysis Team (WIAT), Division of Criminal Investigation,
Oﬃce of Homeland Security, and National Guard.

HAVA funding totals approximately \*\*\$6-8 million\*\* cumulative: \$3M
in FY2018 (90% toward voting equipment), \$1M in FY2022 (\$425K
security, \$425K WyoReg, \$150K training). The Wyoming National Guard
underwent a U.S. Army Cyber Command Cyber Operational Readiness
Assessment in August 2024, but has \*\*no dedicated state cyber unit\*\*
comparable to larger states, relying on federal Guard assets and the
multi-agency CARE Team.

\*\*Wyoming is among the most vulnerable states to CISA withdrawal.\*\*
With only 5 cybersecurity staff, 23 small counties with minimal IT
resources, no dedicated Guard cyber unit, and effectively defunct
EI-ISAC services, the state faces signiﬁcant election security gaps.
Former CISA oﬃcial Kim Wyman's warning applies directly: "The small and
medium counties were already struggling, and this is going to be a
blow."

\### 3. Physical security and polling place protections

Wyoming has the \*\*largest buffer zone in this batch\*\*: \*\*100 yards
(300 feet)\*\* from the building containing a polling place on Election
Day under W.S. 22-26-113, with a 100-foot zone for absentee polling
places. The 10th Circuit upheld the 300-foot Election Day zone as
constitutional in \*\*Frank v. Lee\*\* (October 2023), reversing the
district court. The absentee zone and overbreadth issues were remanded
and continue before Judge Kelly Rankin.

\*\*Wyoming presents the most challenging environment for polling place
ﬁrearms protections.\*\* Wyoming was one of the \*\*earliest
constitutional carry adopters\*\* (2011 for residents, expanded 2021).
State ﬁrearms preemption under \*\*W.S. 6-8-401\*\* is comprehensive:
"No state agency, city, town, county, political subdivision or any other
entity shall authorize, regulate, or prohibit" ﬁrearms carrying or
possession except as speciﬁed by state law. \*\*HB 172 (2025) further
expanded gun rights\*\* by repealing gun-free zones in government
buildings. Polling places are \*\*not listed\*\* as prohibited
locations. Municipal ordinances barring ﬁrearms at polls are legally
impossible under current state law.

Wyoming is \*\*the only state in this batch where voter intimidation is
exclusively a felony\*\*. W.S. 22-26-111 criminalizes inducing fear in
electors through threats of force, violence, harm, or economic/oﬃcial
retaliation for the purpose of impeding voting, with penalties of up to
\*\*5 years imprisonment and/or \$10,000 ﬁne\*\*. Notably, \*\*W.S.
22-26-121\*\* provides that any aggrieved qualiﬁed elector may ﬁle a
complaint with the SOS or district attorney, and if they refuse to act,
with the Attorney General — a \*\*quasi-private right of action\*\*
pathway unique in this batch.

\### 4. Legal strategies and constitutional protections

\*\*Wyoming did not join the 19-state lawsuit.\*\* Wyoming's 2025
legislative agenda was explicitly aligned with EO 14248's goals.
Attorney General \*\*Keith G. Kautz (R)\*\* was appointed by Governor
Mark Gordon effective July 7, 2025, succeeding Bridget Hill (now on the
WY Supreme Court). Wyoming is one of only 7 states where the AG is
\*\*appointed rather than elected\*\*. HB 102 (2025) to make the AG an
elected position passed the House Judiciary Committee but did not become
law. Active litigation includes \*Frank v. Lee\* (buffer zone challenge)
in the District of Wyoming.

\*\*Wyoming has no state voting rights act.\*\* It is exempt from NVRA
motor-voter requirements and HAVA provisional ballot requirements due to
same-day voter registration.

\## Implications for municipal ordinance strategy under 18 U.S.C. § 592

\### The core challenge is more severe than in any other region

This batch represents the \*\*hardest possible terrain\*\* for municipal
ordinance strategies enforcing the federal ban on armed troops at
polling places. The convergence of constitutional carry, comprehensive
ﬁrearms preemption, weakened home rule, and aligned Republican
governance creates legal and political barriers that are qualitatively
different from the Northeast batch. \*\*18 U.S.C. § 592 itself only
addresses federal military and civil service personnel\*\*, not private
armed citizens — meaning municipal ordinances must operate in the gap
between federal law and state ﬁrearms permissiveness, a gap these states
have systematically closed.

\### Priority targets where narrow pathways exist

\*\*Missouri ranks ﬁrst\*\* as the most viable target. It is the only
state in the batch with an existing statutory polling place ﬁrearms
restriction (RSMo 571.215(2)), the nation's oldest home rule tradition
(32+ charter cities), and a narrow but real municipal exception for
regulating open carry (RSMo 21.750(3)(1)). The recommended approach is
to frame ordinances as election security measures under existing
government-building authority (RSMo 571.107), push the pending 50-foot
buffer zone expansion, and act before HB 726/SB 74 eliminates the open
carry regulation exception. Kansas City, St. Louis, Springﬁeld, and
Columbia are priority municipalities.

\*\*North Dakota ranks second\*\* due to the unexpected opening created
by \*\*HB 1588 (2025)\*\*, which allows local governments to prohibit
ﬁrearms in "public areas of publicly owned and operated buildings." Home
rule cities like Bismarck and Fargo could pass ordinances applying this
authority to publicly owned polling locations. The \$100 noncriminal
penalty is weak but establishes a legal framework. This pathway has not
been tested in court and should be pursued promptly before legislative
correction.

\*\*Montana ranks third\*\* thanks to its 43 self-governing
municipalities and the Missoula Ordinance 3618 precedent. The Article
XI, Section 6 self-governing authority argument remains legally viable
despite LR-130 and HB 102. Montana Constitution Article II, Section 13 —
"no power, civil or military, shall at any time interfere" with suffrage
— provides a uniquely powerful constitutional hook that directly
parallels 18 U.S.C. § 592. Missoula, Billings, Great Falls, Helena, and
Bozeman are priority targets, but expect aggressive legal challenge from
AG Knudsen.

\*\*Idaho, South Dakota, and Wyoming\*\* present near-insurmountable
barriers. Idaho's Dillon's Rule governance and "wholly occupies the
ﬁeld" preemption language, South Dakota's Dillon's Rule status combined
with AG-enforced preemption and automatic nulliﬁcation, and Wyoming's
comprehensive preemption plus HB 172's expansion of gun-free zone repeal
leave virtually no legal space for municipal action.

\### Alternative strategies for hostile-terrain states

Where direct ﬁrearms ordinances are impossible, the organization should
pursue:

\- \*\*Property-based authority\*\*: Municipalities in several states
can prohibit ﬁrearms in government-owned buildings used as polling
places (Missouri RSMo 571.107; North Dakota HB 1588) — though typically
with non-criminal enforcement only (denial of entry or order to leave).

\- \*\*Resolutions endorsing federal enforcement\*\*: Non-binding
municipal resolutions supporting 18 U.S.C. § 592 enforcement face no

preemption barriers and build political groundwork.

\- \*\*Buffer zone advocacy\*\*: Push state legislatures to expand
electioneering-free zones (Missouri's pending 25→50 ft expansion;
Idaho's successful 100→250 ft expansion provides a model).

\- \*\*Voter intimidation enforcement\*\*: Wyoming's felony intimidation
statute (W.S. 22-26-111) and citizen complaint mechanism (W.S. 22-

26-121) provide tools that don't require ﬁrearms-speciﬁc ordinances.
South Dakota's \*Daschle v. Thune\* precedent validates federal
intimidation claims.

\- \*\*Polling place location strategy\*\*: Advocate for locating
polling places in schools and other prohibited-carry locations where
existing state law effectively creates de facto ﬁrearms bans.

\### Coalition opportunities are limited but real

Traditional election protection coalitions are thin in these states.
However, several points of leverage exist. \*\*Native American voting
rights organizations\*\* — particularly active in Montana (Western
Native Voice, NARF), South Dakota (\*Daschle v. Thune\* legacy), and
North Dakota (Brakebill v. Jaeger) — have demonstrated litigation
capacity and grassroots networks. \*\*University communities\*\* in
self-governing cities (Missoula, Bozeman, Fargo, Bismarck) provide
activist bases. \*\*Nonpartisan election oﬃcials\*\* — many county
clerks in these states prioritize orderly elections regardless of party
— may quietly support polling place security measures framed as public
safety rather than gun control.

\### How this batch compares to the Northeast

The contrast is stark. Northeast states typically feature strong home
rule authority, limited or no ﬁrearms preemption, existing polling place
ﬁrearms bans, Democratic AGs willing to enforce voting rights,
state-level voting rights acts, and private rights of action. This batch
has \*\*none of these features across any state\*\* (with Missouri
providing partial exceptions). The strategic implication is that the
Plains and Mountain West require a fundamentally different approach:
state legislative advocacy, federal enforcement pressure, creative use
of property-based authority, and long-term coalition building rather
than the municipal ordinance model that works in the Northeast. The
organization should treat these six states as a \*\*long-term engagement
zone\*\* requiring sustained advocacy infrastructure rather than

near-term ordinance wins — with the narrow exception of Missouri, North
Dakota (HB 1588 pathway), and Montana (self-governing charter cities),
where immediate but legally contested action is possible.
