\# Four-state election protection ordinance campaign: legal and political analysis

\*\*Kansas, Missouri, Nebraska, and Iowa each offer viable municipal pathways to enforce 18 U.S.C. § 592 through local ordinances prohibiting city resources from assisting armed federal personnel near pol ing places — but the strategic landscape varies sharply.\*\*

Kansas and Missouri rank as Tier 2 \(Moderate viability\) with strong constitutional home rule and progressive urban targets, while Nebraska and Iowa rank as Tier 3 \(Hostile but worth attempting\) due to aggressive preemption environments. The strongest single target across al four states is the Unified Government of Wyandotte County/Kansas City, Kansas, whose consolidated city-county structure al ows one ordinance to cover an entire county under constitutional home rule authority. The critical legal framing across al states: this ordinance \*enforces\* existing federal law rather than obstructing it, distinguishing it from every sanctuary-type measure that has faced legal chal enge.

## \# KANSAS

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Kansas possesses one of the \*\*strongest home rule frameworks in the nation\*\*. Article 12, Section 5 of the Kansas Constitution \(adopted 1961\) empowers cities to "determine their local affairs and government including the levying of taxes, excises, fees, charges and other exactions." \[KLRD\]\(https://klrd.gov/publications/briefing-book-2021/home-rule/\) The critical clause in Art. 12, §5\(d\) mandates that "\[p\]owers and authority granted cities pursuant to this section shal be liberal y construed for the purpose of giving to cities the largest measure of self-government." \[Kansas Revisor\]\(https://ksrevisor.gov/kanconst/093\_012\_0005.html\) This language is self-executing and constitutional y entrenched — the legislature cannot override it by ordinary statute. 

Kansas operates as \*\*"Dil on's Rule in reverse"\*\* for cities. After the 1961 amendment, municipalities no longer depend on specific enabling acts. Under K.S.A. 12-101, cities can act on any subject where no state law exists \(via ordinary ordinance\), enact additional non-conflicting regulations compatible with uniform state law, and opt out of non-uniform state laws by charter ordinance. \[KLRD\]

\(https://klrd.gov/publications/briefing-book-2021/home-rule/\) Cities face only three constitutional constraints: \(a\) enactments of statewide concern applicable uniformly to al cities; \(b\) uniform enactments limiting taxes by city class; and \(c\) enactments prescribing debt limits. \[Washburnlaw\]\(https://ksag.washburnlaw.edu/opinions/2017/2017-019.pdf\) County home rule derives from \*\*statute\*\* \(K.S.A. 19-101a, enacted 1974\), not the constitution. This distinction matters: city home rule is constitutional y entrenched while county home rule can be modified by the legislature. 

\*\*Key case law\*\* strongly supports expansive local authority. In \*Blevins v. Hiebert\*, 247 Kan. 1, 795 P.2d 325 \(1990\), the Kansas Supreme Court outlined four categories of home rule power: taxation, police power regulation, opting out of non-uniform laws via charter ordinance, and acting where no state law exists. In \*City of Junction City v. Lee\*, 216 Kan. 495, 532 P.2d 1292 \(1975\), the court upheld a city ordinance that went \*further\* than state law — the court held no conflict exists because the local law did not "authorize what state law prohibits or prohibit what state law authorizes." The conflict test from \*Missouri Pacific Railroad v. Board of Greeley County Comm'rs\*, 231 Kan. 225, confirms that conflict requires the local law to permit what the state prohibits or vice versa. \[Justia\]

\(https://law.justia.com/cases/kansas/supreme-court/1989/62-450-3-0.html\)

The proposed ordinance fal s squarely within \*\*police power and public safety regulation\*\* — the most historical y protected area of local authority. It does not regulate elections themselves but rather protects voter safety and restricts deployment of city resources. 

\#\# 2. Preemption landscape

Kansas election law \(K.S.A. Chapter 25\) contains no express preemption of city authority on election security matters. The most relevant preemption provision is \*\*K.S.A. 19-101a\(a\)\(6\)\*\*, which states: "Counties shal be subject to al acts of the legislature concerning elections, election commissioners and officers and their duties as such officers and the election of county officers." \[LawServer\]

\(https://www.lawserver.com/law/state/kansas/ks-statutes/kansas\_statutes\_19-101a\) This is an \*\*express preemption of county home rule on election matters\*\* — but it applies only to counties, not cities. The absence of equivalent city-level preemption is legal y significant. 

The strongest preemption analog is \*\*firearms preemption under K.S.A. 12-16,124\*\*, which provides total field preemption: "No city or county shal adopt or enforce any ordinance, resolution or regulation... governing the requirement of fees, licenses or permits for, the commerce in or the sale, purchase, transfer, ownership, storage, carrying, transporting or taxation of firearms or ammunition." 

\[Uslawshield\]\(https://gunlaws.uslawshield.com/states/kansas/\) The legislature's choice to enact express firearms preemption but not equivalent election-security preemption for cities creates a strong negative-inference argument — the legislature preempts when it intends to, and its silence on city election-security measures signals permissiveness. 

Kansas does \*\*not\*\* currently have a general anti-sanctuary preemption law. However, \*\*SB 5 \(2025\)\*\* amended K.S.A. 25-2436 to prohibit acceptance of federal election funds without legislative approval, \[Bil Track50\]\(https://www.bil track50.com/bil detail/1781454\) signaling growing hostility to local-federal election cooperation. AG Opinion No. 2017-19 \(December 28, 2017\) provides favorable precedent, concluding that cities and counties may enact ordinances going beyond state law where state law does not expressly preempt, applying the \*Junction City v. Lee\* conflict test. \[Washburnlaw\]\(https://ksag.washburnlaw.edu/opinions/2017/2017-019.pdf\)

\*\*Statutes that could be weaponized\*\*: K.S.A. 12-16,124 \(if the ordinance is characterized as firearms regulation\); K.S.A. 25-125

\(restricting modifications to election laws, though this targets the executive and judicial branches, not municipalities\); \[Kansas Revisor\]

\(https://ksrevisor.gov/statutes/chapters/ch25/025\_001\_0025.html\) and the SB 5 amendment to K.S.A. 25-2436. 

\#\# 3. Constitutional basis for the ordinance

The ordinance's constitutional foundation rests on the anti-commandeering doctrine established in \*Printz v. United States\*, 521 U.S. 898

\(1997\), which held the federal government cannot "command the States' officers, or those of their political subdivisions, to administer or enforce a federal regulatory program," \[Legal Information Institute \+2\]\(https://www.law.cornel .edu/constitution-conan/amendment-10/anti-commandeering-doctrine\) and extended in \*Murphy v. NCAA\*, 584 U.S. 453 \(2018\). The ordinance should be framed not as an assertion of Tenth Amendment rights \(municipalities lack independent standing to invoke the Tenth Amendment in federal court\) but as a \*\*local resource al ocation decision\*\* — the city exercising its constitutional y granted home rule authority to manage its own affairs. 

\*\*18 U.S.C. § 592\*\* provides an unambiguous federal statutory foundation: any person in the civil, military, or naval service who "orders, brings, keeps, or has under his authority or control any troops or armed men at any place where a general or special election is held" 

faces up to five years' imprisonment. \[Legal Information Institute \+3\]\(https://www.law.cornel .edu/uscode/text/18/592\) The Brennan Center for Justice has confirmed this prohibition extends to al armed federal law enforcement, not just military. \[Brennan Center for Justice \+2\]\(https://www.brennancenter.org/our-work/research-reports/federal-laws-protecting-against-intimidation-voters-and-election-workers\) The ordinance can therefore be framed as \*\*local enforcement supporting existing federal law\*\* — a dispositive distinction from sanctuary-type measures that resist federal enforcement. 

Kansas constitutional provisions further strengthen the ordinance: Art. 12, §5\(d\)'s liberal construction clause; \[Kansas Revisor\]

\(https://ksrevisor.gov/kanconst/093\_012\_0005.html\) Art. 5, §1's establishment of a state constitutional right to vote; and the Bil of Rights, §11, whose free speech protections Kansas courts have found at least as protective as the First Amendment \(see \*LWV Kansas v. Schwab\*\).

## \# MISSOURI

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Missouri's home rule tradition dates to 1875 — among the oldest in the nation. \[Washington University\]

\(https://openscholarship.wustl.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=3603&context=law\_lawreview\) \*\*Mo. Const. Art. VI, § 19\(a\)\*\* provides charter cities with "al powers which the general assembly of the state of Missouri has authority to confer upon any city, provided such powers are consistent with the constitution of this state and are not limited or denied either by the charter so adopted or by statute." 

\[Missouri Revisor of Statutes\]\(https://revisor.mo.gov/main/OneSection.aspx?constit=y&section=VI\+\+19\(a\)\) This is an exceptional y broad grant. Any city with \*\*5,000\+ population\*\* may adopt a charter \[Tripod\]

\(https://nocofoshojrvbc.tripod.com/sitebuildercontent/sitebuilderfiles/modelchartertbbridgeton.pdf\) under Mo. Rev. Stat. § 82.020. 

\*\*St. Louis City\*\* holds unique status as an \*\*independent city\*\* since 1876 — not part of any county, one of only three independent cities in the United States outside Virginia. \[Wikipedia\]\(https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List\_of\_counties\_in\_Missouri\) St. Louis operates as both a city and county-equivalent entity, \[City of St. Louis\]\(https://www.stlouis-mo.gov/government/about/city-government-structure.cfm\) \[Wustl\]\(https://journals.library.wustl.edu/lawreview/article/4557/gal ey/21390/view/\) performing circuit court functions, election administration, and revenue col ection. Its Board of Aldermen \(14 aldermen plus president\) serves as the legislative body. This dual authority gives St. Louis broader regulatory scope and fewer jurisdictional overlaps. 

\*\*Kansas City\*\* is a charter city under Art. VI, § 19\(a\), with a 13-member City Council \(6 district, 6 at-large, plus mayor\). However, Kansas City faces a \*\*fundamental limitation\*\*: the Kansas City Police Department is uniquely control ed by a \*\*state-appointed Board of Police

Commissioners\*\* — the city does not ful y control its own police department, creating a significant obstacle for an ordinance directing police non-cooperation. 

Key case law supports broad home rule. \*City of Cape Girardeau v. Joyce\*, 884 S.W.2d 33 \(Mo. App. 1994\), affirmed that home rule cities have broad authority to act even in areas touching on state interests. \*Enright v. Kansas City\*, 536 S.W.2d 17 \(Mo. banc 1976\), confirmed the expansive reading of § 19\(a\). \[Missouri Revisor of Statutes\]\(https://revisor.mo.gov/main/OneSection.aspx? 

constit=y&section=VI\+\+19\(a\)\) Under the "statewide vs. local concern" test from \*Flower Val ey Shopping Center, Inc. v. St. Louis County\*, the ordinance should be characterized as governing municipal resource al ocation \(traditional local concern\) rather than election administration \(state concern\). 

\#\# 2. Preemption landscape

Missouri's Republican-dominated legislature has engaged in \*\*extensive preemption of local progressive ordinances\*\*: minimum wage \(2017, after St. Louis's 2015 increase\); \[The Beacon \+2\]\(https://thebeaconnews.org/stories/2024/05/07/missouri-preemption-kansas-city-minimum-wage/\) plastic bag bans \(2015\); paid sick leave repeal \(2025, overriding a voter-approved measure\); and pending housing protection preemption. This pattern establishes that if this ordinance gains traction, retroactive preemption should be anticipated. 

\*\*Firearms preemption \(Mo. Rev. Stat. § 21.750\)\*\* provides the most aggressive analog: the legislature "occupies and preempts the entire field of legislation touching in any way firearms, components, ammunition and supplies to the complete exclusion of any order, ordinance or regulation by any political subdivision." \[GIFFORDS\]\(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-missouri/\) \[Missouri Revisor of Statutes\]\(https://revisor.mo.gov/main/OneSection.aspx?section=21.750\) The critical distinction: \*\*no such comprehensive field preemption language exists for election-related municipal resource al ocation\*\*. 

\*\*Missouri's Second Amendment Preservation Act \(SAPA\)\*\* is the most instructive precedent. SAPA \(Mo. Rev. Stat. §§ 1.410-1.485, enacted 2021\) penalized local law enforcement for enforcing federal gun laws with $50,000 fines. In \*U.S. v. Missouri\* \(W.D. Mo. 2023\), Judge Brian Wimes struck down SAPA as unconstitutional "interposition" violating the Supremacy Clause. The \*\*Eighth Circuit unanimously affirmed\*\* \(August 2024\), and the \*\*Supreme Court declined certiorari\*\* \(October 2025\). SAPA failed because it \(1\) declared federal laws "invalid" and \(2\) penalized cooperation with federal enforcement. The proposed election protection ordinance avoids both errors — it \*enforces\* federal law \(18 U.S.C. § 592\) and does not declare any federal statute invalid. 

The anti-sanctuary statute \*\*Mo. Rev. Stat. § 67.307\*\* \(enacted 2019\) prohibits municipalities from adopting policies limiting cooperation with federal immigration authorities, with state grant eligibility penalties. However, § 67.307 is \*\*immigration-specific\*\* and does not broadly prohibit refusal to cooperate with al federal agencies. The election protection ordinance's enforcement-of-federal-law framing further distinguishes it. 

\*\*Chapter 115 \(Mo. Rev. Stat. §§ 115.001-115.970\)\*\* governs election administration comprehensively but does not address municipal decisions about deploying police resources in coordination with federal agencies. Section 115.007's presumption against implied repeal

\[Justia\]\(https://law.justia.com/codes/missouri/2022/title-ix/chapter-115/\) suggests the legislature did not intend to preempt al election-related matters.

## \# NEBRASKA

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Nebraska operates as a \*\*Dil on's Rule state with constitutional home rule exceptions\*\*. Article XI, § 2 of the Nebraska Constitution al ows any city with more than \*\*5,000 inhabitants\*\* to frame a home rule charter "consistent with and subject to the constitution and laws of \[Nebraska Legislature\]\(https://nebraskalegislature.gov/laws/articles.php?article=XI-5&print=true\) this state." Art. XI, § 5

specifical y addresses cities with more than 100,000 inhabitants. \[Justia\]

\(https://law.justia.com/constitution/nebraska/c0111002000.html\) Only \*\*Omaha and Lincoln\*\* qualify as home rule cities in practice. 

Nebraska's municipal classification system is population-based and codified in statute: cities of the metropolitan class \(400,000\+, only Omaha, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 14-101 et seq.\); cities of the primary class \(100,000-300,000, only Lincoln, Neb. Rev. Stat. § 15-101 et seq.\); 

\[Justia\]\(https://law.justia.com/codes/nebraska/2006/s15index/s1501001000.html\) cities of the first class \(5,000-100,000, § 16-101 et seq.\); cities of the second class \(800-5,000, § 17-101 et seq.\); and vil ages \(under 800, § 17-201 et seq.\). Omaha adopted its metropolitan class home rule charter in 1922; Lincoln operates under a primary class home rule charter. 

The critical legal distinction in Nebraska is between \*\*"statewide concern"\*\* and \*\*"strictly municipal concern."\*\* In \*State v. Albarenga\*, 313 Neb. 72, 892 N.W.2d 799 \(2022\), the Nebraska Supreme Court held that "where a municipality has constitutional y conferred powers to form a charter and enact ordinances, the state law is the superior law only as to matters of statewide concern." In \*State ex rel. City of Omaha v. Lynch\*, 181 Neb. 810, 151 N.W.2d 278 \(1967\), the court found that home rule renders a city "independent of state legislation as to al subjects which are of strictly municipal concern." However, \*Nagle v. City of Grand Island\*, 144 Neb. 67, 12 N.W.2d 540 \(1943\), confirmed that "general law of statewide concern takes precedence over conflicting provision of city home rule charter." 

Election administration is \*\*almost certainly a matter of statewide concern\*\* under Nebraska case law, creating significant risk. The ordinance must be careful y framed as a public safety and resource al ocation measure rather than election regulation.

## \# IOWA

\#\# 1. Home rule authority

Iowa has \*\*strong constitutional home rule\*\* that explicitly repudiates Dil on's Rule. \*\*Article III, § 38A\*\* of the Iowa Constitution \(1968

Home Rule Amendment\) grants municipal corporations "home rule power and authority, not inconsistent with the laws of the general assembly, to determine their local affairs and government." The provision's final sentence is critical: "The rule or proposition of law that a municipal corporation possesses and can exercise only those powers granted in express words is not a part of the law of this state." This language \*\*affirmatively rejects Dil on's Rule\*\*. 

\*\*Iowa Code § 364.1\*\* implements this authority with extraordinarily broad language: cities may "exercise any power and perform any function it deems appropriate to protect and preserve the rights, privileges, and property of the city or of its residents, and to preserve and improve the peace, safety, health, welfare, comfort, and convenience of its residents." Iowa Code § 364.3 enumerates specific areas where cities cannot act — \*\*election administration is NOT listed\*\* as a prohibited area. 

Counties also have constitutional home rule under \*\*Art. III, § 39A\*\*, implemented through Iowa Code § 331.301 with nearly identical broad language. If county and municipal authority conflict, \*\*municipal power prevails\*\* within its jurisdiction. 

Key case law establishes the preemption framework. \*City of Des Moines v. Gruen\*, 457 N.W.2d 340 \(Iowa 1990\), held that a municipal ordinance is "inconsistent" with state law \(and thus preempted\) only when it "prohibits an act permitted by a statute, or permits an act prohibited by a statute" or when it "invades an area of law reserved by the legislature to itself." Statutes and ordinances must be

\*\*harmonized where possible\*\* under Iowa Code § 364.2\(3\). \*Green v. City of Cascade\*, 231 N.W.2d 882 \(Iowa 1975\), confirmed that if statute and ordinance cannot be reconciled, the statute prevails. 

\#\# 2. Preemption landscape

Iowa's preemption environment is the \*\*most aggressively hostile\*\* of the four states. The legislature has demonstrated a pattern of rapid, retroactive preemption of progressive local ordinances:

\*\*Iowa Code Chapter 27A \(2018\)\*\* — the anti-sanctuary statute — is the most dangerous analog. Section 27A.4\(1\) provides that "a local entity shal not adopt or enforce a policy or take any other action under which the local entity prohibits or discourages the enforcement of immigration laws." Section 27A.4\(2\) prohibits local entities from preventing officers from inquiring about immigration status or cooperating with ICE. Section 27A.9 authorizes denial of state funds for noncompliant entities. While immigration-specific, \*\*opponents wil argue Chapter 27A's principles extend to any local policy limiting cooperation with federal law enforcement\*\*. 

\*\*HF 295 \(2017\)\*\* retroactively voided local minimum wage ordinances in Johnson, Linn, Polk, and Wapel o counties — demonstrating the legislature's wil ingness to nul ify existing progressive ordinances. \*\*HF 954 \(2025\)\*\* banned ranked-choice voting, another example of election-specific preemption. \*\*SF 2340 \(2024\)\*\* made it a state crime for certain immigrants to live in Iowa \(currently blocked by the 8th Circuit as unconstitutional federal preemption under \*Arizona v. United States\*\). 

Iowa election law \(Title II, Chapters 39-56\) is comprehensive. Section 331.383 assigns county auditors as commissioners of elections. 

The election protection ordinance does not regulate election procedure, voter qualifications, or election administration — it regulates the use of city resources and armed personnel presence near pol ing places, a public safety measure. 

\*\*AG Brenna Bird\*\* is the most aggressive institutional opponent across al four states. In the 2025 Winneshiek County case, she investigated and sued Sheriff Dan Marx for a Facebook post merely questioning ICE detainer compliance. An actual ordinance restricting cooperation with armed federal personnel would \*\*almost certainly draw immediate AG action\*\*.

\*\*Iowa Constitution, Art. II, § 1\*\* \(Right of Suffrage\) and \*\*Art.
