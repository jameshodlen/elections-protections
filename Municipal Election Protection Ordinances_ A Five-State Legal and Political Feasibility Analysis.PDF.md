\# Municipal election protection ordinances: a ﬁve-state legal and
political feasibility analysis

\*\*Georgia and Kentucky offer the strongest legal pathways for
municipal ordinances enforcing 18 U.S.C. § 592's ban on armed federal
personnel at polling places, while Mississippi is functionally
non-viable and Arkansas and West Virginia fall in between.\*\* The
critical legal obstacle in every state is ﬁrearms preemption—each of the
ﬁve states has a broad ﬁrearms preemption statute that opponents will
invoke, making careful ordinance drafting the single most important
determinant of legal survival. However, a powerful counter-framing
exists: Arkansas, West Virginia, and (to a lesser extent) other states
have themselves adopted anti-commandeering laws refusing to assist
federal ﬁrearms enforcement—the exact constitutional principle this
ordinance invokes. The campaign should launch in Athens-Clarke County,
Georgia, and Louisville Metro, Kentucky, then expand outward in
coordinated waves.

\## 1. Georgia — the strongest launch state

\### Home rule authority provides a real foundation

Georgia's home rule system offers the strongest legal platform among the
ﬁve states, particularly for consolidated governments. \*\*Article IX,
Section II of the Georgia Constitution\*\* grants home rule to both
counties \[Ballotpedia\]
(https://ballotpedia.org/Article_IX,\_Georgia_Constitution) (Paragraph
I) and municipalities (Paragraph II). County home rule is directly
constitutional and includes a powerful "second-tier" delegation allowing
counties to amend or repeal local acts. \[Squarespace\]
(https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5ce4377caeb1ce00013a02fd/t/5f73762dc5be9d788d50f4e7/1601402414100/50States-Georgia.pdf)
Municipal home rule is delegated through the \*\*Municipal Home Rule Act
of 1965\*\* (O.C.G.A. §§ 36-35-1 through 36-35-8), which grants
municipalities power to adopt "clearly reasonable ordinances,
resolutions, or regulations relating to its property, affairs, and local
government" not inconsistent with the Constitution or general law
(O.C.G.A. § 36-35-3). \[Georgia Attorney General\]
(https://law.georgia.gov/opinions/94-4-0)
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2022/title-36/chapter-35/section-36-35-3/)

The critical limitation is \*\*O.C.G.A. § 36-35-6\*\*, which excludes
certain matters from home rule powers, including actions "affecting
elections." Whether a resource-allocation ordinance about federal
personnel near polling places constitutes an action "affecting
elections" under § 36-35-6 will be the central legal question. The
ordinance must be framed as a public safety and resource allocation
measure—not election administration.

\*\*Counties hold stronger home rule authority than municipalities\*\*
in Georgia. The constitutional second-tier delegation gives county
ordinances greater legal durability. \[Squarespace\]
(https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5ce4377caeb1ce00013a02fd/t/5f73762dc5be9d788d50f4e7/1601402414100/50States-Georgia.pdf)
Consolidated city-county governments like Athens-Clarke County exercise
both municipal and county powers, creating the most robust legal
position.

\### Preemption landscape requires surgical drafting

\*\*Firearms preemption (O.C.G.A. § 16-11-173)\*\* is the most dangerous
legal obstacle. It provides that no county or municipality shall
regulate "possession, ownership, transport, carrying, transfer, sale,
purchase, licensing, or registration of ﬁrearms." \[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-georgia/)
However, a critical carve-out in \*\*§ 16-11-173(c)(1)\*\* allows local
governments to regulate transport, carrying, or possession of ﬁrearms by
their own employees in the course of employment.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/georgia/2014/title-16/chapter-11/article-4/part-5/section-16-11-173)
This exception supports an ordinance directing city employees not to
assist armed federal personnel.

\*\*SB 202 (Election Integrity Act of 2021)\*\* centralized election
authority at the state level and gave the State Election Board power to
suspend local election superintendents. \[Election
Integrity\](https://www.election-integrity.org/sb-202) However, SB 202
addresses election administration procedures—not resource allocation
regarding federal law enforcement. The AG's oﬃce secured a major 11th
Circuit victory in January 2026 upholding SB 202's key provisions,
\[Georgia State
Bar\](https://law.georgia.gov/press-releases/2026-01-27/carr-secures-another-victory-defense-georgias-election-integrity-act)
making Georgia's election enforcement apparatus conﬁdent and aggressive.
Anti-sanctuary city provisions under \*\*O.C.G.A. § 36-80-23\*\*
prohibit immigration sanctuary policies,
\[Americanpoliceoﬃcersalliance\](https://americanpoliceoﬃcersalliance.com/states-who-have-banned-sanctuary-cities/)

\## 2. Kentucky — consolidated governments create real opportunity

\### Home rule is statutory but substantive for Louisville

Kentucky's home rule derives from \*\*Section 156b of the Kentucky
Constitution\*\* (ratiﬁed 1994), which authorizes—but does not require
—the General Assembly to grant cities broad powers. The enabling
statute, \*\*KRS 82.082\*\*, provides that a city may "exercise any
power and perform any function within its boundaries... that is in
furtherance of a public purpose of the city and not in conﬂict with a
constitutional provision or statute."
\[Klc\](https://www.klc.org/news/475/What_is_home_rule) Under \*\*KRS
82.082(2)\*\*, conﬂict exists where a power is "expressly prohibited by
a statute" or "there is a comprehensive scheme of legislation on the
same general subject." \[Kyopengov
+2\](https://kyopengov.org/law/ag/2021/oag-21-10) Louisville holds
unique status as a First Class city with "complete home rule" under
\*\*KRS 83.410(4)\*\*, which the statute describes as necessary due to
Louisville being "suﬃciently different from those found in other
cities."
\[Findlaw\](https://caselaw.ﬁndlaw.com/court/ky-supreme-court/1754765.html)

The three-part test for "comprehensive scheme" preemption from
\*Commonwealth v. Do, Inc.\*, 674 S.W.2d 519 (Ky. 1984), asks whether
the subject is (1) fully covered indicating exclusive state concern, (2)
partially covered in terms indicating paramount state concern, or (3)
legislation addresses the same general subject.
\[Kyopengov\](https://kyopengov.org/law/ag/2017/oag-17-028) The Kentucky
Supreme Court applied this test to strike down Louisville's minimum wage
ordinance in \*Kentucky Restaurant Association v. Louisville-Jefferson

County Metro Government\* (Ky. 2016), holding 6-1 that comprehensive
state wage legislation preempted local action. \[Lexology\]
(https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=7ef173ae-c85a-4608-bcb9-44b3b71631e4)

\### Firearms preemption carries criminal penalties

\*\*KRS 65.870\*\* contains one of the most aggressive ﬁrearms
preemption regimes nationally. \[Kyopengov\]
(https://kyopengov.org/law/ag/1993/oag-93-071) It occupies "any part of
the ﬁeld of regulation of the manufacture, sale, purchase, taxation,
transfer, ownership, possession, carrying, storage, or transportation of
ﬁrearms, ammunition, components of ﬁrearms..."
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-kentucky/)
\[Uslawshield\] (https://gunlaws.uslawshield.com/states/kentucky/) The
statute includes criminal liability: public servants who violate "the
spirit thereof" face \*\*Oﬃcial Misconduct in the ﬁrst or second
degree\*\* (Class A misdemeanor with up to 12 months imprisonment).
Private parties can bring suit for declaratory/injunctive relief with
attorney fee recovery.
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-

of-local-laws-in-kentucky/) \*\*OAG 93-071\*\* conﬁrmed that ﬁrearms
preemption trumps even Louisville's ﬁrst-class city complete home rule.
\[Kyopengov\](https://kyopengov.org/law/ag/2021/oag-21-10)

\## 3. Arkansas — hostile environment but constitutional text is an
asset

\### Statutory home rule explicitly abrogates Dillon's Rule

Arkansas provides municipalities statutory (not constitutional) home
rule through \*\*Ark. Code Ann. § 14-43-601 et seq.\*\* Section \*\*§
14-43-602\*\* expressly states that "the rule of decision known as
'Dillon's Rule' is inapplicable to the municipal affairs of
municipalities." Cities may exercise "any function" and "full
legislative power" on municipal affairs.
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Arkansas.pdf)
However, the Arkansas Supreme Court in \*Paragould Cablevision v. City
of Paragould\*, 809 S.W.2d 688 (Ark. 1991), characterized this as a
"limited home rule statute" that the legislature can withdraw at
will—unlike constitutional home rule.
\[Squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Arkansas.pdf)
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Arkansas.pdf)
The \*\*police power exception\*\* in § 14-43-601(a)(1)(J), which
reserves "minimum public health, pollution, and safety standards" to the
state,
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Arkansas.pdf)
creates ambiguity for election security ordinances.

Counties have stronger constitutional home rule under \*\*Amendment 55
(1974)\*\*, which allows counties acting through Quorum Courts to
exercise "local legislative authority not denied by the Constitution or
by law." \[Encyclopedia of Arkansas\]
(https://encyclopediaofarkansas.net/entries/arkansas-plan-2345/)
\[squarespace\](https://turquoise-red-rdag.squarespace.com/s/Arkansas.pdf)
Since election administration is traditionally a county function in
Arkansas, a county-level ordinance may have a stronger legal basis.

\### The state's own anti-commandeering law is a powerful precedent

\*\*Arkansas's Article 3, § 2\*\* contains remarkably favorable text:
"Elections shall be free and equal. \*\*No power, civil or military,
shall ever interfere to prevent the free exercise of the right of
suffrage.\*\*" \[Ballotpedia
+2\](https://ballotpedia.org/Arkansas_Constitution) This directly
parallels 18 U.S.C. § 592. More importantly, Arkansas itself passed
\*\*HB 1957 / Act 1023 of 2021\*\*, the "Arkansas Sovereignty Act,"
which invokes anti-commandeering principles to prohibit state and local
law enforcement from enforcing certain federal ﬁrearms laws. \[Bearing
Arms\](https://bearingarms.com/camedwards/2021/04/27/arkansas-senate-votes-to-overridego-governors-veto-of-2a-sanctuary-bill-n44312)
\[Uslawshield\](https://gunlaws.uslawshield.com/states/arkansas/) This
creates a powerful rhetorical and legal

precedent: if the state can refuse to cooperate with federal ﬁrearms
enforcement under anti-commandeering principles, cities can invoke the
same principle to refuse to assist armed federal personnel who violate
18 U.S.C. § 592.

Firearms preemption under \*\*Ark. Code Ann. § 14-54-1411\*\* is broad
\[GIFFORDS\](https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-arkansas/)
and was recently expanded by the court in \*Eureka Gun & Pawn, LLC v.
City of Eureka Springs\* (Ark. Sup. Ct., Jan. 2026).
\[Reason.com\](https://reason.com/volokh/2026/01/16/arkansas-case-on-zoning-laws-being-used-to-exclude-gun-shops/)
\*\*Act 137 of 2015\*\*
\[ThinkProgress\](https://thinkprogress.org/arkansas-lawmaker-wants-to-block-all-local-civil-rights-ordinances-8f0d40c905fb/)
preempted Fayetteville's civil rights ordinance, and the Arkansas
Supreme Court unanimously upheld the preemption in \*Protect
Fayetteville v. City of Fayetteville\*, 2017 Ark. 49.
\[Artleonardobservations\](https://www.artleonardobservations.com/tag/anti-discrimination-ordinances/)

\## Part III: Campaign rollout strategy

\### Three-wave deployment maximizes momentum and minimizes risk

\*\*Wave 1 (April–June 2026): Strongest legal foundations.\*\* Launch
simultaneously in Athens-Clarke County, Georgia, and Louisville Metro,
Kentucky. These consolidated governments have the strongest home rule
authority and progressive political infrastructure. Timing after the
Georgia legislature's April 6 adjournment and the Kentucky legislature's
likely spring adjournment minimizes immediate preemption risk. Build the
national narrative around "municipalities enforcing existing federal
law" rather than "local resistance."

\*\*Wave 2 (July–September 2026): Expansion within anchor states.\*\*
Following Wave 1 passage, expand to Atlanta and Savannah (Georgia);
Lexington-Fayette (Kentucky); and Fayetteville and Little Rock
(Arkansas). Use Wave 1 successes to demonstrate legal viability and
build coalition momentum. Deploy ACLU and NAACP aﬃliates for public
education campaigns in all target cities.

\*\*Wave 3 (October–November 2026): Maximum pre-election visibility.\*\*
Push Charleston (West Virginia) and any remaining targets. Introduce
non-binding resolutions in Mississippi (Jackson, Hattiesburg, Oxford)
for symbolic coalition-building. Time ﬁnal wave to maximize visibility
during the November 2026 election cycle, when federal election integrity
will be a prominent issue.

\### Critical ordinance drafting principles across all states

The ordinance must be built on ﬁve structural pillars to survive legal
challenge:

\- \*\*Resource allocation framing\*\*: The ordinance directs how city
resources (personnel, equipment, facilities) are deployed, not who may
carry ﬁrearms. This is a traditional local government function under
police power and home rule authority.

\- \*\*Non-assistance rather than obstruction\*\*: The ordinance
prohibits city employees from providing aﬃrmative assistance to armed
federal personnel near polling places. It does not obstruct, resist, or
impede federal activities—it simply declines to volunteer local
resources.

\- \*\*Federal law enforcement language\*\*: Every recital and operative
provision should reference 18 U.S.C. § 592 as the existing federal
prohibition the ordinance supports. The ordinance "enforces" federal
law, not "resists" it.

\- \*\*Zero ﬁrearms regulatory language\*\*: The ordinance must contain
no reference to ﬁrearms possession, carrying, transport, or ownership.
The operative term should be "armed federal personnel" as deﬁned by
reference to § 592, not "persons carrying ﬁrearms."

\- \*\*Anti-commandeering citations\*\*: Recitals should cite \*Printz
v. United States\*, \*Murphy v. NCAA\*, and—in Arkansas and West
Virginia —the state's own anti-commandeering statutes.

\### Coalition architecture should leverage state-speciﬁc strengths

Georgia's deep bench of voting rights organizations (Fair Fight, New
Georgia Project, Lawyers' Committee, SPLC, UGA First Amendment Clinic)
should lead public advocacy and pre-passage legal vetting. Kentucky's
labor unions (14 locals endorsed Mayor Greenberg) and KFTC grassroots
network should drive Louisville Metro Council vote-counting. Arkansas's
constitutional text (Art. 3, § 2) and the anti-commandeering precedent
(Act 1023) should anchor the legal narrative. West Virginia's WVU
College of Law (Prof. Bastress) should provide academic authority.
Mississippi's NAACP State Conference and Mississippi Center for Justice
should lead the non-binding resolution and federal litigation strategy.

\### Long-term litigation and defense planning

Litigation is virtually certain in every state where a binding ordinance
passes. Pre-litigation preparation should include: \*\*(1)\*\* securing
pro bono defense commitments from national organizations (ACLU National,
Brennan Center, Lawyers' Committee) before any ordinance is introduced;
\*\*(2)\*\* ﬁling aﬃrmative declaratory judgment actions in favorable
forums immediately after passage to choose the court; \*\*(3)\*\*
preparing preliminary injunction opposition briefs in advance;
\*\*(4)\*\* building amicus networks (States United Democracy Center,
National Policing Institute, and academic election law experts) to
support the ordinances in court.

The most dangerous legal scenario is \*\*ﬁrearms preemption
litigation\*\*, where conservative courts may accept opponents'
characterization of the ordinance as ﬁrearms regulation despite careful
drafting. The second most dangerous is \*\*legislative preemption\*\*,
particularly in Arkansas (January 2027 regular session), Kentucky
(Republican supermajority can override Beshear's veto), and West
Virginia (91-9 House majority). Georgia's lack of veto-proof
supermajority provides meaningful protection only until 2027, when the
next governor (potentially Republican) would sign preemptive
legislation.

\## Conclusion: viable but fragile

This campaign faces a fundamental tension: the ﬁve target states
selected for their need for election protection are also states where
conservative state governments will aggressively resist local
progressive action. \*\*Georgia and Kentucky offer genuine legal
pathways\*\* through consolidated governments with substantial home rule
authority—Athens-Clarke County and Louisville Metro are not merely
symbolic targets but legally defensible ones. \*\*Arkansas's
constitutional voting rights text and anti-commandeering precedent\*\*
provide the strongest constitutional arguments, even if the political
environment is hostile. \*\*West Virginia's own anti-commandeering
law\*\* creates the campaign's most ironic and rhetorically powerful
precedent. \*\*Mississippi should be treated as a non-binding resolution
and federal litigation venue\*\*, not an ordinance target.

The campaign's success depends on three factors: surgical ordinance
drafting that maintains the resource-allocation/non-assistance framing;
strategic timing that exploits windows between legislative sessions; and
the anti-commandeering narrative that transforms this from a progressive
"resistance" measure into an enforcement of existing federal law using
constitutionally conservative principles these states have already
embraced.
