\# Election security infrastructure across six states: a guide for
municipal ordinance strategy

\*\*Five of six states researched — Arkansas, Kentucky, Oklahoma, Texas,
and Utah — have no meaningful prohibition on ﬁrearms at polling places
(or only partial coverage in Texas's case), yet all ﬁve enforce
aggressive state preemption laws that block municipalities from ﬁlling
these gaps.\*\* This creates a paradox at the heart of any municipal
ordinance campaign to enforce 18 U.S.C. § 592: the states where armed
presence at polls poses the greatest risk are precisely the states where
local action faces the steepest legal barriers. New Mexico stands alone
in this group as a state with both strong existing protections and
friendly institutional allies. The 2025–2026 collapse of federal
election security infrastructure — CISA's effective withdrawal,
EI-ISAC's transition to paid membership, and HAVA funding's 96% decline
— makes state-level and municipal-level action more urgent than ever,
even as the legal environment in most of these states grows more
hostile. This guide provides the detailed legal, institutional, and
strategic intelligence needed to navigate that terrain for the 2026
election cycle.

\## I. The federal legal foundation: what 18 U.S.C. § 592 does and does
not do

The statute at the core of this effort — \*\*18 U.S.C. § 592\*\* —
prohibits any person in the civil, military, or naval service of the
United States from ordering, bringing, keeping, or having under their
authority any \*\*troops or armed men at any place where a general or
special election is held\*\*, unless necessary to repel armed enemies of
the United States. Penalties include ﬁnes, \*\*up to ﬁve years'
imprisonment\*\*, and permanent disqualiﬁcation from holding federal
oﬃce. \[U.S. House of Representatives\]
(https://uscode.house.gov/view.xhtml?req=granuleid:USC-prelim-title18-section592&num=0&edition=prelim)
\[Legal Information Institute\]
(https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/592) Its companion statute,
\*\*18 U.S.C. § 593\*\*, criminalizes military interference with the
voting process itself.
\[GovRegs\](https://www.govregs.com/uscode/expand/title18_partI_chapter29_section592)
\[Legal Information
Institute\](https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/18/592)

Despite its severity on paper, \*\*§ 592 has never produced a reported
conviction\*\*. The statute's enforcement depends entirely on the
federal executive branch — the same branch that could order the
deployment it prohibits. This structural vulnerability is what makes
municipal ordinances critical: they create \*\*parallel local
enforcement mechanisms\*\* that bypass the federal enforcement gap.
Judge Charles Breyer's September 2, 2025 ruling in \*Newsom v. Trump\*
rejected three Trump Administration arguments that would have disabled
judicial enforcement of §§ 592–593, calling it "ahistorical and
illogical" to leave prosecution solely to the government ordering the
interference.
\[Societyfortheruleoﬂaw\](https://societyfortheruleoﬂaw.org/trump-national-guard-threatens-elections/)

The broader federal framework includes \*\*52 U.S.C. § 10307(b)\*\*
(Voting Rights Act § 11(b), prohibiting voter intimidation with no
intent requirement), \*\*42 U.S.C. § 1985(3)\*\* (Ku Klux Klan Act,
allowing private civil enforcement against conspiracies to prevent
voting), \*\*18 U.S.C. § 594\*\* (voter intimidation, up to one year
imprisonment), and \*\*18 U.S.C. § 241\*\* (conspiracy against rights,
up to ten years). The KKK Act's private right of action under § 1985(3)
is especially signiﬁcant because it does not depend on federal
prosecution and can be brought by individual citizens in federal court.
\[Protect
Democracy\](https://protectdemocracy.org/work/revitalizing-the-ku-klux-klan-act-to-protect-voters/)

\## II. New Mexico: the strongest existing framework and a natural ally

(https://www.abqjournal.com/opinion/opinion-fully-funding-our-elections-is-essential-to-new-mexicos-future/2973757)

\[Nmml\](https://nmml.org/DocumentCenter/View/179/Chapter-28---Home-Rule-Charters-PDF)

\### The ﬁrearms gap — a deliberate legislative choice

5-73-306(7)\*\* — the list of prohibited concealed carry locations.
\[Straydogsﬁrearmstraining\](http://www.straydogsﬁrearmstraining.com/changes-to-current-ar-chcl-rules/)

\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/2010/title-5/subtitle-6/chapter-73/subchapter-3/5-73-306)

Democrat-Gazette\](https://www.arkansasonline.com/news/2016/feb/29/guns-allowed-at-some-polling-places-201-1/)

under Act 777 of 2023.
\[Uslawshield\](https://gunlaws.uslawshield.com/states/arkansas/) The
only protection is that under \*\*§ 5-73-306(18-19)\*\*, the person or
entity controlling a polling place facility may post written notice

\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/title-5/subtitle-6/chapter-73/subchapter-3/section-5-73-306/)
Whether ﬁrearms are permitted at any given polling place therefore

signage policy. \[Arkansas
Democrat-Gazette\](https://www.arkansasonline.com/news/2016/feb/29/guns-allowed-at-some-polling-places-201-1/)

\*\*Firearms preemption\*\* is among the strongest in the nation.

14-16-504(b)(1)\*\* (counties) prohibit local governments from enacting
"any ordinance or regulation pertaining to, or regulate in any other
manner, the ownership, transfer, transportation, carrying, or possession

\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/2014/title-14/subtitle-2/chapter-16/subchapter-5/section-14-16-504)
The only exception is regulating unsafe discharge. \[GIFFORDS\]
(https://giffords.org/lawcenter/state-laws/preemption-of-local-laws-in-arkansas/)
\*\*Act 161 of 2025\*\* further prohibited local emergency ﬁrearms
ordinances.
\[Justia\](https://law.justia.com/codes/arkansas/2014/title-14/subtitle-2/chapter-16/subchapter-5/section-14-16-504)
In \*Eureka Gun & Pawn, LLC v. City of Eureka Springs\* (Arkansas
Supreme Court, January 2026), the court reaﬃrmed the broad scope of this
preemption.

The "except as otherwise provided in state or federal law" clause in the
preemption statute is the only potential hook for a § 592 enforcement
ordinance, but it is untested and would face strong opposition.

\### CISA withdrawal impact assessment

All six states face increased risk from CISA's effective withdrawal.
Texas is best positioned with its statewide MS-ISAC membership and
robust state-funded infrastructure. New Mexico's dedicated OCS and
national lab relationships provide some resilience but the state's small
budget limits capacity. Oklahoma's Cyber Command and OK-ISAC provide
continuity but CISO instability is concerning. Kentucky's statutory
internet prohibition and paper ballot mandate reduce attack surface but
rural county resources remain thin. Utah's Cyber Center is new and still
maturing. Arkansas's cybersecurity apparatus was only formally
established in 2025 and is still building capacity precisely when
federal support has vanished.

\## IX. Strategic implications for the municipal ordinance campaign

\### Priority targeting: where the need is greatest and legal paths most
viable

\*\*The ﬁve states without meaningful polling-place ﬁrearms prohibitions
— Arkansas, Kentucky, Oklahoma, Texas, and Utah — represent the greatest
need for 18 U.S.C. § 592 enforcement ordinances, but they also present
the toughest legal barriers.\*\* The strategic challenge is identifying
jurisdictions where political will, legal creativity, and institutional
support can converge despite hostile state-level environments.

\*\*New Mexico\*\* is the natural starting point for demonstrating the
model. Both the SOS and AG are allies. Home rule cities like Albuquerque
and Santa Fe could adopt ordinances enforcing § 592 that focus on
prohibiting organized armed/paramilitary presence at polling places
rather than regulating individual ﬁrearms possession — threading the
needle of Article II, § 6 constitutional preemption. Success here
creates precedent and a template for other states.

\*\*Texas\*\* offers the most complex strategic landscape. The state
already prohibits ﬁrearms inside polling place buildings, but the
parking lot/outdoor gap is the critical vulnerability. Home rule cities
— Houston, San Antonio, Austin, Dallas, El Paso, Fort Worth — have the
political will and legal infrastructure to test a § 592 enforcement
ordinance, and the "Death Star" bill's constitutionality remains
unresolved before the Texas Supreme Court. The Texas Municipal League
has published resolution templates regarding ﬁrearms and polling places.
Any ordinance would face immediate AG challenge, but a
federal-law-enforcement framing is the strongest available theory.

\*\*Kentucky\*\* has home rule authority (KRS 82.082) but complete ﬁeld
preemption (KRS 65.870). The most viable near-term approach is
leveraging KRS 237.115(2)'s exception allowing government entities to
prohibit concealed carry in government-owned buildings (with no criminal
penalty). Louisville, Lexington, and other Democratic-leaning cities
could adopt this approach for government-building polling places while
pursuing state legislative change for broader coverage.

\*\*Oklahoma and Arkansas\*\* present near-insurmountable legal
obstacles for direct municipal ordinances. Oklahoma's punitive
preemption (21 O.S. § 1289.24) with monetary damages against
municipalities makes any direct ordinance ﬁscally dangerous. Arkansas's
deliberate 2015 removal of polling places from the prohibited-carry list
signals strong legislative intent. In both states, the most viable
short-term strategy is \*\*working with polling-place property
owners\*\* (churches, community centers) to voluntarily post no-ﬁrearms
signage under existing property-rights provisions, and pursuing voter
intimidation enforcement under existing criminal statutes.

\*\*Utah's\*\* extensive vote-by-mail system fundamentally changes the
calculus. Fewer physical polling locations means protection efforts can
be concentrated on Vote Centers and county clerk oﬃces. The Firearm
Preemption Enforcement Act's damages provision makes direct ordinances
risky, but Salt Lake City and other urban areas could explore
resolution-based approaches and coordination protocols with law
enforcement for the limited number of in-person locations.

\### The federal-law-enforcement argument: the best available legal
theory

Across all ﬁve hostile states, the strongest legal theory for municipal
ordinances is that they \*\*enforce existing federal law\*\* (18 U.S.C.
§ 592) rather than create new ﬁrearms regulations. Section 592
speciﬁcally addresses "troops or armed men" stationed at polling places
by persons in federal service — this targets organized armed presence,
not individual civilian carry. An ordinance carefully drafted to mirror
§ 592's scope — prohibiting organized armed groups from stationing
themselves at or near polling places — might survive preemption
challenges because it addresses conduct (organized intimidation) rather
than possession (individual ﬁrearms carry). This distinction has not
been tested in any of these states' courts, and it would face aggressive
challenge, but it represents the most legally defensible approach.

\### Coalition opportunities

The most promising coalition opportunities exist in \*\*Texas and New
Mexico\*\*, where large urban populations, existing election protection
organizations (ACLU aﬃliates, Brennan Center partners, League of Women
Voters chapters), and municipal league infrastructure can support
ordinance campaigns. The \*\*Texas Municipal League\*\* and \*\*New
Mexico Municipal League\*\* (arml.org for Arkansas, klc.org for
Kentucky, though these are less likely allies) are critical
institutional partners. The 866-OUR-VOTE hotline network and
state-speciﬁc election protection coalitions provide additional
capacity. In states with hostile AGs, partnership with local district
attorneys in supportive jurisdictions may provide alternative
prosecution pathways for voter intimidation.

\## Conclusion

This research reveals a stark asymmetry: the states where polling-place
ﬁrearms protections are weakest are precisely the states where municipal
authority to act is most constrained. The ﬁve states without adequate
polling-place ﬁrearms bans — Arkansas, Kentucky, Oklahoma, Texas, and
Utah — all enforce robust ﬁrearms preemption that makes direct municipal
ordinances legally perilous. The simultaneous collapse of federal
election security infrastructure (CISA defunding, EI-ISAC transition,
96% HAVA funding reduction) compounds the vulnerability by eliminating
the support systems that previously compensated for state-level gaps.

Three novel strategic insights emerge from this analysis. First, the
federal-law-enforcement framing — crafting ordinances that implement 18
U.S.C. § 592 against organized armed presence rather than regulate
individual ﬁrearms possession — represents an untested but legally
distinct theory that may survive preemption in some jurisdictions,
particularly Texas home-rule cities where the building-level prohibition
already demonstrates legislative acceptance of polling-place ﬁrearms
limits. Second, the property-owner signage approach in Arkansas (§
5-73-306(18-19)) and Oklahoma offers an immediate, no-ordinance pathway
that operates entirely within existing state law. Third, New Mexico's
2024 enactment of § 1-20-24 provides a live model of successful
state-level polling-place ﬁrearms legislation that can inform advocacy
in other states while its home-rule cities serve as the proving ground
for the § 592 municipal enforcement model. The 2026 election cycle
demands action across all these fronts simultaneously.
